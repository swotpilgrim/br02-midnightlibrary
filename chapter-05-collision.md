# Chapter 5: Collision

I arrive at the library at 12:30 AM, later than usual because Caroline took forever to fall asleep.

She'd been restless, tossing and turning, sighing dramatically every few minutes. I'd lain perfectly still in my bed, eyes closed, pretending to be asleep while my internal clock ticked away the minutes. Finally, around midnight, her breathing had evened out into the steady rhythm I'd been waiting for. I'd counted to two hundred to be absolutely sure before slipping out.

Now I'm climbing through the window with the practiced ease of someone who's done this a dozen times. The periodicals section is dark and familiar. I ease the window almost-closed behind me, careful to leave it exactly as I found it—slightly ajar, the faulty latch holding it in position.

I'm already thinking about my second-floor corner. The wingback chair. The view of the moonlit quad. Maybe I'll actually read tonight instead of just sitting and thinking. I brought *Wuthering Heights* from my room—one of the few books I own rather than borrowed from the library. I want to reread it without the pressure of finding symbols and themes for an essay.

I take three steps toward the stairs.

And freeze.

Footsteps.

Above me. Second floor. The distinctive creak of the old wooden floorboards that I've learned to recognize over my nights here.

My heart slams into my throat.

Security. It has to be security. They've finally noticed the window, or spotted me entering, or decided to do a sweep of the building. I'm caught. Everything I've been risking—my Honor Council position, my spotless record, my carefully maintained reputation—it's all about to crumble because I couldn't just deal with insomnia like a normal person.

The footsteps are getting closer. Moving toward the stairs.

I have maybe ten seconds before whoever it is comes down and sees me standing here like an idiot in the middle of the periodicals section.

Run back to the window. Escape before they see my face. That's the smart move.

But my legs won't cooperate. I'm frozen, paralyzed by panic and the sick certainty that I'm about to face consequences I should have anticipated but somehow convinced myself would never actually materialize.

The footsteps reach the top of the stairs.

I dive behind the nearest periodical shelf, pressing myself against the dusty magazines, barely breathing. My heart is pounding so loudly I'm certain whoever it is will hear it. I make myself as small as possible, wedged between the shelf and the wall, hidden in the shadow cast by the emergency lighting.

The footsteps descend. Steady. Unhurried. Not the heavy tread of security patrol but something lighter. Quieter.

A student.

Someone else is here. Someone else knows about the window, or found another way in, or has been using this space just like I have. The realization that I might not be caught is overwhelmed by the shock that my sanctuary isn't solely mine.

I peer around the edge of the shelf, just enough to see without being seen.

A figure descends the stairs, moving through the pools of emergency lighting. Jeans, not the khakis of Thornbridge's dress code. A dark hoodie. Carrying a book in one hand. Tall—taller than me, definitely male. Moving with the kind of easy confidence that comes from familiarity with the space.

He reaches the bottom of the stairs and passes through a patch of brighter lighting near the circulation desk.

Evan Cross.

My brain stutters trying to process this. Evan Cross, the isolated transfer student who sits alone and never talks to anyone, is in the library at 12:30 in the morning, looking completely at ease in a space he shouldn't be able to access.

He knows about the window.

He's been coming here.

How long? How many times have we been here at the same time without knowing it? Has he seen me? Does he know I've been using his space, or does he think it's his alone?

Evan walks toward the window—my window, except apparently it's his window too—with the kind of purposeful movement that speaks of routine. This isn't his first time. He's done this before. Multiple times. Maybe since the beginning of the semester, just like me.

He's going to pass within five feet of where I'm hiding.

I press myself flatter against the wall, making myself invisible through sheer force of will. I'm barely breathing. My heart is hammering. If he looks in my direction, if he hears even the slightest sound, this whole thing falls apart.

Evan walks past my hiding spot.

For one suspended moment, I see him clearly. The book in his hand is worn paperback fiction—I can't read the title from this angle but it's definitely not a textbook. His profile is illuminated by the ambient light from the windows. And his expression—

It stops my breath.

He looks peaceful. Open. Completely different from the closed-off, isolated figure I observed during lunch and in the hallways and in calculus class. During the day, Evan Cross wears his isolation like armor, shoulders curved inward, eyes forward, radiating "don't approach me" from every angle.

But here, at night, in this supposedly-empty library, his armor is gone.

He looks like someone who can finally breathe.

The recognition hits me like a physical blow. Because I know that feeling. It's exactly what I experience when I climb through that window and settle into my corner. That sense of being able to shed the performance and just exist.

Evan has been looking for the same thing I have. The same escape. The same sanctuary.

He reaches the window and pauses, glancing back at the library interior. I freeze, certain he's somehow sensed my presence. But his gaze sweeps past my hiding place without stopping. He's just taking one last look at the space before he leaves.

Then he's climbing through the window with practiced efficiency. One leg out, then the other, dropping to the ground outside. I hear the soft thud of his sneakers hitting the ground. The window eases almost-closed, held by the broken latch.

And he's gone.

I stay pressed against the periodical shelf for a full five minutes, maybe longer. My heart gradually slows. My breathing returns to normal. The library settles back into absolute silence.

But everything has changed.

My sanctuary isn't mine. It's ours. Evan Cross has been coming here too—maybe longer than I have, maybe on different nights, maybe at different hours. We've been orbiting each other in this space, two people seeking the same refuge for probably the same reasons, completely unaware of each other's existence.

Until now.

I unstick myself from my hiding place and move on shaky legs to the window. Outside, the quad is empty. Evan is already gone, disappeared back to whichever dorm he came from. I stare out at the dark campus, trying to process what just happened.

Part of me wants to be angry. This was my space. My secret. My solution to the problem of not being able to sleep, not being able to think clearly, not being able to exist without the constant weight of performance and expectation.

But I can't work up any real anger. Because Evan's expression—that moment of peace I witnessed—tells me he needs this just as much as I do. Maybe more.

I think about him sitting alone at lunch. Walking through hallways like a ghost. Never raising his hand even when he knows the answer. Choosing isolation or having it chosen for him—I still don't know which.

And at night, he comes here. To this library. Through this window with the broken latch. Seeking the same sanctuary I've claimed.

We're not so different, Evan Cross and me.

I climb the stairs to the second floor slowly, my mind spinning. Should I leave? Go back to my room and avoid potential discovery? What if he comes back? What if this wasn't his only visit tonight?

But I'm already here. And I need this space tonight—need it more than usual after lying awake for an extra half hour waiting for Caroline to sleep, after a long day of performing perfect Harper Mills for teachers and friends and Honor Council meetings.

I settle into my wingback chair, but I can't relax the way I normally do. Every sound makes me tense, wondering if Evan is coming back. Every creak of the building settling sounds like footsteps. The silence that usually brings peace now feels charged with potential discovery.

After an hour, I give up. I'm not going to find peace tonight. Too much has changed.

I make my way back down to the periodicals section and climb through the window at 2 AM, earlier than usual. The walk back to Hargrove Hall feels different. I keep thinking I see someone ahead of me in the shadows, but it's just my imagination, my brain still spinning from the shock of discovery.

Back in my room, Caroline is still sleeping peacefully, oblivious to everything. I slip into bed and stare at the familiar ceiling stains.

Forty-three of them. I've counted them so many times.

But tonight I'm not counting stains. I'm thinking about Evan Cross and the way he looked when he thought no one was watching. The way he moved through that library like he belonged there. The peace on his face that I've never seen during daylight hours.

I'm thinking about the fact that I'm not the only one breaking rules to find a space where I can breathe.

And I'm wondering what happens next. Do we keep pretending the other doesn't exist? Do we coordinate schedules to avoid collision? Do we acknowledge it somehow?

I have no answers. Just the strange, unsettling knowledge that my secret sanctuary has been shared all along, and I never knew it.

The person I was watching with curiosity during lunch and calculus class, the outsider everyone dismisses and ignores, has been inhabiting my refuge. Has been seeking the same escape I've been desperate enough to risk everything for.

Evan Cross isn't just the weird transfer kid anymore.

He's someone like me. Someone struggling. Someone who needs the library at midnight because the daytime world is too loud, too demanding, too full of expectations he can't or won't meet.

I close my eyes, but I don't sleep for a long time. I'm too busy thinking about what it means that I'm not alone in my midnight rebellion.

And wondering if that makes it better or worse.
