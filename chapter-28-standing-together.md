# Chapter 28: Standing Together

After Dr. Whitmore's closing remarks about tradition and progress, the celebration shifts into a more casual reception. People drift toward the refreshments, cluster in conversation groups, examine the renovated spaces more closely.

Evan and I stay together. His hand is still in mine—a statement, a promise, a choice I'm making visible to everyone.

And people are noticing.

Some with curiosity. Some with judgment. Some with the kind of speculative whispers that make my skin prickle with awareness. But I don't let go.

"You're sure about this?" Evan asks quietly as we navigate through the crowd. "It's not too late to—"

"I'm not backing down," I say firmly. "This is what I want. What I need."

A few students we pass do double-takes. A junior I recognize from Honor Council gives me a confused look. Mr. Harrison, my debate coach, stops mid-conversation to stare.

Let them stare.

Then I notice something unexpected: Thomas Brennan, the senior library assistant, heading directly toward us with a genuine smile.

"Harper, Evan." He nods to each of us. "Finally seeing you two together in daylight. About time."

Evan looks surprised. "You knew?"

"Found you in here a few times during my late-night security volunteer shifts," Thomas admits. "Figured you had your reasons for keeping it quiet. But I'm glad you're being open about it now." He turns to Evan specifically. "You know, we could use someone on the library committee who actually appreciates this space. Interested?"

It's such a casually friendly overture—the first time I've seen anyone at Thornbridge approach Evan like he's just a normal student instead of a curiosity to avoid.

Evan hesitates, clearly uncertain how to respond. I squeeze his hand encouragingly.

"Maybe," Evan says carefully. "What does the committee do?"

"Book acquisitions, event planning, preserving the library's character while modernizing its function." Thomas grins. "Basically we get to spend time with books and pretend it's extracurricular leadership."

"That actually sounds interesting," Evan admits.

"Great. We meet Tuesdays at four. You should come." Thomas glances at me. "You too, Harper, if you're interested. Though I know Honor Council keeps you pretty busy."

He walks away before I can respond, leaving Evan looking slightly shell-shocked.

"Did that just happen?" Evan asks.

"Someone invited you to join something," I say, smiling. "Yes, that just happened."

"People don't usually..." He trails off, but I understand what he's not saying. People don't usually approach him. Don't usually treat him like someone they'd want in their group. Don't usually see past his isolation to consider he might have something to offer.

"Maybe they will now," I say. "You're not invisible anymore."

"I'm not sure that's better."

"It's real. Real is better than safe."

We're interrupted by another unexpected approach—this time from a pair of sophomore girls I recognize from the school newspaper.

"Excuse me," one of them says, looking nervous but determined. "Are you Evan Cross? The transfer student from New York?"

Evan tenses. "Yes."

"We're working on a profile series about students who've joined Thornbridge this year. Would you be interested in being interviewed? We'd love to hear about your perspective on the school, what made you transfer, that kind of thing."

I can see Evan's immediate instinct is to refuse. To protect himself from exposure, from people digging into his past, from the possibility of judgment.

But then he glances at me, and I give him a small nod. *Your choice. But I'll support whatever you decide.*

"Can I think about it?" Evan asks.

"Of course! Here's my email." The girl hands him a small card. "Just let us know by next week if you're interested."

They walk away, and Evan stares at the card like it might explode.

"You don't have to do it," I tell him.

"I know. But maybe..." He pockets the card. "Maybe it's time to stop hiding what happened at my old school. Maybe if I tell the truth first, on my own terms, it won't have power over me anymore."

"That's brave."

"Or stupid." He echoes my words from earlier, and we both smile.

We continue through the celebration, and I start noticing a pattern. More people are approaching Evan. Not many—he's still the isolated transfer student in most people's minds. But a few.

A guy from his calculus class asks about a homework problem.

A girl who's apparently in his English section comments on his essay from last week.

An underclassman who saw us together asks if we're actually dating, then cheerfully says her older sister dated someone "unexpected" and they've been together for three years.

Each interaction is small. Brief. Nothing dramatic. But together they add up to something significant: Evan is becoming visible. Not as the weird loner to avoid, but as an actual person people might get to know.

And he's handling it. Awkwardly at first—he's not used to friendly approaches, not practiced at small talk with people who might become friends. But he's trying. Responding. Opening up incrementally.

I watch this unfold with something that feels like pride mixed with hope.

Meanwhile, I'm navigating my own shifting landscape.

Word has spread quickly about Evan and me. I can feel the whispers following us, see the speculative looks, sense the judgment from some corners. But it's not universally negative.

A few people seem genuinely curious rather than condemning. One of my classmates from history approaches to say she thinks it's "cool that you're being real instead of just maintaining your perfect image." A junior I barely know gives me a thumbs up.

But there are also the harder moments.

I overhear someone say, "I thought Harper Mills had better judgment than that."

I see a cluster of pre-law students whispering while looking in my direction.

I catch Madison watching me from across the room with an expression I can't quite read—not quite disapproving, but not accepting either. Confused, maybe. Or reassessing who she thought I was.

Each moment of judgment stings. But not as much as I feared. Because I'm standing next to Evan, and our joined hands feel more real than anyone's approval ever did.

Caroline approaches us again, looking thoughtful. "Can I ask you something? Without you getting defensive?"

"Sure," I say, bracing myself.

"Are you happy?" Caroline asks simply. "Like, actually happy, not just performing happiness?"

The question catches me off guard. It's not what I expected.

"Yes," I say, and realize it's true. "I'm terrified and uncertain and probably making mistakes. But yeah. I'm happier than I've been in years."

Caroline nods slowly. "Then I guess that's what matters. Even if I don't totally get it yet."

It's not a ringing endorsement. But it's acceptance. Or at least the beginning of it.

"Thank you," I tell her.

"Don't thank me yet. You're still going to have to deal with your mother finding out you're dating the mysterious transfer student and questioning your pre-law path." Caroline's expression is wry. "That conversation is going to be brutal."

"I know. But I'll handle it."

"We'll handle it," Evan corrects quietly. "You're not alone in this anymore."

The simple statement makes my throat tight. He's right. For the first time in my carefully controlled life, I'm not navigating everything alone. I have someone standing with me, willing to face the consequences alongside me.

The celebration begins to wind down. People drift toward the exits, heading to dinner or back to their dorms to pack for winter break. The library empties gradually, returning to its usual quiet.

Evan and I linger near the periodicals section. Our section, though it looks nothing like it did at midnight.

"This place is so different now," I say, looking around at the bright lighting, the new security panels, the gleaming renovations.

"But the memories are the same," Evan says. "This is still where I first really talked to you. Where we figured out we understood each other. Where we fell for each other, even if we were too scared to admit it for a while."

"Fell for each other," I repeat, testing the words. "Is that what happened?"

"Isn't it?" Evan turns to face me fully. "I fell for you, Harper. Somewhere between the first night you climbed through that window and the last night we left through the front door. I fell completely."

My heart does something complicated in my chest. "I fell for you too. I just didn't know how to admit it while I was still pretending to be someone else."

"And now?"

"Now I'm done pretending." I step closer to him. "Now I'm choosing to be real, even when it's scary. Especially when it's scary."

Evan cups my face gently, and we're standing in the exact spot where he almost kissed me weeks ago. But this time there's no fear of interruption, no need to hide, no pretending this is anything other than what it is: two people who found each other in the dark and are choosing to stay together in the light.

"Thank you," Evan says softly.

"For what?"

"For being brave enough to choose this. To choose us. To risk your perfect life for something real."

"Thank you for seeing me," I reply. "For looking past the performance to the actual person underneath. For making me brave enough to stop hiding."

We kiss in the bright, renovated library while the last few people leave the celebration. It's soft and sweet and witnessed by at least three people based on the giggles I hear from near the entrance.

But I don't care.

This is my choice. My truth. My life.

And for the first time in years, it actually feels like mine.

When we finally leave the library, walking out hand-in-hand through the main entrance, the sun is setting. The campus is bathed in golden winter light, and students are heading to the dining hall for dinner.

"Want to eat together?" I ask. "At a table. Where everyone can see."

"In the dining hall," Evan confirms. "Our first official public meal."

"Terrifying?"

"Absolutely." He squeezes my hand. "Let's do it."

We walk toward the dining hall together, and I can see people noticing us. Some with curiosity, some with judgment, some with the kind of interest that comes from witnessing something unexpected.

But we're together. We're visible. We're choosing authenticity over approval.

And that makes all the difference.
