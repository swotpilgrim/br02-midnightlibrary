# Chapter 10: Secrets Shared

The first snow falls on a Thursday night in late November.

I arrive at the library to find the quad transformed—white and pristine, untouched by footprints yet. The snow is still falling, fat flakes drifting down through the lamplight. It's beautiful in a way that makes campus feel even more removed from the real world.

When I climb through the window, I notice Evan is already here.

That's unusual. I almost always arrive first. But when I reach the second floor, I find him standing at one of the tall windows, staring out at the snow-covered quad.

He doesn't turn when I approach, even though he must have heard my footsteps on the stairs.

"Hey," I say quietly.

"Hey." His voice is flat.

I set my bag down on my usual chair but don't sit. Something about Evan's posture—shoulders tense, hands shoved in his pockets—tells me this isn't a normal night.

"Are you okay?" I ask.

"Yeah. Fine."

He's lying. I've gotten good at reading Evan over the past few weeks. This is not fine.

I cross to the window and stand next to him, looking out at the falling snow. The quad looks like a photograph, perfect and still. Inside, the silence stretches.

"You don't have to tell me," I say eventually. "But if you want to talk about whatever's bothering you, I'm here."

Evan is quiet for so long I think he's not going to respond. Then:

"Do you know why I transferred to Thornbridge?"

His voice is careful. Controlled. Like he's working very hard to keep it level.

"I heard rumors," I admit. "But I don't know the actual story."

"Right. The rumors." Evan lets out a bitter laugh. "That's kind of the problem. The rumors are always louder than the truth."

I wait. Don't push. Just stand next to him and let him decide whether to continue.

After a long moment, he does.

"There was a cheating scandal at my old school. Dalton, in New York. Last spring. It was massive—like, the biggest academic integrity violation the school had ever seen. A dozen students were involved. They'd been stealing tests and answer keys for two semesters, sharing them through a private group chat. Some of the students caught were really high-profile. Legacy admissions, kids whose parents donated buildings, that kind of thing."

Evan pauses, still looking out the window.

"I wasn't part of it," he continues. "I wasn't in that group chat. I never saw a stolen test. I didn't cheat. But I was friends with some of the people who did. We studied together sometimes. I'd loaned my notes to a few of them. I was... peripherally connected."

"That doesn't mean you were involved," I say.

"I know. And that's what the investigation concluded eventually. But for a while—for weeks while they were interviewing everyone and pulling records—my name kept coming up. People said I'd been in study groups with the cheaters. I'd been seen with them. My notes had been used. None of it was illegal or even suspicious in isolation, but when you put it all together, it looked really bad."

I can hear the pain in his voice, carefully suppressed but still there.

"Even after the actual cheaters confessed and the investigation cleared me, the damage was done. Teachers treated me differently. They'd watch me during tests. Question my work. Ask if I was sure I'd done it myself. My classmates—people I thought were friends—started avoiding me. Not everyone, but enough that it was obvious. The whole school knew my name had been connected to the scandal, and clearing my name didn't erase that connection."

"That's horrible," I say quietly.

Evan finally turns to look at me. His expression is raw in a way I've never seen before.

"The worst part was my girlfriend. Melissa. She was actually one of the cheaters—though I didn't know that until everything came out. She'd used my notes without telling me, then when she got caught, she told the investigators that I'd known what she was doing. That I'd helped her, given her access to my work specifically so she could use it for cheating. It was a lie to deflect blame from herself, and she eventually recanted when they pressed her on it. But for a few days, everyone thought I was not just peripherally involved but actively helping."

My chest tightens with anger on his behalf. "She threw you under the bus to save herself."

"Yeah. And when the truth came out and she admitted she'd lied, she tried to apologize. Said she'd panicked. That she hoped we could work through it. But I was done. I couldn't—" His voice cracks slightly. "I couldn't trust her after that. Couldn't trust anyone, really. Because if the person you're dating will betray you to save their own skin, who can you trust?"

"So you transferred," I say.

"My parents decided I needed a fresh start. They thought getting away from Dalton and all the associations would help. New school, new people, new reputation. Except I brought it all with me—the fear, the mistrust, the certainty that if I let anyone get close, they'd eventually hurt me."

"Is that why you sit alone? Why you don't talk to anyone?"

"At first it was self-protection. I figured if I didn't make friends, I couldn't be betrayed. If I didn't try to fit in, I couldn't be disappointed when people decided I wasn't worth their time. It was easier to choose isolation than to risk having it chosen for me."

The vulnerability in his admission makes my heart ache.

"But it's lonely," I say softly.

"Yeah," Evan agrees. "It's really fucking lonely."

We stand there in silence, watching the snow fall. I'm processing everything he just shared—the cheating scandal, the false accusations, the girlfriend who betrayed him, the friends who abandoned him. The way he came to Thornbridge already wounded and decided that staying wounded was safer than trying to heal.

"I'm sorry that happened to you," I say. "And I'm sorry people believed rumors over truth. That's not fair."

"Life's not fair." Evan's voice is tired. "I learned that pretty definitively."

"Maybe not. But you shouldn't have had to learn it that way."

He looks at me, and something in his expression shifts. "Do you know what the worst part is? I still don't know if I did something wrong. Not cheating—I know I didn't cheat. But should I have known what Melissa was doing? Should I have questioned why she wanted my notes so often? Should I have noticed that some of my study group friends were doing better than their understanding of the material suggested? Was I willfully blind because it was easier than confronting uncomfortable truths?"

"You can't blame yourself for not being suspicious of people you trusted," I say firmly. "That's not naivety. That's normal. You're supposed to be able to trust your girlfriend and your friends. If they violated that trust, that's on them, not you."

"Maybe." He doesn't sound convinced.

"Definitely," I insist. "Evan, you were cleared by the investigation. You didn't do anything wrong. The people who hurt you are the ones who cheated and lied and threw you under the bus. Not you for being trusting."

"Then why does it still feel like my fault?"

"Because betrayal rewrites history," I say quietly. "It makes you question everything you thought you knew. But that doesn't mean you actually did anything wrong. It just means someone you trusted proved they weren't trustworthy."

Evan studies me for a long moment. "How do you know that?"

"I don't, really. But I'm trying to believe it. For both of us."

His almost-smile appears, sad but genuine. "Both of us?"

"I spend every night breaking rules because I can't cope with the pressure of being perfect. I lie to everyone in my life about where I am and what I'm doing. I sit on Honor Council and vote on discipline cases while committing violations that could get me suspended. If that's not proof that none of us are actually who we present ourselves to be, I don't know what is."

"That's different," Evan says. "You're not hurting anyone."

"I'm being a hypocrite."

"You're surviving. There's a difference."

I realize he's turning my own argument back on me. Defending me the way I just defended him.

"So we're both just... surviving," I say.

"In our own ways, yeah."

"By breaking rules and hiding in libraries at midnight."

"By finding the only space where we can be honest," Evan corrects.

The chapel clock chimes midnight. We've been standing at this window for over an hour, and I didn't even notice the time passing.

"Thank you," Evan says quietly.

"For what?"

"For listening. For not judging. For not immediately assuming the rumors were true, or that I must have done something to deserve what happened."

"I would never think that."

"Most people did."

"I'm not most people," I say.

"No," Evan agrees, his eyes holding mine. "You're really not."

Something passes between us in that moment. An understanding that goes deeper than shared rule-breaking or mutual insomnia. We've both been hurt by people we trusted. We're both hiding parts of ourselves from the daytime world. We're both searching for somewhere we can be real.

And somehow, improbably, we've found that in each other.

"I'm glad you're here," I say. "I mean, I'm glad I found the window when I did. I'm glad we both... found this."

"Me too," Evan says softly.

We turn back to the window together, watching the snow transform campus into something beautiful and unfamiliar. The silence between us is comfortable now, the heavy emotions of confession giving way to quiet companionship.

After a while, we settle into our usual chairs. But tonight feels different. Deeper. Like we've crossed some invisible threshold from allies to something more.

Friends, maybe. Or something that doesn't have a name yet but feels important enough to protect.

I don't know what happens next. Don't know how we navigate the growing connection between us while still maintaining our separate daytime lives. Don't know if this fragile trust can survive outside these midnight hours.

But for now, in this moment, with snow falling outside and Evan reading quietly six feet away, I'm not worried about any of that.

For now, this is enough.
