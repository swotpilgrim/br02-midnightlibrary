# Chapter 9: Midnight Becomes Routine

I start watching the clock.

It's Tuesday evening study hall, and I'm supposed to be outlining my history paper on the Progressive Era. Instead, I'm staring at the clock on the wall:  8:47 PM. Three hours and thirteen minutes until midnight.

Madison notices. "Are you okay? You keep spacing out."

"Just tired," I lie. "Long day."

But I'm not tired. I'm anticipating. Counting down to the moment when I can slip out of Hargrove Hall and cross the quad to the library where Evan will arrive around 1 AM and we'll talk about things that actually matter instead of college rankings and LSAT prep.

When did this happen? When did midnight become the part of my day I look forward to most?

The routine has solidified over the past two weeks. I arrive around midnight. Evan arrives between 1 and 1:15 AM. He comes straight to the second floor reading room and takes the leather chair about six feet from mine. We talk for an hour, sometimes two. About everything and nothing.

Last night we debated whether *The Great Gatsby* is actually about the American Dream. Evan argued it's about obsession and the impossibility of recapturing the past. I countered that those themes serve the larger critique of American materialism. We went back and forth for forty-five minutes.

Sunday night we talked about music. Evan likes indie rock bands I've never heard of. He wrote down recommendations in my notebook.

Friday we talked about our families. Evan's parents are both in finance with high expectations and very specific ideas about his future, none of which involve his actual interests. My mother is a corporate lawyer who decided when I was twelve that I'd follow in her footsteps. My father is a doctor who works constantly and mostly agrees with whatever my mother says.

"Do you actually want to be a lawyer?" Evan asked.

"I don't know," I admitted. "I've been on this track for so long I've never really questioned whether it's what I want or just what I'm supposed to want."

"What would you do if you could do anything?"

The question stopped me. Because I genuinely don't know. I've spent so long focusing on achievement and admission and the next step in a pre-planned path that I've never considered alternatives.

"I don't know," I said again. "Maybe something with writing? Or teaching? I like literature. I like thinking about why stories matter and how they shape the way we see the world."

"So study literature," Evan said, like it was simple.

"My mother would kill me. 'What are you going to do with an English degree, Harper? Teach high school? You're capable of more than that.'"

"More by whose definition?"

I didn't have an answer.

Tonight is Wednesday. I finish my history outline at 9:30 and pack up early. Madison gives me a strange look as I leave the library—the daytime library, crowded with students and bright fluorescent lighting and all the social dynamics that make it feel completely different from the midnight version.

Back in my room, Caroline is at her desk doing calculus homework with her field hockey stick propped against the wall like she's prepared to defend herself from derivatives.

"You're back early," she observes.

"Finished what I needed to do."

She accepts this and returns to her problem set. I pretend to read in bed, watching the clock. 10:00. 10:30. 11:00. Caroline starts getting ready for bed, brushing her teeth, changing into pajamas, setting her alarm for 6:30 AM.

By 11:45 she's in bed, lights out. I lie still in the darkness, counting her breaths until they even into sleep rhythm.

At 12:10 AM I slip out.

The route to the library is automatic now. My feet know which floorboards to avoid, which doors to use, where the shadows are deepest. The November night is cold enough that I can see my breath, but I don't mind. In less than an hour, I'll be sitting in the reading room with Evan, talking about something real.

I climb through the window at 12:18 AM and settle into my chair. Tonight I've brought *Wuthering Heights*, which I haven't read since freshman year. I want to reread it without the pressure of finding symbols and writing essays. Just experience it as a story.

Evan arrives at 1:06 AM. I've been tracking the minutes unconsciously, aware of every moment between now and when he'll appear.

He comes straight to the second floor and drops into his chair with the exhausted satisfaction of someone who's been waiting for this too.

"Long day?" I ask.

"Endless." He pulls out a worn notebook and a book I don't recognize. "You?"

"Madison tried to recruit me for an LSAT study group. For a test we won't take for two years."

Evan almost-smiles. "Did you join?"

"I said yes because saying no would require explaining why I'm not as focused on law school as I'm supposed to be."

"So you're trapped in a study group for a test you don't want to take for a career you're not sure you want."

"When you put it that way, it sounds pathetic."

"It sounds like surviving an impossible situation," Evan counters. "Which is different."

He has a way of reframing my failures as adaptations. It should feel like empty reassurance, but it doesn't. Because Evan understands what it's like to perform a version of yourself that everyone else expects while the real you suffocates underneath.

"What did you do today?" I ask.

"Avoided people, mostly. Sat through classes. Read during lunch so no one would try to talk to me."

"Does anyone try?"

"Not anymore." He says it matter-of-factly, without self-pity. "I've established myself as the weird loner who transferred for mysterious reasons. People have stopped being curious."

"Do you want them to be curious?"

Evan considers this. "I don't know. Part of me wants to be left alone because it's safer than letting people close enough to hurt me. Part of me knows that isolation is just... lonely."

The honesty in his voice catches at something in my chest.

"You're not alone right now," I point out quietly.

"No," Evan agrees, meeting my eyes. "I'm not."

The moment stretches between us, charged with something I can't quite name. Then Evan breaks eye contact and opens his notebook.

"I want to show you something," he says.

He passes me the notebook, open to a page filled with handwriting. Not notes—prose. A story, I realize as I start reading.

It's about a girl who lives in a house where every room shows a different version of her life. In one room, she's a doctor like her father wanted. In another, she's a musician. In a third, she's traveling the world. But she can't figure out which room is real, or if any of them are. So she spends all her time walking between rooms, trying to decide who she actually is.

"It's not finished," Evan says. "I don't know how it ends yet."

"This is really good," I say, and I mean it. The prose is clean and evocative. The metaphor is obvious but effective. "Is this what you want to do? Write?"

"Maybe. If I'm allowed to want things that aren't 'practical.'"

I hand back the notebook. "You should want things. Even impractical ones."

"So should you."

We talk for another hour about writing and stories and whether it's possible to major in something your parents don't approve of without destroying your relationship with them. We talk about Thornbridge and how strange it is that three hundred students can live in such close quarters and still feel so isolated from each other. We talk about whether anyone is actually happy here or if everyone's just performing happiness as one more achievement to maintain.

Around 2:30 AM, we fall into comfortable silence. Evan reads his philosophy book—tonight it's Sartre—while I work through *Wuthering Heights*. The chapel clock chimes three. Neither of us moves to leave.

I glance over at Evan and find him looking at me.

"What?" I ask.

"Nothing. Just..." He pauses. "This is the best part of my day. Every day. I look forward to midnight more than anything else."

My heart does something complicated in my chest. "Me too."

"It's strange, right? That the thing I look forward to most is sitting in an empty library with someone I'm not supposed to know, talking about things I can't say to anyone else."

"It's not strange," I say. "It's just... real."

"Yeah," Evan says softly. "Real."

We hold each other's gaze for a moment longer than necessary. Then I force myself to look back at my book even though I'm not reading anymore.

Because something is happening here. Something beyond finding sanctuary or establishing a truce. I'm starting to care about Evan Cross in ways that go beyond shared rule-breaking and midnight conversations.

I want to know what he's thinking. I want to make him do that almost-smile thing where his guard drops for half a second. I want to hear about his day and tell him about mine. I want this—these hours between midnight and three AM—to keep happening indefinitely.

And that's dangerous. Because eventually we'll graduate. Eventually this will end. Eventually we'll have to go back to being strangers who pass each other in hallways and pretend we never shared anything.

Unless we don't. Unless we figure out how to bring this into daylight.

But I'm not brave enough for that yet. During the day, I still sit with Madison and the pre-law group while Evan sits alone. I still walk past him in the hallway without acknowledgment. I still perform my role as successful Harper Mills while he performs his role as isolated outsider.

At night, we're different people. Real people.

I just don't know how to reconcile the two versions.

At 3:20 AM, we pack up without needing to discuss it. We've developed this unspoken synchronization—knowing when it's time to leave, when we've stayed as long as we can risk.

We slip through the window and walk across the quad together until our paths diverge.

"See you tomorrow," Evan says.

"See you tomorrow," I echo.

Back in my room, I climb into bed and stare at the ceiling. Forty-three water stains. I still count them sometimes, but not tonight.

Tonight I'm thinking about Evan's story about the girl with all the different rooms. Wondering which room is mine. Wondering if maybe the real me only exists in that library at midnight, and everything else is just performance.

Or maybe I'm starting to believe that real can exist in more than one place.

If I'm brave enough to let it.
