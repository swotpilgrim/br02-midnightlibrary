# Chapter 3: Sanctuary

I go back the next night. And the night after that. And the night after that.

It becomes a ritual, careful and precise. I wait until Caroline's breathing deepens into the steady rhythm of sleep. I count to one hundred to be sure. Then I slip out of bed, pull on my hoodie and sneakers, and ease out of the room with the practiced stealth of someone who's done this enough times to know which floorboards creak and how to turn the doorknob without making the mechanism click.

The route to the library becomes familiar. Past the bathroom, down the stairs, avoiding the squeaky third step. Out the side door of Hargrove Hall. Across the quad, keeping to the shadows. Around to the west side of the library where the boxwood hedge hides the window with the faulty latch.

By the fourth night, I can do it in eight minutes.

The library at night becomes mine in a way nothing else at Thornbridge has ever been. During the day, every space is shared, observed, evaluated. The dining hall with its assigned tables and social hierarchies. The classrooms where I sit in the front row and raise my hand at exactly the right frequency—enough to show engagement, not so much that I seem desperate. The Honor Council meeting room where I weigh in on discipline cases with careful consideration of fairness and precedent. Even my dorm room belongs partially to Caroline, whose field hockey equipment and Boston old-money confidence fill half the space.

But at midnight, the library is entirely mine.

I claim a corner on the second floor, tucked between the literature section and the tall windows that overlook the quad. There's a leather wingback chair there that's been broken in by generations of students—the leather cracked in places, the seat cushion molded to the shape of whoever sat there last. It faces the windows, giving me a view of the moonlit campus and the dark silhouettes of buildings against the October sky.

The bookshelves surrounding my corner hold the classics: Austen and the Brontës on one side, Dickens and Hardy on the other, American literature—Hawthorne, Melville, Twain—on the shelf behind me. During the day, these books are props for assigned essays and class discussions. At night, they're different. Companions, almost. The spines with their faded gold lettering catch what little light filters through the windows. I pull them out sometimes, read a few pages of *Pride and Prejudice* or *Wuthering Heights*, remembering when I used to read for pleasure instead of for grades.

I don't always read, though. Sometimes I just sit in the wingback chair and exist. I watch the shadows move across the quad as clouds pass in front of the moon. I listen to the absolute silence—no voices, no footsteps, no radiator clanking or Caroline's breathing or the constant ambient noise of three hundred students living in close quarters. Just the occasional creak of the old building settling and the distant chime of the chapel clock marking the hours.

For the first time since arriving at Thornbridge, I feel like I can breathe.

The library is different at night. During the day, it's functional and busy. Students crowd around the study tables, their textbooks spread out, highlighting and taking notes. The computers are always occupied. The circulation desk has a line during peak hours. People whisper to each other—it's technically a quiet space, but there's always the hum of suppressed conversation, the rustle of papers, the tap of keyboards, the occasional buzz of a phone someone forgot to silence.

Ms. Chen, the head librarian, patrols with a sharp eye for rule violations. No food or drink except water. No feet on the furniture. No sleeping in the reading chairs—this is a place for academic work, not napping.

At night, there's none of that. Just emergency lighting that creates pools of greenish illumination and vast stretches of shadow. The study tables sit empty. The computers sleep in darkness. The circulation desk is abandoned, its organized chaos of returned books and hold requests frozen until morning.

The library becomes what it must have been in 1889 when it was first built—a place of quiet contemplation, of mahogany and leather and the particular smell of old books. A sanctuary.

I bring my schoolwork sometimes. The calculus problem sets that twist my brain into knots during the day somehow make more sense at midnight. I spread them out on a small table near my chair and work through the equations in the pool of light from my phone. Without the pressure of morning deadlines and the anxiety about maintaining my perfect GPA, the math becomes almost meditative. Numbers and formulas instead of constant self-evaluation.

But mostly, I don't work. I sit in my chair and let my mind slow down.

I think about things I don't have time to consider during the day. Like whether I actually want to be a lawyer, or if that's just my mother's unrealized dream that I've absorbed as my own. Like what it means that I'm happier alone in an empty library at two AM than I am surrounded by people who are supposedly my friends. Like whether the version of Harper Mills that everyone sees during the day is actually me, or just a very convincing performance.

The thoughts aren't always comfortable. But at least they're honest.

By the end of the first week, I notice a change.

I'm sleeping better. Not perfectly—I still lie awake sometimes after returning from the library, my mind processing the quiet hours I've just spent. But it's different. Instead of anxious spiraling about everything I need to do and every way I might fail, my thoughts are calmer. More centered. The desperate, clawing panic that used to keep me staring at the ceiling has subsided.

During the day, I function better. I'm not running on fumes anymore. In calculus, I actually follow the lecture instead of just copying notes to review later when my exhausted brain might cooperate. In English, my contributions to the Gatsby discussion are sharp enough that Ms. Patterson gives me an approving nod. At Honor Council, I don't have to fight to stay focused through the entire meeting.

"You seem different lately," Madison says one afternoon as we're walking from the dining hall to the library for afternoon study period.

My heart stutters. "Different how?"

Madison shrugs, adjusting her bag on her shoulder. She's dressed in the unofficial pre-law uniform: pressed khakis, a crisp button-down, loafers that cost more than my entire wardrobe. "I don't know. More relaxed, maybe? You were pretty stressed the first few weeks of semester. I was worried you might be burning out."

"Oh." I try to sound casual. "I've just been managing my time better. Getting more sleep."

It's not technically a lie. I am getting more sleep. I'm just leaving out the part about breaking into the library every night to achieve it.

Madison accepts this with a nod. "Good. You had me worried. Junior year is brutal enough without falling apart." She launches into a discussion of her own schedule—Mock Trial practice, SAT prep, the community service project she's leading that will look good on college applications—and I make appropriate responses while my mind is elsewhere.

I'm lying to her. Not directly, but by omission. Madison is supposed to be my closest friend here, and I can't tell her about the one thing that's actually keeping me sane.

Because if I told her, she'd probably report me. Or at least tell me I was being reckless and stupid, risking everything I've worked for. Madison understands achievement and ambition. She wouldn't understand desperate need for a space where I can stop performing and just exist.

That afternoon, I sit in the library during official hours and it feels completely different from my midnight visits. The space is crowded and purposeful. Every chair is occupied. Students hunch over textbooks, type furiously on laptops, whisper about problem sets and essay deadlines. Ms. Chen moves through the stacks, reshelving books with practiced efficiency.

I find a seat at one of the long tables—not my leather wingback chair, which is currently occupied by a senior I vaguely recognize from debate team. The table is crowded with other pre-law track students, including Madison. We spread out our work in the shared space, our elbows occasionally bumping, our papers mixing together.

It's fine. Functional. This is what the library is for—productive academic work in a shared community space.

But it's not the same.

At midnight, the library is mine. A secret refuge where I can shed the weight of being Harper Mills, successful student, and just be Harper. A person who's scared and uncertain and doesn't have all the answers. A person who's allowed to question whether the path she's on is actually the right one, or just the expected one.

I don't tell Madison about it. I don't tell Caroline, even though we live in the same small room and she's practical enough that she might even understand. I don't tell anyone.

The library is too precious. Too necessary. Sharing it would make it real in a way that might ruin it. Right now, my midnight visits exist in a separate space from my daytime life—a pocket of time that doesn't count against the rules and expectations that govern everything else.

If I told someone, I'd have to defend it. Justify it. Explain why I'm willing to break Thornbridge's code of conduct and risk my Honor Council position for a few hours of peace.

And I'm not sure I could articulate it in a way that would make sense to anyone else.

So I keep it secret. My sanctuary. My salvation.

That night, I return to the library at 12:15 AM. I climb through the window, ease it almost-closed behind me, and make my way to my second-floor corner. The wingback chair is empty, waiting. The windows frame the quad in moonlight. The books surround me like old friends.

I settle in and feel the anxiety that's been building all day—through classes and meals and conversations and the constant pressure to be perfect—finally start to dissolve.

Out there, I'm Harper Mills, junior class Honor Council representative, pre-law track, 4.0 GPA, on track for Ivy League admissions.

In here, I'm just Harper. And for these few hours, that's enough.
