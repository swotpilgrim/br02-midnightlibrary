# Chapter 21: Crisis Point

Four days. I haven't been to the library in four days.

Twelve days left until the renovation starts, and I'm wasting them. But I can't make myself climb through that window, can't face the possibility that Evan won't be there or that he will be. Both options feel impossible.

So I lie awake in my dorm room instead, staring at the ceiling while Caroline sleeps, and I pretend I'm honoring my promise to her. I pretend this is the responsible choice.

But mostly I just feel hollow.

During the day, I go through the motions. Classes, meals, study sessions. I sit with Madison and nod at appropriate moments. I attend Honor Council meetings. I participate in debate practice with the precision that's won me tournaments.

But I'm not really there. I'm somewhere else—somewhere dark and quiet, surrounded by emergency lights and the ghost of conversations with the one person who saw past my performance.

"Harper!"

Madison's sharp voice cuts through my thoughts. I'm standing in the debate room, holding talking points I don't remember picking up, while the rest of the team stares at me with varying degrees of concern and annoyance.

"Sorry, what?"

Madison exchanges a look with our debate coach, Mr. Harrison. "I asked you twice to run through your rebuttal for Saturday's tournament. Are you even listening?"

"I'm listening. Just...tired."

"You've been tired for a week," Madison says, irritation edging her voice. "What's going on? You missed our study session Tuesday. You barely said ten words at dinner last night. And right now you're supposed to be demolishing Emma's argument, but instead you're just standing there."

The rest of the team shifts uncomfortably. Emma, a sophomore who's usually terrified of me, looks sympathetic.

"I'm fine," I say automatically. "Just stressed about exams."

"We're all stressed about exams," Madison counters. "But the rest of us are still functioning. You're literally the anchor of our debate team, Harper. If you fall apart now, we're done for the tournament."

Something about the way she says it—*you're the anchor*—makes anger flash through me. Not: are you okay? Just: don't let us down.

"Maybe I don't care about the tournament," I hear myself say.

The room goes silent.

Madison blinks. "Excuse me?"

"Maybe I don't care." The words are coming out now, and I can't seem to stop them. "Maybe I'm tired of performing for judges and colleges and everyone who treats debate like it's the most important thing in the world when actually it's just teenagers arguing about policy they'll never implement."

Mr. Harrison clears his throat. "Miss Mills, perhaps you should take the rest of practice off. Get some rest."

It's not a suggestion. I grab my bag and leave before anyone can say anything else, Madison's shocked expression burning into my memory.

In the hallway, I lean against the wall and close my eyes. What is wrong with me? I just sabotaged myself in front of my debate coach and my entire team. Madison will tell Victoria, who'll wonder if I'm stable enough for Honor Council leadership. Mr. Harrison will reconsider me as anchor for the tournament.

I'm unraveling, and I can't seem to stop it.

---

Victoria finds me later, in the library. The regular library, during normal hours, where I'm supposedly studying for my pre-law seminar but actually just staring at the same page I've been staring at for twenty minutes.

"Harper. Can we talk?"

I look up to find Victoria Ashford standing next to my table, her expression carefully neutral in that way that means she's about to say something serious. She's wearing her Honor Council pin on her blazer, and seeing it makes my stomach twist with guilt.

"Sure." I close my book.

Victoria sits across from me, folding her hands on the table with the same precise manner she uses in council meetings. "I wanted to talk to you about junior class nominations. For Honor Council senior leadership positions."

Right. The nominations are next week. I'd almost forgotten.

"You've been on my radar for vice president," Victoria continues. "Your academic record is excellent, you've served on the council for two years, and you generally represent Thornbridge's values well."

*Generally.* The qualifier hangs in the air.

"However," Victoria says carefully, "I've been hearing some concerning things. Madison mentioned you've been distracted, unfocused. Mr. Harrison said you walked out of debate practice today. And I've noticed myself that you seem...different lately. Less engaged."

I should defend myself. Should explain that I'm just stressed, that it's temporary, that I'll get back on track. But I can't make the words come.

"The Honor Council vice presidency is a significant responsibility," Victoria continues. "It requires someone who's consistent, reliable, fully committed to upholding school standards. Someone who serves as a role model for the student body."

"Someone perfect," I say quietly.

Victoria's expression softens slightly. "Someone authentic. Someone who actually lives the values they're supposed to represent, not just performs them."

The word *authentic* lands like a physical blow. I almost laugh. If Victoria knew how inauthentic I've been—sneaking out, breaking rules, lying to my roommate—she'd never consider me for anything.

"I want you to think about whether you're in a place to take on this role," Victoria says. "Because if you're struggling now, the added pressure of vice presidency will only make it worse. And I'd rather have you step back and take care of yourself than burn out trying to be something you're not ready to be."

She means well. I can see it in her face—she's genuinely concerned, trying to be kind. But all I hear is: *you're failing*, *you're not good enough*, *you're becoming a liability*.

"I'll think about it," I manage.

Victoria nods and stands. "Good. Nominations are next Monday. Take the weekend, figure out where your head is. And Harper? Whatever's going on—it's okay to ask for help. You don't have to have everything together all the time."

She leaves, and I sit alone at the table surrounded by other students studying, laughing, living their uncomplicated lives. Nobody else seems to be falling apart. Nobody else seems to be lying awake every night wondering who they actually are underneath all the achievements and expectations.

Everybody else seems to fit. Seems to know how to be the person Thornbridge wants them to be without losing themselves in the process.

---

That night, I lie in my bed and catalog my relationships.

Madison: cares about my performance, not my person. Values me for what I can do for her debate team, her study group, her social standing. Has no idea who I actually am.

Caroline: well-meaning but rigid, more concerned with rules than understanding. Would rather I follow protocol than be honest about what I need.

Victoria: sees me as a representative of Thornbridge values, a role model, a trajectory. Wants me to be authentic but only within carefully prescribed boundaries.

My mother: who calls once a week to ask about my GPA and college applications and whether I'm maintaining my Honor Council position. Who'd be devastated if I told her I'm not sure I want to be a lawyer, that I'm tired of performing excellence, that I've been sneaking out to break rules and be with a boy she'd never approve of.

I'm surrounded by people. My days are full of conversations and meetings and group activities. But I'm completely alone. Because nobody actually knows me. Nobody sees past the carefully constructed image of Harper Mills, perfect student, perfect daughter, perfect representative of everything Thornbridge stands for.

Except Evan.

Evan, who saw through the performance from the beginning. Who asked real questions and listened to real answers. Who let me be imperfect and uncertain and afraid without trying to fix me or mold me into something more acceptable.

Evan, who I haven't seen in four days because I'm too much of a coward to face him. Too afraid to admit that he was right about me—that when it comes down to it, I'll choose the safety of my perfect image over the risk of real connection.

I roll over and pull my blanket up to my chin. The room is dark except for the faint moonlight through the window. Caroline's breathing is even and peaceful. The clock on my desk reads 2:47 AM.

I could go to the library right now. Climb through the window, see if Evan is there, try to explain...what? That I've been too scared to face him? That I don't know how to choose between my carefully planned future and the only authentic relationship I've ever had? That I'm paralyzed by fear of making the wrong choice?

Twelve days left until the renovation. Twelve more chances to sit in that sanctuary, to be honest, to be real.

And I'm wasting them hiding in my dorm room, lying awake alone, pretending that's the same thing as being safe.

The truth crashes over me with sudden, painful clarity: I'm not protecting my future by staying away from the library. I'm destroying my present. I'm choosing emptiness over risk, performance over authenticity, the approval of people who don't really know me over connection with the one person who does.

And for what? So I can be Honor Council vice president? So I can maintain my perfect GPA and get into an Ivy League school and become a lawyer I'm not sure I want to be?

So I can spend the rest of my life surrounded by people while being completely alone?

I sit up in bed, my heart pounding. Caroline stirs but doesn't wake. The clock now reads 2:53 AM.

I could still go. It's not too late. Evan might be there, might not. But either way, I'd be choosing to show up. Choosing to risk being honest instead of safe.

But what if he's not there? What if he's given up on me, decided I'm exactly what he feared I'd be—someone who talks about authenticity but chooses reputation when it matters?

What if I've already lost the only real thing I had?

The fear is paralyzing. But lying here alone in the dark feels like a different kind of loss. A slower kind of dying.

I need to decide what I'm willing to sacrifice. What I actually want. Whether I'm brave enough to choose authenticity over approval.

I need to decide if Evan Cross is worth risking everything.

No—that's the wrong question. The real question is whether being honest, being real, being myself is worth risking everything.

Whether I'm worth fighting for.

I lie back down, but I know I won't sleep. The choice is forming in my mind, taking shape, becoming clear in a way it hasn't been before.

Twelve days left.

Tomorrow night, I'm going back to the library.

Whatever happens, I'm done hiding.
