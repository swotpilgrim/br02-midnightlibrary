# Chapter 23: Clarity

I don't sleep for two days.

Not because of insomnia. Because every time I close my eyes, I see Evan walking away. Hear him saying *maybe it was never real*. Feel the empty space where something important used to be.

So I don't sleep. I go to classes and take notes. I sit in the dining hall and push food around. I attend meetings and nod at appropriate moments. I perform the role of Harper Mills, model student, while inside I feel like I'm disappearing.

On the second day, I'm in an Honor Council meeting. We're discussing dress code violations—a junior wore ripped jeans to assembly, a sophomore's skirt was too short, someone was seen without a blazer in the academic building. Victoria is going through the infractions with her usual thoroughness, and everyone is taking it seriously because this is Thornbridge and appearances matter and rules exist for a reason.

And something in me just...breaks.

I'm sitting in this room full of accomplished students, all of us wearing our blazers with our Honor Council pins, all of us upholding Thornbridge's standards of excellence and propriety. We're the model students, the leaders, the ones other people are supposed to look up to.

And I suddenly see us with painful clarity.

We're not role models. We're performing. Every single one of us is carefully curating who we appear to be, maintaining the exact right balance of achievement and humility. We're resume builders masquerading as people.

And nobody in this room actually knows me.

Victoria is talking about the importance of consistent enforcement, and I look around the table. These are my peers—students I've served with for two years, people I see every week, colleagues in the important work of upholding school standards.

I don't know any of them. Not really. I know their academic tracks and extracurricular achievements. I know which colleges they're applying to and what leadership positions they hold. But I don't know what keeps them up at night or what they're afraid of or who they are when they're not performing for judges and teachers and college admissions officers.

And they don't know me.

They know Harper Mills, 4.0 GPA, debate team anchor, pre-law track. They don't know the girl who lies awake every night feeling like she's suffocating under the weight of expectations. The girl who's been sneaking out to break into the library. The girl who fell for the isolated transfer student everyone dismisses.

They see the performance. They praise the performance. But they have no idea who I actually am underneath it.

"Harper? Do you agree?"

Victoria's voice cuts through my spiraling thoughts. Everyone is looking at me, waiting for my opinion on something I didn't hear.

"I'm sorry, what?"

Victoria's eyebrows raise slightly. "I asked if you agreed that we should recommend a formal warning for the dress code violations. You seem distracted."

"I'm fine." The automatic response. "And yes, formal warnings seem appropriate."

Victoria makes a note and moves on, but I see her exchange a look with another council member. *Harper's not herself lately.* I can practically hear them thinking it.

The meeting continues. I say the right things at the right moments. But I'm not really there. I'm watching myself from a distance, watching this girl in her blazer with her Honor Council pin dutifully discussing skirt lengths and proper attire, and I want to scream.

This isn't me. Or maybe it is me, and that's the problem. Maybe this is who I've become—a collection of achievements and appropriate responses and careful performances. Maybe there's nothing underneath the polish.

Except there was. For a few months, late at night in the library, there was someone real underneath. Someone honest and uncertain and imperfect. Someone Evan saw and accepted and cared about.

And I let her go because I was too afraid to be her in daylight.

---

After the meeting, I go back to my dorm room and lie on my bed, staring at the ceiling.

I catalog my relationships like I'm making a list for a debate:

*Madison Riley, best friend:* Cares about my performance on the debate team. Values my ability to help her study. Appreciates me as long as I'm useful, successful, consistently excellent. Has no idea I've been struggling with insomnia. Doesn't know I've been sneaking out. Would be confused and disappointed if I told her I'm reconsidering pre-law. Values what I can do for her, not who I am.

*Caroline Fletcher, roommate:* Well-meaning but fundamentally concerned with rules and propriety. Discovered me breaking curfew and immediately threatened to report me. Offered friendship but only on the condition that I conform. Softened when I revealed vulnerability, but still prioritized institutional rules over genuine understanding. Would rather I follow protocol than be honest about what I need.

*Victoria Ashford, Honor Council president:* Sees me as a representative of Thornbridge values. A trajectory, not a person. Wants me to be vice president but only if I can maintain perfect consistency. Advised me to "figure out where my head is" but only in terms of whether I can serve the institution effectively. Cares about my authenticity, but only within carefully prescribed boundaries. Values me as a symbol, not as a person.

*My mother:* Who I call once a week to report my GPA and college application progress. Who'd be devastated to learn I'm questioning the legal career she's planned for me since middle school. Who sees my achievements as validation of her parenting and my worth as her daughter. Who loves the successful version of me but has no idea who I am when I'm not succeeding.

I'm surrounded by people. My days are full of interactions and conversations and group activities. But I'm completely, utterly alone.

Because nobody actually knows me. Nobody sees past the carefully maintained facade of Harper Mills, perfect student. Nobody asks what I want or who I am or what scares me.

Nobody except Evan.

Evan, who saw through the performance the first time we talked. Who asked real questions and listened to real answers. Who let me be anxious and uncertain and imperfect without trying to fix me. Who understood that the girl lying awake at three in the morning was more authentic than the girl collecting debate trophies.

Evan, who I let walk away because I couldn't find the courage to choose authenticity over approval.

The realization settles over me like a weight: Evan was wrong about one crucial thing.

He said I was slumming it with the outcast, that I'd eventually return to my real life and realize he was just a brief rebellion. But it wasn't slumming. It wasn't rebellion.

It was the opposite.

Those nights in the library—those were the only times I was being real. The girl who sat with Evan at midnight and talked about fears and dreams and the suffocating weight of being perfect—that was me. The actual me, underneath all the performance and achievement and carefully cultivated image.

And the life I've been calling "real"—the Honor Council meetings and debate tournaments and pre-law seminars—that's the fantasy. That's the role I'm playing for everyone else's benefit.

I've had it backward this entire time.

I thought I was risking my real life for something temporary and ultimately meaningless. But what if I've been risking my actual self for a life that's all performance and no substance?

What if the girl in the library at midnight is more real than the girl collecting accomplishments?

What if Evan was the only person who ever really knew me, and I was too afraid to let anyone else see that version of myself?

The clarity is devastating and liberating in equal measure.

I've spent my entire life building a perfect image. Perfect grades, perfect resume, perfect trajectory toward the future my mother wants and Thornbridge expects. I've performed excellence so consistently that I forgot it was a performance. I thought the girl everyone praised was the real me.

But she's not. She's a carefully constructed facade designed to earn approval and avoid disappointment. She's what everyone expects Harper Mills to be, not who I actually am.

And I'm so tired of maintaining the facade.

I sit up in bed, my heart pounding with something that feels like revelation. It's three in the afternoon—Caroline is at field hockey practice, the dorm is quiet, and for the first time in days I can think clearly.

Evan said I'd choose my perfect future over him. And he was right to be afraid of that, because that's exactly what I've been doing. Every time I chose not to acknowledge him in the hallway, every time I sat with Madison and her dismissive comments about "the weird transfer kid," every time I prioritized my reputation over our connection—I was choosing the performance over the reality.

But Evan was also wrong about one thing: he said I wasn't brave enough to risk my perfect life for him.

He thought the question was whether he was worth the risk.

But the real question isn't whether Evan is worth it. It's whether I'm worth it. Whether the authentic version of me—the one who doesn't have all the answers, who struggles and questions and breaks rules—whether she deserves to exist in daylight instead of just in secret.

Whether I'm brave enough to stop performing and start being real.

I've been asking the wrong questions. I've been so focused on what I'd lose—Honor Council position, debate tournaments, perfect GPA, mother's approval—that I never stopped to ask what I'd gain.

Maybe I'd gain myself. The actual version, not the carefully curated one.

Maybe I'd gain relationships where people know me instead of just knowing my accomplishments.

Maybe I'd gain the courage to live a life I actually want instead of the life everyone expects.

And maybe—maybe—I'd gain Evan back. Not because he makes me complete or because I need him to be happy. But because he's the one person who ever saw the real me and thought she was worth knowing.

The revelation doesn't solve anything. I still have nine days until the renovation. I still don't know how to bridge the gap between my daytime life and my nighttime truth. I still don't know if Evan will forgive me for choosing safety over authenticity, or if I can find the courage to choose differently.

But at least I understand now. At least I can see clearly what I've been doing and what it's cost me.

I've been so afraid of losing everything that I lost myself instead.

And if I want any chance of getting myself back—if I want any chance of living authentically instead of just performing—I need to stop being afraid.

I need to choose truth over approval.

I need to choose me.

Starting now.
