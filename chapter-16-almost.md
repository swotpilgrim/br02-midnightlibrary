# Chapter 16: Almost

It's two in the morning when Evan does his impression of Mr. Carmichael, our English teacher, and I have to clap both hands over my mouth to keep from laughing loud enough to wake the entire campus.

"The *symbolism*," Evan intones, his voice pitched into that particularly nasal register that Carmichael uses when he's being pretentious, "is not merely a *literary device*—it is a *window into the very soul* of the human condition."

I'm shaking with suppressed laughter, my shoulders trembling. The emergency lighting casts Evan's face in alternating shadow and warmth, and his eyes are dancing with amusement. I've never seen him like this—playful, unguarded, fully present. For the past hour, we've been dissecting the absurdity of our classes, trading observations about teachers and students with the kind of easy irreverence that only comes when you feel completely safe.

"Stop," I manage between giggles. "I can't breathe."

"He actually said that today," Evan continues, grinning now. "About *The Great Gatsby*. Like Fitzgerald was performing literary surgery instead of just telling a story about people who make terrible choices."

"To be fair," I say, finally catching my breath, "they do make spectacularly terrible choices."

"True. But Carmichael acts like every green light is a profound meditation on desire instead of just, you know, a green light."

I lean back in my chair, still smiling. "You're terrible."

"You love it."

And the thing is—I do. This lightness between us is new. For weeks, our conversations have been intense, meaningful, weighted with the things we've never told anyone else. But tonight feels different. Tonight we're just two people who've found something rare: someone who sees the same absurdities, who laughs at the same moments of pretension, who understands without explanation.

The laughter fades into comfortable silence, but something's shifted in the air between us. It feels charged, electric. I'm suddenly aware of how close we're sitting—when did that happen? Our usual careful distance has dissolved over the past hour, and now there's maybe six inches between us. I can see the exact gray-green shade of his eyes in the dim light, can count his eyelashes if I wanted to.

"You're different at night," Evan says quietly, his voice losing its playful edge. "More yourself."

My heart does something complicated in my chest. "So are you."

He holds my gaze, and I can't look away. During the day, Evan keeps his expression carefully neutral, almost blank. But here, now, I can see everything—the intelligence that's always analyzing, the vulnerability he works so hard to hide, the loneliness he's carried since arriving at Thornbridge. And something else. Something that makes my breath catch.

The silence stretches between us, but it's not awkward. It's full of all the things we're not saying, all the careful boundaries we've been maintaining for weeks. The library feels smaller suddenly, like the shadows have pressed in around us, creating a space that contains only this: him, me, the understanding that we've become something more than two people who happen to share a secret.

I should say something. Break the tension with a joke or a question about homework or anything that would restore the comfortable friendship we've built. But I can't make myself speak.

Evan reaches across the space between us. His hand moves slowly, deliberately, giving me time to pull back if I want to. I don't move. His fingers brush my cheek, gentle and warm, and then he tucks a strand of hair behind my ear. It's such a simple gesture, intimate and tender, and it steals whatever breath I had left.

We're inches apart now. I don't remember moving closer, but I must have. Or maybe he did. Maybe we both did, drawn together by some gravitational pull I don't fully understand but can't resist.

His eyes search mine, asking a question I can barely process through the hammering of my heart. I can feel the warmth radiating from him, can see the way his chest rises and falls with each breath. Time has slowed down, stretched like taffy, and there's only this moment: the library shadows, the smell of old books and furniture polish, the feeling of his hand still resting near my face.

I lean forward. Just slightly. Just enough to close an inch of distance.

He mirrors the movement, and my heart is pounding so loud I'm certain he can hear it. I'm certain everyone in Massachusetts can hear it. This is happening. We're going to kiss. Here, in our sanctuary, surrounded by emergency lighting and a century of books, Evan Cross is going to kiss me and I'm going to let him and everything is going to change.

Our faces are so close I can feel his breath on my lips. His hand moves from my hair to cup my jaw, his thumb brushing my cheekbone, and I think I might shatter from how much I want this. His eyes drop to my mouth and then back up to meet mine, one last silent question.

I close my eyes and lean in the final distance, and—

Footsteps.

Distant but approaching. The particular heavy tread of an adult in no hurry, echoing through the library's main floor.

Security.

We spring apart like we've been electrocuted. Evan's eyes go wide, and I can see my own panic reflected there. How did we not hear them coming? How did we let ourselves get so lost in the moment that we forgot where we were, forgot the risk, forgot everything except each other?

"Go," Evan whispers urgently, already grabbing his backpack.

I snatch my bag from the floor, my hands shaking so badly I almost drop it. The footsteps are getting closer—they're in the main reading room now, maybe thirty seconds from the stairs. My heart is racing for entirely different reasons, adrenaline flooding my system.

Evan gestures toward the back staircase, the one that leads to the third floor and the rear exit. I nod and start moving, keeping low, trying to control my breathing. Behind me, I can hear Evan heading the opposite direction, toward our usual window exit.

The stairs creak under my feet and I freeze, but the footsteps below don't change pattern. Whoever it is hasn't heard me. I take the remaining stairs as quickly as I dare, reaching the third floor just as I hear a voice below: "Hello? Anyone up there?"

I don't stop. I run on my toes toward the back of the building, toward the window I've never used but have always kept in mind as an emergency exit. My fingers fumble with the old latch—please don't be stuck, please—and then it gives and the window slides open with a sound that seems deafening in the silence.

The January air hits me like a slap, cold enough to steal my breath. I don't care. I climb through the window onto the fire escape, close it behind me, and press myself flat against the brick wall. Below me, the campus is dark and still. I can't see Evan. I don't know if he made it out or if security found him or if—

No. He's smart. He's careful. He made it.

I wait two minutes that feel like twenty, then climb down the fire escape as quietly as possible. The metal is freezing under my hands, and I'm shaking—from cold, from adrenaline, from the memory of how close we came to kissing, from the terror of almost being caught.

When I reach the ground, I run.

I stick to the shadows, avoid the main paths, take the long way around to Hargrove Hall. My breath comes in white clouds, and my fingers are numb by the time I reach the side door we sometimes leave propped open. It's still wedged, thank God. I slip inside, ease the door closed, and stand in the warm hallway trying to get my breathing under control.

The dorm is silent except for the familiar sounds: pipes clanking, someone's muffled music playing through headphones, the general settling sounds of an old building full of sleeping teenagers. Safe. Normal. Nothing like the wild drumbeat of my heart or the chaos of thoughts crashing through my mind.

I climb the stairs to my floor, moving on muscle memory, and carefully open the door to my room. Caroline is asleep, her breathing deep and even. I undress in the dark, pulling on my pajamas and sliding under my covers.

I should sleep. It's nearly three in the morning, and I have a full day of classes tomorrow. But I'm wide awake, staring at the ceiling, my lips still tingling with the ghost of a kiss that didn't happen.

We were so close. Another ten seconds and Evan would have kissed me. I would have kissed him back. And then what? Would everything have been different? Or would we still be two people hiding in the dark, still keeping our connection secret, still living two separate lives?

I press my fingers to my mouth, remembering the exact distance between us, the look in his eyes, the gentle way he touched my face. The wanting was so clear, so mutual. This isn't one-sided. Whatever is happening between us, we're both falling into it.

And that terrifies me almost as much as it thrills me.

Because this isn't just a friendship anymore. This isn't just two insomniacs finding comfort in late-night conversations. This is something that could upend my entire carefully constructed life. This is Evan Cross—the isolated transfer student everyone dismisses—becoming the person I think about constantly, the person whose almost-kiss has me lying awake with my heart still racing hours later.

I roll onto my side, pulling my blanket up to my chin, and try to slow my breathing. Tomorrow I'll see him in calculus class. We'll sit in our usual spots—me in the third row, him in the back—and we won't acknowledge each other. We'll maintain the careful fiction that we're strangers who happen to attend the same school.

But tomorrow night, when I climb through that library window, everything will be different. We'll have to talk about what almost happened. We'll have to decide if we're going to let it happen for real, or if we're going to pretend that moment never existed.

Sleep, I tell myself firmly. But my mind replays the scene on an endless loop: his hand in my hair, our faces inches apart, the wanting so strong it felt like gravity. The footsteps. The running. The cold.

The question I can't answer: what happens next?

I finally drift off sometime after four, still tasting the possibility of a kiss that never was.
