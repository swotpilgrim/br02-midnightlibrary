# Chapter 13: Unspoken Pressure

The comment comes at dinner on Tuesday, casual and cutting.

I'm sitting with my usual group—Madison, Caroline, Julia, and a few others from the pre-law track. We're eating the dining hall's attempt at chicken parmesan while Madison complains about her English teacher's grading standards.

Caroline, who's usually quiet during these meals, suddenly says, "Does anyone else think it's weird that Evan Cross barely talks to anyone?"

My fork stops halfway to my mouth.

"Who?" Julia asks.

"Transfer kid. Junior. Always sits alone." Caroline gestures vaguely toward the other end of the dining hall where Evan is, predictably, eating by himself with a book. "He's been here since September and I've literally never heard him speak. It's strange."

"Maybe he's just shy," I hear myself say.

Madison shakes her head. "I don't think it's shyness. My cousin goes to Dalton—that's where he came from. She said there was some kind of scandal. He probably got kicked out."

"What kind of scandal?" Julia leans in, always hungry for gossip.

"She didn't know details. But it must have been bad if his parents sent him here mid-high school. Nobody transfers to boarding school junior year unless something went really wrong."

The conversation continues around me—speculation about what Evan might have done, whether he's troubled or just antisocial, why anyone would choose to be so isolated.

I should defend him. Should say something. Anything.

But what can I say? "Actually, I know him really well because we spend every night breaking curfew together in the library"? "He's not antisocial, he's self-protective because his girlfriend betrayed him and his friends abandoned him"?

I can't say any of that without revealing everything.

So I stay silent.

And my silence feels like active betrayal.

"I honestly don't know why he even bothers coming here if he's going to act like that," Caroline continues. "Thornbridge is a community. If you're not going to participate, what's the point?"

"Maybe he has his reasons," I try again, knowing it's inadequate.

Madison gives me a strange look. "Why are you defending him?"

"I'm not defending him. I'm just saying maybe there's more to the story than we know."

"Or maybe he's exactly what he seems—antisocial and weird." Madison dismisses it and changes the subject to college applications.

I stare at my plate, appetite gone.

Across the dining hall, Evan turns a page of his book, completely unaware that people are speculating about why he's so isolated. Or maybe he's aware and just doesn't care anymore.

I wonder which is worse.

After dinner, I have Honor Council. As I'm heading to the meeting room, Victoria Ashford falls into step beside me.

"Harper, do you have a minute before the meeting?"

"Sure."

We step into an empty classroom. Victoria closes the door, and something about her expression makes anxiety spike in my chest.

"Is everything okay?" I ask.

"Yes, everything's fine. I just wanted to check in with you. Make sure you're doing well."

"I'm doing fine."

Victoria studies me for a moment. "You know, as junior class representative, you're in a unique position. You're a role model for underclassmen. A voice of leadership on the council. People watch who you are and who you associate with."

The words are careful, but the message is clear.

"I understand," I say, keeping my voice neutral.

"I'm sure you do. You've been an excellent council member. Thoughtful, principled, committed to Thornbridge's standards. I'd hate to see anything compromise that."

"Is there something specific you're concerned about?"

"Not at all. Just a general reminder that junior year is when colleges really start paying attention. Your reputation matters. Your associations matter. We hold ourselves—and each other—to higher standards."

The subtext is deafening: Stay in your lane. Maintain your reputation. Don't associate with people like Evan Cross.

"I appreciate the advice," I manage.

"Good. I'm glad we understand each other." Victoria smiles, warm and patronizing. "Shall we head to the meeting?"

I follow her to Honor Council feeling like I've swallowed broken glass.

The meeting itself is routine—reviewing policy updates, discussing a minor dress code violation, planning the semester-end report. I contribute when expected, say the right things, play my role perfectly.

But all I can think about is Victoria's "friendly reminder" that my associations matter. That being seen with the wrong people could damage my carefully maintained reputation.

That I'm supposed to care more about what others think than about... what? Fairness? Honesty? Evan?

After the meeting, I go back to my room and try to do homework. But I can't focus. Caroline's dismissive comments about Evan keep replaying in my head. Madison's assumption that he must have done something wrong. Victoria's pointed reminder about reputation and associations.

None of them know Evan. They've decided who he is based on isolation and rumors and the fact that he doesn't perform the kind of social engagement Thornbridge values.

And I'm complicit in that judgment because I don't defend him. Because defending him would require explaining how I know he's thoughtful and funny and kind. Would require admitting that the weird loner everyone dismisses is actually the person I talk to more than anyone else.

Would require choosing between Evan and my reputation.

At 11:30, Caroline falls asleep. I lie in bed until midnight, then slip out as usual.

The library is cold tonight. November has shifted into early December, and the old heating system can't keep up. I settle into my chair with my history textbook, but I'm too agitated to read. Everything from today sits heavy in my chest.

Evan arrives at 1:05 AM. He takes his usual chair, closer than six feet now—maybe three, close enough that if we both reached out we could easily touch.

"Hey," he says.

"Hey."

He pulls out his current book—finished with Woolf, moved on to Salinger—but doesn't open it. Just looks at me.

"Are you okay? You seem tense."

The question is gentle, concerned. He knows me well enough now to read my moods.

I should tell him. Should confess that people were talking about him at dinner, that Victoria basically warned me to stay away from him, that I'm trapped between two lives and don't know how to choose.

But I can't make myself say it.

Because telling Evan what Victoria said would mean admitting that I'm ashamed to acknowledge him publicly. That I'm choosing reputation over integrity. That I'm exactly the kind of person who cares more about appearances than truth—the kind of person who hurt him at his old school.

"Just stressed about school," I lie. "College essay is killing me."

Evan accepts this, probably because he's used to people keeping things from him. "Want to talk about it?"

"Not really. Just want to sit here and not think for a while."

"I can do that."

We sit in silence, but it doesn't have the comfortable quality it usually does. I'm too aware of everything unsaid between us. Too conscious of the fact that six hours ago, I sat silent while my friends dismissed him as weird and antisocial.

After about twenty minutes, Evan asks, "Harper, did something happen?"

"What do you mean?"

"You're being weird. Different. If I did something or said something—"

"You didn't," I interrupt. "I promise. It's not you."

"Then what is it?"

I want to tell him. Want to be honest the way we're supposed to be with each other.

But the words won't come.

Because if I tell him that Victoria warned me about my associations, I'm admitting that being associated with him is considered a problem. That my friends think he's troubled. That everyone at Thornbridge has written him off, and I've done nothing to challenge that narrative.

If I tell him the truth, I have to own my cowardice.

And I'm not ready to do that.

"Just tired," I say finally. "Not sleeping enough."

It's such an inadequate answer. Evan knows it's inadequate. But he doesn't push.

"Okay," he says quietly.

We try to fall into our usual routine—talking, reading, the comfortable companionship we've built over weeks of midnight meetings. But something's off. The ease between us has developed a sharp edge.

Because I'm lying. Not overtly, but by omission. And Evan can sense it even if he doesn't know what I'm hiding.

At 2:30, earlier than usual, I pack up my things.

"Leaving already?" Evan asks.

"Yeah. Early class tomorrow."

I don't have an early class. We both know I don't have an early class. But Evan doesn't call me on the lie.

"See you tomorrow night?" he asks.

"Yeah. Tomorrow."

I slip out through the window and walk back to Hargrove Hall feeling worse than when I arrived.

Because tonight, I brought my daytime life's dishonesty into our nighttime sanctuary. I let the pressure and judgment and social politics contaminate the one space where we're supposed to be honest with each other.

I let Caroline's dismissiveness and Madison's assumptions and Victoria's pointed warnings into my head. And instead of pushing back against them, instead of defending Evan or defending our friendship or defending truth over reputation, I retreated into silence.

I chose comfort over courage.

And now I'm lying in bed staring at the ceiling (forty-three water stains, still there, always there) knowing that I'm becoming exactly the kind of person I didn't think I was.

The kind who cares more about what people think than about what's right.

The kind who stays silent when someone they care about is being dismissed and judged.

The kind who chooses reputation over integrity.

I don't know how to fix this. Don't know how to bridge the gap between daytime Harper and nighttime Harper without destroying one or both versions.

All I know is that the cognitive dissonance is becoming unbearable.

And something has to give.
