# Chapter 20: Ticking Clock

The announcement comes during a mandatory assembly on a gray January afternoon.

I'm sitting with Madison in the chapel, half-asleep like most students, expecting the usual announcements about athletic schedules and exams.

Then Dr. Whitmore steps up to the podium, and something in her expression makes me sit up straighter.

"I'm pleased to announce that our historic library will undergo significant renovations beginning at the end of this month," she begins, her voice carrying the pride of someone about to reveal a long-awaited improvement. "Thanks to a generous donation from the class of 1975, we'll be updating the security systems, improving the climate control to better preserve our rare books collection, and addressing various maintenance issues that have accumulated over the building's hundred-and-thirty-year history."

Polite applause ripples through the assembly. I keep my hands folded in my lap, my pulse suddenly loud in my ears.

"The renovation will include new electronic locks, updated lighting, and—most importantly—replacement of all window hardware, including several latches that have failed over the years."

My stomach drops.

"The contractor begins work January 28th," she says with satisfaction. "I'm particularly pleased that we'll finally have proper security at all hours. No more faulty latches or windows that don't quite close properly."

She smiles like this is unambiguously good news. Around me, students nod or look vaguely interested or continue scrolling through their phones hidden in their laps. No one else understands what she just announced.

She just announced the end of my sanctuary.

I do the math automatically. Today is January 12th. Sixteen days until the renovation starts, and then every window will be replaced, every latch fixed. The library will be sealed.

Sixteen days until I can never climb through that window again.

Dr. Whitmore continues talking, but I've stopped listening. The window that's been our way in will be gone in just over two weeks.

And Evan and I still haven't resolved things from our argument.

I haven't seen him since he left me alone in the library with his questions hanging in the air. Haven't climbed through the window. Haven't texted—we don't text, it's too risky. We exist only in that nighttime space, and now that space has an expiration date.

The assembly ends. Students file out, talking and laughing. Madison touches my arm.

"You okay? You look pale."

"Fine. Just thinking about exams."

She nods and launches into a review schedule proposal. I make agreeable sounds while my mind spins.

Sixteen days. Not even two and a half weeks.

After that, even if I wanted to keep going to the library—even if Evan wanted to keep meeting me—we couldn't. The physical space that allowed our connection to exist will be sealed off.

Maybe that's for the best, a voice suggests. A clean ending, saving us from the impossible choice.

But another voice says this feels like losing something essential before I fully understood what I had.

---

I go to the library that night.

I don't expect Evan to be there. We've been avoiding each other for two days, letting his ultimatum sit between us like a wall.

But when I climb through the window, he's already there.

He looks up, surprise flickering across his face. "I didn't think you'd come."

"I didn't think you would either."

We stare at each other, all the things we didn't say two nights ago still hanging between us. But there's something else now—urgency created by the knowledge that our time here is limited.

"Did you hear about the renovation?" I ask.

Evan nods. "I was at the assembly."

We were in the same room, both hearing the news and understanding what it meant in a way no one else did.

I sit down. After a moment, Evan sits too. The silence is heavy with everything—the argument we haven't resolved, the feelings we haven't named, the deadline we just discovered.

"Two and a half weeks," I finally say. "That's all we have left."

"Yeah."

I wait for him to say more, but he doesn't. He's looking at his hands, and I can see the tension in his shoulders, the way he's holding himself carefully like he's afraid of what might happen if he relaxes.

"Evan—"

"Maybe it's for the best," he says quickly, cutting me off. "A clean ending. We don't have to figure out all those impossible questions about what you're willing to risk or what I'm worth or whether this has a future. The renovation makes the decision for us."

"That's not—" I stop, frustrated. "You can't seriously think this is better. Us just...stopping because a construction crew is replacing some windows."

"Why not?" He finally looks at me. "It's less painful than the alternatives."

"What alternatives?"

"You choosing your perfect future over me. Or worse—you choosing me and regretting it when everything falls apart." His voice is matter-of-fact, like he's already accepted this as inevitable. "At least this way, we end while it's still good. Before reality forces us to make choices we'll hate."

I feel anger rising again, the same frustration from our last argument. "You're doing it again. Deciding for both of us that this can't work, that I'm going to betray you or regret you or whatever scenario you've constructed in your head."

"I'm being realistic—"

"You're being afraid," I interrupt. "You're so terrified of being hurt again that you're ending this before it even has a chance to survive."

"And you're being naive," Evan counters. "You think we can just keep meeting in secret forever? That eventually we won't have to choose between this and everything else? The renovation is just accelerating the inevitable."

"So that's it?" My voice rises slightly, and I force it back down to a whisper. "We have sixteen days left in this place, and you want to spend them...what? Not speaking? Avoiding each other? Pretending we don't exist?"

"I don't know what I want," Evan admits, and for the first time his careful control cracks slightly. "I want to keep seeing you. I want to believe this could somehow work. But I also want to protect myself from the moment when you realize I'm not worth the cost."

"And you've decided that moment is inevitable."

"Haven't you?" He leans forward, genuine question in his eyes. "Honestly, Harper. Can you see this working? Can you see us together outside these walls, in daylight, where everyone can see? Where you'd have to publicly acknowledge the weird transfer kid, where your friends would judge you, where you'd risk everything you've worked for?"

The question deserves honesty. I try to imagine it: walking across the quad with Evan in broad daylight. Sitting with him at lunch while Madison looks on disapprovingly. Holding his hand in the hallway while Caroline worries about my reputation and Victoria reconsiders my Honor Council position.

I can't quite picture it. The image won't form clearly.

"I don't know," I whisper. "I want to say yes, but I can't quite see it."

Evan nods like I've just confirmed what he already believed. "That's why the renovation is a mercy. It gives us an ending before we have to test whether we're brave enough for a beginning."

We sit in silence. The library feels different now—less like a sanctuary and more like a countdown. Sixteen days. Sixteen midnights. Sixteen chances to sit in this space before it's locked away forever.

"So what do we do?" I ask. "Just...stop coming?"

"I don't know." Evan's voice is tired. "Maybe we use the time we have left. Make peace with the ending. Say goodbye properly instead of just disappearing."

"That sounds depressing."

"It sounds honest."

I want to argue with him, to insist that we can figure this out, that the renovation doesn't have to mean the end of us. But sitting here in the emergency lighting with Evan carefully maintaining distance between us, I can't quite make the words believable even to myself.

"Is this what you want?" I ask quietly. "Really?"

Evan is silent for a long moment. "I want what's best for you."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only answer I have." He stands up, shouldering his backpack. "I need time to think. About what I want, about what's fair to both of us. About whether we're just postponing inevitable hurt or if there's actually something here worth fighting for."

"And if there is?" My heart is pounding. "If there is something worth fighting for?"

"Then we have sixteen days to figure out what that fight looks like." He pauses at the window, looking back at me. "But I can't promise I'll be brave enough to take that risk, Harper. I can't promise I won't choose the safe option of just letting this end."

"I can't promise I'll choose differently," I admit.

He nods, something that might be understanding or resignation crossing his face. "Then I guess we're both just as scared."

He climbs through the window and disappears into the January night, leaving me alone in the library with emergency lights and the weight of a deadline I didn't ask for.

Two and a half weeks. Sixteen days. Barely any time at all.

I sit in the silence and wonder if Evan is right—if the renovation is a mercy, saving us from having to discover whether we're brave enough to choose each other when it actually costs something.

Or if it's the cruelest kind of ending: making the decision for us before we can make it for ourselves.

When I finally leave, climbing through the window for the—I count backward—thirty-seventh time, I think: only fifteen more chances.

The library has always been my refuge from the pressure of perfection, the place where I could stop performing and just exist.

But now it feels less like sanctuary and more like a countdown clock, each night ticking closer to the moment when this space, and maybe everything it represents, disappears forever.

I walk back to my dorm through the winter darkness, my breath clouding in the cold, and try to imagine what comes after day sixteen.

I can't see it. Can't picture my life without these midnight hours. Can't imagine going back to lying awake alone, unable to sleep, with no Evan and no library and no escape from the perfectionism that's slowly suffocating me.

But I also can't picture the alternative—actually choosing him publicly, risking everything, living outside the careful boundaries I've maintained my entire life.

Evan was right about one thing: we're both terrified.

The question is whether sixteen days is enough time to become brave.
