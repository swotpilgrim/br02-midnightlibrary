# Chapter 25: Evan's Truth

We sit in our chairs as the hours stretch toward dawn. The emergency lighting seems dimmer now, fading as darkness gives way to gray.

We've been talking for over an hour—about fear, courage, authenticity. But there's still something unspoken. I can feel it in the way Evan keeps starting sentences and stopping, in the way his hands clench on the armrests.

"There's something I need to tell you," he finally says. "Something I've never told anyone. Not completely."

I wait, giving him space.

"You know about the cheating scandal at my old school," he continues, his voice quiet. "What I told you before—that I was falsely accused, that I got caught up in it even though I wasn't involved—that's true. But it's not the whole truth."

The emergency lights flicker slightly, and shadows dance across Evan's face.

"The whole truth is worse," he says. "Not because I was guilty. But because of who betrayed me."

I stay silent, sensing he needs to get this out without interruption.

"Melissa Hayes. My girlfriend." Evan's jaw tightens. "We'd been together almost a year. I thought I could trust her completely. We studied together all the time—I'd share my notes, help her prep for exams. We were planning to apply to the same colleges."

He pauses, steeling himself for what comes next.

"When the cheating ring got exposed, it turned out she was one of the main participants. She'd been part of it for over a semester—stealing tests, sharing answer keys. I had no idea. I trusted her completely and never suspected."

My chest tightens at the pain in his voice.

"When they started investigating, my name came up constantly. Because I'd studied with Melissa and her friends. Because I'd loaned them my notes. Because I'd been in their group chats about assignments. All circumstantial, but it looked bad. Really bad."

Evan stands up and starts pacing, unable to sit still anymore.

"The administration questioned me. My parents hired a lawyer. Other students started avoiding me in the hallways. But I kept thinking it would be okay because I was innocent and Melissa would tell them the truth. She'd explain that I had nothing to do with it, that I didn't know."

He stops pacing and looks at me directly.

"But she didn't. When they questioned her, she said I was involved. She claimed I'd known about the cheating and had helped her. That I'd deliberately given her access to materials I shouldn't have had access to. She threw me under the bus to try to get a lighter punishment for herself."

"Evan," I whisper, horrified.

"My lawyer tore apart her story. The investigators realized she was lying—there was no evidence I'd been involved. She finally admitted I hadn't known, that she'd used me and then lied to protect herself."

He sits back down, not looking at me.

"By the time she confessed the truth, it didn't matter. Everyone had heard the accusations. Teachers looked at me differently. My best friend stopped talking to me. Said he couldn't risk being associated with someone under investigation. Other friends disappeared. Some apologized later, but the damage was done."

"That's why you came to Thornbridge," I say quietly.

"My parents thought a fresh start would help. New school, clean slate. But I brought it with me anyway—the knowledge of what people do when things get difficult. How quickly someone can betray you. How easily friends abandon you."

He finally looks at me again, vulnerability clear in his eyes.

"I came here and decided I wouldn't make the same mistake. No close relationships. No trusting people with anything that matters. I'd get through junior and senior year without getting attached to anyone."

"Isolation as protection," I say.

"Exactly. If I don't let anyone close, no one can betray me. It's lonely, but it's safe."

"But then I kept showing up," I say softly.

Evan's expression softens slightly. "But then you kept showing up. And you were honest when you didn't have to be. You told me about your insomnia, your perfectionism, your fears. You let me see the real version of you instead of the performance. And despite everything I'd learned about trust and betrayal, I started to believe maybe you were different."

"And then I proved I wasn't," I say, shame washing over me. "I stayed away for four days. I couldn't picture choosing you publicly. I did exactly what you were afraid I'd do."

"You did," Evan agrees. "But then you came back. You didn't have to—the easy thing would have been to let the renovation end this for you, to accept the clean break. But you came back and you fought for us. You chose honesty over comfort."

"I should have done it sooner."

"Maybe. But you did it." He reaches across the space between our chairs and takes my hand. "Do you understand why I was so afraid? Why I tried to end things before you could hurt me?"

"Yes," I whisper. "Because Melissa made you believe that when it matters, people will always choose self-preservation."

"And you proved her wrong," Evan says. "You had every reason to choose the safe option—your reputation, your college applications, your mother's approval. But you're sitting here telling me you're willing to risk all of it for authenticity. For us."

"I'm still terrified," I admit.

"So am I," Evan says. "I'm terrified of trusting you and being wrong. Terrified of opening up again and getting destroyed. Terrified that this time tomorrow, or next week, or next month, you'll realize it's too hard and choose the easier path."

"I can't promise I'll be perfect," I say. "I'm going to make mistakes. I'm going to be afraid. I might temporarily freak out when things get difficult."

"I know."

"But I can promise I won't betray you. I won't throw you under the bus to save myself. I won't claim you don't matter when it's convenient." I squeeze his hand. "I'm choosing to trust you with the real me. And I'm asking you to trust me back."

The emergency lights are definitely fading now—not dying, but being overtaken by the growing daylight outside the windows. Our last night in this sanctuary is almost over.

"I want to trust you," Evan says. "I want to believe this is different than what I had with Melissa, that you're different. But I need you to understand—if you betray that trust, I won't survive it. Not again. I barely survived it the first time."

The weight of what he's saying settles over me. This isn't just about whether I'm brave enough to sit with him at lunch or introduce him to my mother. This is about whether I can be trusted with his most vulnerable self. Whether I'll protect his heart instead of breaking it when things get difficult.

"I understand," I say. "And I know I can't prove it with words. I have to prove it through actions. Through choosing you, choosing us, choosing authenticity even when it's scary."

"Especially when it's scary," Evan corrects gently.

"Especially then," I agree.

We sit in silence as the library transforms around us. The emergency lights shut off entirely as natural light fills the space through the tall windows. The shadows recede. The sanctuary becomes just a library—beautiful and historic but no longer secret, no longer ours alone.

"What if I mess this up?" I ask quietly. "What if I try to be brave and fail?"

"Then you try again," Evan says. "That's what courage is—not being unafraid, but choosing to act despite the fear. You're going to be scared. You're going to want to retreat to safety. But if you keep choosing truth anyway, we'll figure it out."

"Together?"

"Together." He stands and pulls me to my feet. "The renovation crew will be here in a few hours. We should probably leave before they arrive and wonder why two students are emerging from a library that was supposed to be locked."

I look around the space one last time. The reading room where we first talked. The shelves where I discovered my favorite hidden corners. The chairs that became ours. The window that let us in.

"I'm going to miss this place," I say.

"Me too. But maybe it served its purpose. Maybe we needed this sanctuary to find each other, to find ourselves. But we can't stay in it forever."

"Time to live in daylight," I say.

"Time to live in daylight," Evan echoes.

We walk to the window together. Evan climbs through first, then reaches back to help me. For the last time, I squeeze through the opening that's been my escape for months.

Outside, the January morning is cold and bright. The sun hasn't fully risen yet, but the sky is lightening rapidly. Campus is still quiet—it's barely five-thirty in the morning, and most students won't be up for another two hours.

But we're not hiding anymore.

We stand together on the library grounds, holding hands in the growing light, and it feels like a beginning.

"What now?" I ask.

"Now," Evan says, "we walk across campus together. We go to our dorms, get a few hours of sleep, and when we wake up, we start figuring out what this looks like in the real world."

"That simple?"

"That terrifying," he corrects with a slight smile.

We walk across the quad side by side as the sun rises. A few early morning runners pass us, and I don't drop Evan's hand. A faculty member getting coffee waves from the academic building, and I wave back without letting go.

It's small. It's nothing dramatic. But it's the first time I've publicly acknowledged our connection, and my heart is hammering in my chest.

"You okay?" Evan asks quietly.

"Terrified," I admit. "But yes. I'm okay."

"That's enough," he says. "For now, that's enough."

We reach the point where we have to split—his dorm in one direction, mine in another. The sun is fully up now, painting everything in cold winter light.

"I'll see you later?" Evan says.

"At lunch," I say, the promise both terrifying and exhilarating. "I'll find you."

"In the dining hall? In front of everyone?"

"In front of everyone."

Evan squeezes my hand once more, then lets go. "Then I'll see you at lunch, Harper Mills."

"See you then, Evan Cross."

I watch him walk away, and for the first time, it doesn't feel like losing him.

It feels like choosing him.

Choosing us.

Choosing truth.

I head to my dorm as the campus wakes around me, and I'm not hiding anymore.

The library is sealed. Our sanctuary is gone.

But maybe we don't need it anymore.

Maybe we're finally brave enough to be real in daylight.
