# Chapter 24: Last Night

The renovation starts tomorrow.

This is the last night the library will be accessible through the broken window. Tomorrow, contractors will arrive and begin securing every entrance, making this building impenetrable.

I need to see Evan.

I don't know if he'll come. He ended things six days ago, and I haven't seen him since. But if there's any chance he'll come one last time, I have to be here. I have to tell him everything I've realized.

I have to fight for us. For the version of me that exists when I'm with him.

It's midnight when I climb through the window. Nine hours from now, this entrance will be sealed forever. I move through the familiar shadows to our spot—my spot? our spot?—and settle into my chair.

And I wait.

The emergency lighting casts the same pools of shadow. The leather chairs smell like furniture polish and old books. The silence is absolute. Everything is the same as it's been for months.

Except Evan isn't here.

I wait. Midnight becomes one o'clock. I try to read, but the words won't stick. Mostly I just sit and listen for the sound of the window opening, for footsteps, for any sign.

He doesn't come.

One o'clock becomes two. The library feels different tonight—not like a sanctuary but like a memorial. A space transforming from present to past.

I'm starting to accept that he's not coming. He ended things cleanly. Protected himself from inevitable hurt. He's probably glad to be done with this painful waiting.

He's probably right to stay away.

But I wait anyway. Because this is the last night, and if there's any chance—

The window opens.

I hear it before I see him—the familiar scrape of wood on wood, the quiet grunt of effort, the soft thud of feet landing on the library floor.

My heart hammers in my chest.

Footsteps approach. Slow, hesitant, like he's not sure he should be here.

Then Evan appears, emerging from the shadows into the pool of emergency light where I'm sitting. He stops when he sees me, surprise flickering across his face.

"I didn't think you'd be here," he says quietly.

"I didn't think you'd come."

We stare at each other across the small distance that feels enormous. He looks tired—the same exhaustion I've been carrying. Shadows under his eyes. Tension in his shoulders. The expression of someone who hasn't been sleeping well.

"Last night before the renovation," Evan says, like he needs to explain his presence. "Thought I should see it one more time."

"Me too."

More silence. He doesn't sit down. Doesn't move closer. Just stands there holding his backpack like he might bolt at any moment.

I stand up. My legs feel shaky, but I need to do this. I need to say what I came here to say before I lose my nerve.

"You were wrong," I tell him.

Evan blinks. "About what?"

"About what I'd choose. About me." I take a breath, trying to organize thoughts I've been processing for days. "You said I was slumming it with the outcast, that I'd eventually go back to my perfect life and realize you were just a rebellion. But you had it backward."

"Harper—"

"No, let me finish. Please." My voice is shaking slightly, but I push forward. "This—" I gesture at the library around us. "—this wasn't the rebellion. This wasn't me escaping my real life. This was my real life. The only time I've been actually real instead of just performing."

Evan is very still, watching me with an expression I can't read.

"I've spent my entire life being what everyone expects," I continue. "Perfect grades for my mother. Perfect debate record for the team. Perfect Honor Council member. I've been performing excellence for so long I forgot it was a performance."

"And now?" Evan asks quietly.

"Now I know the truth. The girl everyone praises—she's not real. She's carefully constructed for approval. But the girl who sat here with you at midnight and talked about her fears and insecurities and the suffocating weight of being perfect—that was me. The actual me."

I step closer to him, and he doesn't move away.

"I've been hiding," I say. "But not you. I've been hiding myself. Keeping the real version locked away in secret while I perform the acceptable version for everyone else. And I'm done."

"Done with what?"

"Done hiding. Done performing. Done choosing approval over authenticity." My eyes are burning with tears I'm trying not to shed. "You said I wouldn't choose you when it mattered. And you were right to be afraid of that, because that's exactly what I was doing. Every time I didn't acknowledge you in the hallway, every time I prioritized my reputation—I was choosing safety over truth."

"So what's changed?" Evan's voice is careful, guarded. Like he's afraid to hope.

"I have. I finally understand that the question isn't whether you're worth the risk. The question is whether I'm worth fighting for. Whether the real version of me deserves to exist in daylight instead of just in secret."

I take another step closer. We're just a few feet apart now.

"I don't have all the answers," I admit. "I don't know how to merge my two lives or what it'll look like when I stop performing. I don't know how to tell my mother I'm not sure I want to be a lawyer, or how to face Madison when I choose differently than she expects. But I know I'm done lying about who I am. I'm done pretending the girl everyone approves of is the real me."

"And you're telling me this because...?" Evan asks, and I can hear the vulnerability underneath the guarded tone.

"Because I want to try. With you. In daylight." My voice breaks slightly. "I don't know how yet. But I want to figure it out. I want to stop hiding us, hiding me. I want to be brave enough to live authentically even when it's scary."

Evan is quiet for a long moment. When he speaks, his voice is soft. "You'd really risk your perfect reputation for the weird transfer kid?"

Despite everything—the fear, the uncertainty, the weight of what I'm choosing—I smile. "You're not weird. And my reputation isn't perfect. It's just performed."

"Harper." My name sounds like a prayer and a question. "What you're talking about—it's going to be hard. People will judge you. Madison might not understand. Your mother might be disappointed. You could lose your Honor Council position, mess up your college applications. Everything you've worked for could fall apart."

"I know," I say. "And I'm terrified. But I'm more terrified of spending the rest of my life performing for approval while the real me disappears entirely. I'm more terrified of losing myself than losing everyone's good opinion."

"What about losing me?" Evan asks quietly. "You've already lost me once. What if you try and it doesn't work? What if being public tears us apart instead of bringing us together?"

"Then at least I'll have tried," I say. "At least I'll have chosen authenticity over safety. At least I'll know I was brave enough to fight for something real instead of just maintaining something perfect."

I close the remaining distance between us until we're standing inches apart.

"You asked me to prove I'd choose you publicly," I say. "To prove I was willing to risk everything. And I couldn't do it then. But I'm ready now. Tomorrow, when we walk out of here for the last time, I want to walk out together. I want to face whatever comes next together. Not in secret. Not in darkness. In daylight, where everyone can see."

Evan's eyes search mine, looking for cracks in my resolve, for signs I'll change my mind when things get difficult. "You're sure?"

"I'm terrified," I correct. "But I'm sure. I'm sure that what we have is real. I'm sure that the person I am with you is who I want to be all the time. I'm sure that I'm done choosing comfort over truth."

"It won't be easy," Evan warns.

"I don't need easy. I need real."

Evan raises his hand slowly, giving me time to pull back, and cups my face gently. "What if I've already decided it's safer to end this? What if I protected my heart by letting you go?"

"Then I'll fight to win you back," I say. "Because you're worth fighting for. And maybe more importantly—I'm worth fighting for."

Something shifts in Evan's expression—the careful guard dropping, hope breaking through the protection. "I don't want to end this," he admits. "I've been miserable for six days trying to convince myself I did the right thing. But I was so afraid you'd choose your perfect future over me—over us."

"My perfect future was killing me," I say. "Slowly suffocating the actual person I am underneath all the achievements. You showed me there was another option. You helped me find the real version of me. And I'm not giving that up. I'm not giving you up."

"What happens tomorrow?" Evan asks. "When the renovation starts and we can't hide here anymore?"

"Tomorrow we start living in daylight," I say. "We figure out what this looks like when it's not a secret. We make mistakes and face consequences and learn how to be authentic instead of perfect."

"Together?"

"Together."

Evan's thumb brushes my cheekbone, and I lean into the touch. "I've missed you," he whispers.

"I've missed you too. I've missed myself when I'm with you."

We stand in the emergency lighting, in our sanctuary that exists for nine more hours, and for the first time in days I don't feel hollow. I feel scared and uncertain and hopeful and real.

"Stay," I say. "Stay until dawn. One last night in our library."

Evan nods, and we sit down in our chairs. Not touching, but close. Connected.

We talk until the sky starts to lighten outside the windows. We talk about what comes next, about the fears we're both carrying, about the possibility that this could fail spectacularly. But we also talk about hope—that maybe choosing authenticity over approval is its own kind of courage.

That maybe we're both brave enough to try.

As dawn breaks, we prepare to leave the library for the last time through the window. Tomorrow—today, technically—contractors will seal this entrance forever.

But we'll leave together. We'll walk across campus side by side as the sun rises, and we won't hide.

It's terrifying.

It's the bravest thing I've ever done.

"Ready?" Evan asks at the window.

I take his hand. "Ready."

We climb out together into the January morning, and I don't let go.
