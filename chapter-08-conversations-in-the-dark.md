# Chapter 8: Conversations in the Dark

The next night, I arrive at midnight and settle into my corner with my biology textbook. Cell respiration. Mitochondria. The Krebs cycle. Information that should be straightforward but keeps sliding out of my brain because I can't focus.

At 1:10 AM, I hear the window open. Hear Evan's careful footsteps crossing the periodicals section, ascending the stairs.

When he reaches the second floor—my floor—he pauses.

I look up from my failed attempt to memorize the steps of glycolysis.

Evan nods once. A simple acknowledgment. Hey, you're here.

I nod back. Hey.

Then he continues up to the third floor, and I go back to my biology notes.

But something has shifted. That small nod—that tiny acknowledgment of each other's presence—feels significant. We're not pretending anymore. Not completely. We're... coexisting. With awareness.

The next night, the same thing happens. Evan arrives at 1:08 AM. Nods when he reaches the second floor. I nod back. He goes up to the third floor.

It becomes part of the routine. A small ritual that punctuates our otherwise separate existences.

On the third night after our conversation, everything changes again.

I'm in my wingback chair, reading the Plath collection Evan helped me retrieve. The poems are dense and difficult, full of imagery that feels both precise and deliberately obscure. I'm rereading "Lady Lazarus" for the third time, trying to understand the layers of meaning, when I hear footsteps on the stairs.

Descending, not ascending.

Evan appears on the second floor, hesitating at the top of the staircase like he's not sure if he's crossing a line.

"The lighting's better down here," he says. "Do you mind if I read nearby?"

I should probably say yes, I mind. Maintain our territorial boundaries. Keep the second floor as my space and the third as his.

But I don't want to say yes.

"That's fine," I hear myself say.

Evan crosses to one of the other leather chairs about ten feet from mine—close enough to share the better emergency lighting from this section, far enough to maintain personal space. He settles in with his book and a notebook, getting comfortable.

We read in silence for what must be close to an hour. It's not awkward, which surprises me. I expected to feel self-conscious, observed. But Evan is absorbed in whatever he's reading, occasionally making notes, and I find myself able to focus on Plath's complicated metaphors in a way I couldn't when I was alone.

Maybe because I'm not alone. Maybe having another person present—someone who's also choosing to spend Friday night reading poetry instead of doing literally anything else—makes the choice feel less strange.

The chapel clock chimes two AM.

"What are you working on?" Evan asks.

I look up, surprised. He's set his book aside and is watching me with genuine curiosity.

"Just... reading," I say. "Poetry. For myself, not for class."

"Plath?"

I nod, showing him the cover.

"She's intense," Evan observes.

"That's an understatement."

"What's drawing you to intense poetry at two in the morning?"

It's a real question, not small talk. I can tell by the way he's looking at me—actually interested in the answer, not just filling silence.

I should probably deflect. Keep things surface-level. We've established a truce, not a friendship.

But something about the darkness, the late hour, the fact that we're both here breaking the same rules makes honesty feel easier than evasion.

"I'm supposed to be working on my college essay," I admit. "But I keep staring at the prompt and freezing up. I needed to read something that had nothing to do with presenting myself as an ideal college applicant."

Evan's expression shifts to something like understanding. "What's the prompt?"

"'Describe a challenge you've overcome and what it taught you.'" I can't keep the frustration out of my voice. "It's so artificial. They want you to share something personal, but not too personal. Show vulnerability, but not actual weakness. Prove you can overcome obstacles, but only the right kind of obstacles."

"The kind that make you look resilient instead of damaged," Evan says.

"Exactly."

We sit with that for a moment. Then Evan asks, "What would you write about if you were being actually honest?"

I laugh, but it comes out bitter. "I'd write about lying awake at 2 AM because I can't turn my brain off. About breaking into the library because following all the rules and maintaining a perfect record is slowly crushing me. About feeling like I'm performing my entire life for an audience of college admissions officers I've never met."

"You should write that."

"Right. And get rejected from every school I apply to."

"Or get accepted because you'd be the only applicant telling the truth."

I look at him more closely. "Is that what you did? Tell the truth?"

Evan shakes his head. "I transferred junior year. My college essays are going to be an exercise in explaining why that happened without actually explaining why that happened."

"What would you write if you were being honest?"

He considers this. "I'd write about learning that reputation matters more than truth. That people would rather believe a convenient story than a complicated reality. That starting over somewhere new doesn't actually solve anything if you bring all your fear and mistrust with you."

The vulnerability in his voice catches me off guard. This is more than he's shared with anyone at Thornbridge, I'm certain of it. More than he planned to share with me.

"Is that why you can't sleep?" I ask quietly. "Too loud in your head?"

Evan meets my eyes. "Yeah. Is that why you're here?"

"Yeah."

We've just acknowledged something huge without actually saying it directly. We're both here because the daytime world is too much. Too loud, too demanding, too full of performance and pretense. The library at midnight is the only place where we can stop pretending.

"Madison thinks I'm doing better," I say, not sure why I'm sharing this. "She said I seem more relaxed lately. She doesn't know it's because I'm breaking curfew and risking my Honor Council position to sit in an empty library and read poetry."

"Sometimes survival looks like rule-breaking," Evan says.

"Is that what this is? Survival?"

"What else would you call it?"

I think about that. About the six weeks I spent lying awake, my anxiety building until I thought I might shatter. About the desperation that drove me to climb through that window the first time. About how the library has become necessary instead of just nice to have.

"Yeah," I admit. "Survival."

"Then don't feel guilty about it," Evan says. "You're doing what you need to do to function. That's not weakness. It's adaptive."

I've never thought about it that way. In my head, this has always been a failure—evidence that I can't handle the pressure of Thornbridge like everyone else seems to. That I'm not strong enough, disciplined enough, good enough.

But Evan is reframing it as strength. As an adaptive response to an unsustainable situation.

"Do you ever feel like you're living two separate lives?" I ask. "Like there's the daytime version of you that everyone sees, and then there's the real you that only exists in spaces like this?"

"Every day," Evan says quietly.

"Which one is real?"

"Maybe they both are. Maybe we're just... more ourselves in some contexts than others." He pauses. "Or maybe the daytime version is what we have to be to survive, and the nighttime version is what we actually are."

I think about the Harper Mills everyone sees at Thornbridge. The accomplished student. The Honor Council representative. The girl with the perfect GPA and the clear pre-law track. She's real—I'm not faking the work or the achievement.

But she's not complete. She's what I can manage to be when I'm performing at maximum capacity.

This version of me—sitting in a library at 2 AM, admitting to insomnia and anxiety and the constant pressure of pretending to have everything together—feels more honest. More whole.

"I've never told anyone about the insomnia," I admit. "Not Madison, not Caroline, not my parents. Just you."

"I won't tell anyone," Evan says. "For what it's worth."

"I know."

And I do know. We have mutually assured destruction, sure. But it's more than that. There's something about the way Evan listens—really listens, without judgment or advice—that makes me trust him in a way I don't trust people I've known much longer.

"What time do you usually leave?" I ask.

"Three, three-thirty. You?"

"Around the same."

"We could walk back together," Evan suggests, then immediately looks like he regrets it. "Or not. If that's too risky. If someone saw us—"

"We could take different routes," I interrupt. "But walk at the same time. So we're both less likely to run into security alone."

It's a practical suggestion. Nothing more. Definitely not me wanting to extend this conversation, this connection, even just a few minutes longer.

"That works," Evan says.

We fall into comfortable silence again. Evan returns to his book—*The Stranger* by Camus, I notice when I glance over. Of course he's reading existentialist philosophy at 2 AM. It fits.

I go back to Plath, but I'm not really reading anymore. I'm thinking about what just happened. About how easy it was to talk to Evan. How he asked real questions and listened to real answers. How neither of us performed or pretended or tried to impress the other.

How this might be the most honest conversation I've had since arriving at Thornbridge.

At 3:15 AM, we both start packing up without needing to coordinate. Some internal clock we've both developed that says this is when it's time to leave.

We descend the stairs together, slip through the window one at a time, and then walk across the quad in the same direction until our paths diverge—Evan toward his dorm, me toward Hargrove Hall.

"See you tomorrow night?" he says, making it a question.

"Yeah," I say. "See you tomorrow."

Back in my room, Caroline is sleeping soundly. I slip into bed and stare at the familiar ceiling stains, but my mind isn't racing the way it usually does.

I'm thinking about Evan's voice when he said "sometimes survival looks like rule-breaking." About the way he understood the college essay problem without me having to explain it. About how he admitted to being loud in his head too, and somehow that made me feel less broken.

I close my eyes and fall asleep easier than I have in weeks.

Because I'm not alone in this anymore.
