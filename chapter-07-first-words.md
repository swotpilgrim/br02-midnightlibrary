# Chapter 7: First Words

Two weeks into our unspoken truce, I need a book I can't reach.

It's 1 AM on a Friday. I've been in my corner for an hour, trying to write my college essay about "a challenge you've overcome." I've deleted three versions—either too revealing or too generic.

I need a break. I decide to find something completely unrelated to read.

I'm browsing the literature section when I spot it: a slim volume of Sylvia Plath's poems on a high shelf. At least a foot above my head, wedged between larger volumes.

I stretch up on my toes, reaching. My fingers brush the spine but can't get purchase. I stretch harder, rising on the balls of my feet, arm extended as far as it will go.

Not close enough.

I reach again, jumping slightly, and my fingers just barely graze the bottom of the book. So close. One more try—

A hand extends above mine and plucks the book from the shelf easily.

I freeze and turn.

Evan stands directly behind me—close enough that I can see his face in the dim lighting. Several inches taller than me. Close enough that I should have heard him approaching.

He's holding the Plath collection, his expression careful and neutral, like he's not sure how I'll react.

"You looked like you needed this," he says.

His voice is quiet but warmer than I expected. During the day, the few times I've heard him speak in class, his voice has been flat and careful. But now there's a hint of something else in it. Not quite humor, but something adjacent to it.

I stare at him for a beat too long before my brain remembers how words work.

"Thanks," I manage.

He extends the book toward me. I take it, and for a second our fingers brush against the spine. The contact lasts maybe half a second, but I'm hyperaware of it.

Evan takes a step back, creating distance. He's already turning to leave, to disappear back into the stacks and maintain our careful pretense of mutual invisibility.

"Wait," I say, surprising myself.

He stops. Turns back. Waits.

Now that I've said it, I have to follow through. But what am I even doing? We've been successfully avoiding each other for two weeks. We have a system that works. Why am I disrupting it?

Except I know why. Because pretending we don't both know what we know is exhausting. Because the silence between us has started to feel heavier than words might be. Because something about him reaching for that book—helping me without being asked, without making it awkward—makes me think maybe acknowledging this doesn't have to be catastrophic.

"We should probably..." I pause, searching for the right words. "We should probably acknowledge that we're both breaking the same rule here."

Evan's expression shifts slightly. Not quite a smile, but something in his eyes that might be relief. Or maybe recognition.

"Mutually assured destruction?" he offers.

I blink. Then I realize he's making a Cold War reference—the idea that two parties can keep each other in check through the threat of mutual consequences. It's actually perfect.

"Something like that," I say.

We stand there in the awkward silence of two people who've been pretending not to see each other for weeks and are now suddenly forced to acknowledge reality.

"I won't tell if you won't," I add, because apparently I'm committed to stating the obvious now.

"Deal," Evan says.

Another pause. This is the point where we should probably go back to our separate floors and continue the truce. But now that we've started talking, stopping feels wrong. Like leaving a conversation half-finished.

"I'm Harper," I say, even though I'm ninety percent certain he already knows my name. We're in the same calculus class. Same English class. Thornbridge is small enough that most juniors at least know each other's names even if they don't actually know each other.

"I know," Evan says. Then, after a beat: "I'm Evan."

"I know."

The corner of his mouth twitches. It's not quite a smile, but it's close. The almost-smile transforms his face, makes him look less like the isolated figure from the dining hall and more like someone my age. Someone who might be capable of humor and lightness and things that don't fit with the careful armor he wears during daylight hours.

"So," I say, because the silence is stretching again and I don't know what else to do with it.

"So," Evan echoes.

"How long have you been..." I gesture vaguely at the library around us, at the illegality of our presence here.

"Since the second week of September," he says. "I found the window by accident. Was walking around campus one night because I couldn't sleep, and I saw it was cracked open."

The second week of September. Before I found it. This has been his space longer than mine.

"I'm sorry," I say. "If I'm—I didn't mean to invade your space."

Evan shakes his head. "It's not my space. It's not anyone's space, technically. We're both trespassing."

"Equal opportunity rule-breaking," I say.

"Exactly."

I think I see the almost-smile again. It's strange, talking to him like this. Strange how different he seems from the silent, isolated figure I've been observing during the day. His voice is thoughtful. His responses are quick enough that I can tell he's actually engaged in this conversation, not just waiting for it to end.

"I won't mess with your schedule," I offer. "I can come earlier, or later, or different nights—"

"It's fine," Evan interrupts. "The library's big enough for both of us."

"The third floor and second floor system seems to be working," I venture.

"Yeah." He pauses. "Though I have to admit, I was on the second floor first. You kind of claimed it while I was gone for a few days."

I feel my face heat with embarrassment. "I didn't know—"

"It's fine," he says again. "I like the third floor better anyway. Better view."

I don't know if he's just being polite or if he means it. The third floor does have a better view—the windows up there overlook more of campus, not just the quad.

"Well," I say, feeling like this conversation has reached its natural endpoint but not quite ready to end it. "I guess we have an agreement. You take the third floor, I take the second, and we both pretend we have no idea that Honor Council has a curfew violation happening right under its nose."

The almost-smile becomes an actual smile. It's small and quick, gone almost as soon as it appears, but it's real.

"Right under your nose, specifically," Evan points out. "Since you're on Honor Council."

"Don't remind me," I mutter. "The irony is not lost on me."

"Must be strange," Evan says. "Enforcing rules you're actively breaking."

There's no judgment in his voice. Just observation. But it hits something true and uncomfortable in my chest.

"It's..." I pause, trying to articulate something I've been avoiding thinking about. "It's less strange than you'd think. The rules make sense during the day. The curfew exists for good reasons. I genuinely believe in academic integrity and community standards. But at night..."

"The rules don't account for insomnia," Evan finishes.

"Exactly."

We look at each other for a moment, and I have the strangest sense that we've just shared something significant. Not just the fact that we're both rule-breakers, but the why behind it. The desperate need for space and silence that outweighs the risks.

"See you around, I guess," Evan says, breaking the moment. "Or not. Depending on how good we are at avoiding each other."

There's a hint of humor in his voice. Like he's acknowledging the absurdity of our situation—the fact that we just had an entire conversation agreeing to continue pretending we don't exist to each other.

"Third floor and second floor," I confirm.

"Mutually assured destruction."

"Right."

Evan nods once, then turns and walks toward the staircase. I watch him go, the Plath collection still in my hands.

Before he reaches the stairs, I call out: "Evan?"

He stops and looks back.

"Thanks. For the book."

"No problem."

Then he's gone, his footsteps ascending to the third floor. I hear him settle into whatever space he's claimed up there—furniture creaking, the soft sound of a book opening.

I return to my wingback chair, but I don't open the Plath collection right away. I'm too busy processing what just happened.

I talked to Evan Cross. And he was... normal. Quick and thoughtful and almost funny in a dry way. His voice was warm. His eyes were intelligent and present.

Nothing about that interaction matched the silent figure I see during the day.

It's like there are two Evan Crosses. The daytime version, locked behind walls of isolation. And the nighttime version, who retrieves books and makes Cold War references and almost-smiles.

I wonder which one is real. Or if they're both real, just different facets adapting to different environments.

I wonder if he thinks the same about me. If the Harper he just talked to seems different from the Honor Council representative who projects careful competence.

Maybe we're both performing during the day and only real at night.

The chapel clock chimes 1:30 AM. Above me, I hear the soft sounds of Evan moving around. Turning pages. Shifting in his chair. Evidence that I'm not alone in this building, but also not intruding on his solitude.

We've found a strange balance. Acknowledging each other while maintaining distance. Sharing space while respecting boundaries.

I finally open the Plath collection. The pages are yellowed and well-worn, like many people have read these poems before me. I page through until I find "The Moon and the Yew Tree."

*"This is the light of the mind, cold and planetary. The trees of the mind are black. The light is blue."*

I read the whole poem twice, thinking about light and darkness and sanctuary and the way people become different versions of themselves depending on where they are and who's watching.

Thinking about Evan Cross on the floor above me, and how strange it is that we've gone from strangers to something like allies in the span of a five-minute conversation about a book I couldn't reach.

When I finally leave at 2:30 AM, slipping through the window and across the dark quad, I realize I'm smiling.

Not because anything particularly amazing happened. Just because for the first time since arriving at Thornbridge, I had a conversation with someone that felt completely honest.

And that matters more than I knew it could.
