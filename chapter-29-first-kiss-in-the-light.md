# Chapter 29: First Kiss in the Light

As the celebration winds down and people filter toward the exits, Evan and I find ourselves gravitating back to the periodicals section. The space that was ours—that is ours, even if it's been transformed.

The new lighting is so bright compared to the emergency lights we used to navigate by. Everything is visible now, no shadows to hide in, no dark corners for secrets. The windows gleam with their new latches, properly secured, impossible to open from outside.

It's strange being here like this.

"Legal library access during operating hours," Evan says, looking around with a slight smile. "This is weird."

I laugh. "So weird. No sneaking in through windows. No worrying about security patrols. Just...normal use of a public space."

"We're terrible at normal."

"The worst," I agree.

The lightness between us feels good after the intensity of the past hour. Making our relationship public, facing Madison's confusion and Victoria's careful approval, watching Evan begin to open up to other students—it's been emotionally exhausting and exhilarating in equal measure.

But here, in our section, some of the tension eases.

"Thank you for this," Evan says, his voice turning serious. "For choosing this. For choosing us. I know it cost you."

I shake my head. "I didn't choose you, Evan. I chose us. There's a difference."

"Is there?"

"Yes. Because choosing you would make it sound like I did it for you, like I sacrificed something for your benefit. But that's not what happened." I step closer to him. "I chose us because being with you—being real with you—that's not a sacrifice. That's the first time I've felt like myself in years."

Evan's eyes search mine. "Still. You risked a lot. Your reputation, your Honor Council position, your friends' approval—"

"All things that were based on a performance," I interrupt gently. "I'm not saying it doesn't scare me. It does. But I'd rather be scared and authentic than safe and empty."

"You're extraordinary," Evan says softly.

"I'm terrified."

"You can be both."

We stand in the bright light of the renovated library, and I'm suddenly acutely aware that this is a full-circle moment. This is where we first really talked, where we began to know each other. Where months of midnight conversations turned into something neither of us was brave enough to name.

"We have unfinished business," I say, stepping even closer.

Evan's eyebrows raise slightly. "Do we?"

"There was an almost-kiss. In this exact spot, actually." I gesture around us. "Emergency lighting, late at night, that moment when we both knew what was about to happen."

"And then security showed up and we ran in opposite directions," Evan remembers, a smile tugging at his lips.

"Terrible timing."

"The worst."

"But we're not hiding anymore," I say, looking up at him. "And there's no one to interrupt us now."

"No?" Evan glances around the mostly-empty library. A few people are still lingering near the entrance, and through the tall windows I can see students crossing the quad outside. We're not alone, not really. But we're not hiding.

"No security patrols coming," I confirm. "No need to run. No reason to pretend this isn't exactly what it looks like."

Evan reaches up and cups my face with both hands, the gesture gentle and deliberate. His eyes hold mine with an intensity that makes my breath catch.

"Harper Mills," he says, "you're the bravest person I know."

"Then you should kiss me," I whisper, the words I almost said weeks ago finally finding their voice.

And he does.

His lips meet mine soft and sweet, nothing rushed or desperate. Just tender and certain and right. My hands come up to rest on his chest, feeling his heartbeat steady and strong beneath my palms. The library around us—bright and renovated and no longer secret—fades away until there's only this: Evan's hands on my face, his lips on mine, the feeling of being completely and authentically present in a moment.

It's nothing like the quick kiss we shared this morning in the quad, hurried and adrenaline-fueled. This is different. This is taking our time, neither of us afraid of being caught or judged or interrupted.

This is us, together, in daylight.

When we finally break apart, we're both smiling. And I'm acutely aware that we're visible—through the tall windows, students outside can see us. The few remaining people in the library can see us. Anyone passing by can witness exactly what's happening.

Harper Mills kissing Evan Cross in the bright, public space of the newly renovated library.

I don't care.

In fact, I think I want people to see. Want them to know that this is real, that I'm choosing this, that I'm not ashamed or hiding or pretending this is anything other than what it is: me and the person who sees me most clearly.

"People are watching," Evan says quietly, though he doesn't let go of me.

"I know."

"And you're okay with that?"

"More than okay." I lean my forehead against his. "I'm done hiding. Done pretending. This is who I am—someone who fell for the guy everyone else dismissed. Someone who'd rather be real than perfect."

"I fell for you too," Evan says. "The real you. The one who lies awake at three in the morning wondering if she's living her own life or someone else's. The one who's scared and brave and beautifully imperfect."

"That's a lot of falling," I say, trying to lighten the intensity of the moment even though my heart is full.

"Yeah. It is." Evan brushes a strand of hair from my face. "Terrifying and wonderful in equal measure."

"Like most things worth having."

We stand together in the periodicals section, in the space that used to be our secret sanctuary and is now just another part of the library. But the memories remain—all those nights of honest conversations, of learning each other's fears and dreams, of slowly falling for someone we weren't supposed to want.

The library gave us that. This space, with its broken window latch and emergency lighting and absolute silence, gave us somewhere to be real when nowhere else felt safe.

But we can't live in midnight forever. We have to bring what we found here into daylight and let it exist in the real world, with all its complications and judgments and consequences.

And that's what we're doing. Right now. Visibly. Together.

"Do you think it'll last?" I ask quietly. "Once we're not in our special space anymore, once this becomes just our regular lives—will we still have what we found here?"

"I don't know," Evan admits honestly. "We might struggle. We might find out that being together in public is harder than being together in secret. We might face judgment and pressure that tests whether we're strong enough to withstand it."

"That's not very reassuring."

"But," he continues, "I think we have something real. Not a fantasy we created in the dark, but actual connection based on actually knowing each other. And if we can hold onto that—if we can keep being honest even when it's difficult—then yeah. I think it'll last."

"So we just have to keep being brave."

"Just that." He smiles slightly. "Simple."

"Terrifying."

"That too."

I look around the periodicals section one more time. The shelves are the same ones we used to sit between. The window—now properly latched—is the one we climbed through dozens of times. The reading chairs are positioned exactly where we used to talk for hours.

But everything is different now. Bright instead of shadowed. Secure instead of accessible. Public instead of secret.

We're different too. Braver. More honest. More ourselves.

"Ready to leave?" Evan asks.

"Our section?"

"Our section. Our sanctuary. Our past." He takes my hand. "Time to focus on our future."

"Which involves dinner in the dining hall where everyone can see us."

"Absolutely terrifying dinner," Evan confirms.

"Good thing we're getting good at being terrified together."

We walk toward the library exit hand in hand, passing the few remaining celebration attendees. A couple of freshman girls smile at us. An older faculty member nods approvingly. I catch a glimpse of someone taking a photo—probably for social media, probably to speculate about Harper Mills' surprising choice of boyfriend.

Let them speculate. Let them take their photos. Let them talk.

This is my life now. My choice. My truth.

As we step through the library's front entrance into the winter evening, I look back one more time at the building that changed everything. The place where I learned to be honest, to be imperfect, to be real.

The place where I fell in love.

"Thank you," I say quietly.

"For what?" Evan asks.

"For seeing me. For being patient while I figured out how to be brave. For not giving up on me even when I was too scared to choose us publicly."

"You chose us in the end," Evan points out. "That's what matters."

"I chose us," I agree. "And I'll keep choosing us. Every day. Even when it's scary."

"Especially when it's scary."

"Especially then."

We walk toward the dining hall together, hand in hand, visible to anyone who cares to look. The sun has set fully now, and the campus is lit by streetlamps and building lights. Students cluster in groups, heading to dinner or back to their dorms, living their normal Friday evening lives.

And we're part of that normalcy now. Not secret midnight visitors sneaking through broken windows. Just two students who found each other and decided to be brave enough to stay together in daylight.

I can already see a few people pointing at us as we approach the dining hall. Can imagine the whispers that will follow us when we walk in together. Can picture Madison's continued confusion and Caroline's concerned watching and all the other reactions from people who thought they knew exactly who Harper Mills was.

But I'm not that person anymore. Or maybe I never was, and I'm finally letting people see who I've been all along.

Either way, I'm done performing.

I'm choosing real.

And real is scary and uncertain and wonderful.

Just like Evan said: terrifying and extraordinary in equal measure.

We reach the dining hall doors, and I take a deep breath.

"Ready?" Evan asks.

"Not even a little bit," I admit. "But let's do it anyway."

He squeezes my hand, we push through the doors together, and our new life—our real life—begins.
