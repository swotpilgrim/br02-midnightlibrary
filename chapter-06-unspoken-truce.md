# Chapter 6: Unspoken Truce

I return to the library the next night with my nerves strung tight.

Part of me expects Evan to be there already, waiting, ready to confront me about invading his space. Or maybe he'll report me—report both of us—and this whole fragile secret will explode into disciplinary hearings and consequences. Part of me wonders if he'll tell people during the day, if Madison will pull me aside with wide eyes saying she heard I've been sneaking into the library, if Victoria will summon me to an Honor Council meeting to explain my hypocrisy.

But when I climb through the window at midnight, the library is empty.

I stand in the periodicals section, listening. No footsteps. No breathing. No presence except my own. The emergency lighting casts its familiar greenish glow. The building settles with its usual creaks and groans. Outside the windows, campus sleeps.

I'm alone.

I should feel relieved. Instead, I feel strangely on edge as I climb to the second floor and settle into my wingback chair. Every sound makes me hyperalert. The chapel clock chiming twelve-thirty. The radiator ticking. The wind pushing against the old windows. My book sits unopened on my lap while I listen for the sound of the window opening, for footsteps on the stairs, for any indication that Evan is coming.

He doesn't.

I stay until 2:30 AM, later than usual, as if making up for the time I lost last night when I fled early. But I never fully relax. The library doesn't feel like sanctuary tonight. It feels like contested territory, and I'm waiting to see who has the stronger claim.

The next night, I arrive at 11:45 PM, earlier than usual. If there's going to be some kind of confrontation or negotiation, I want to be here first. Want the psychological advantage of already being settled when he arrives.

Except he doesn't arrive.

I wait in my corner until 2 AM, watching the stairs, alert for any sound. Nothing. Maybe Evan has decided the risk isn't worth it anymore. Maybe knowing someone else has discovered his secret has scared him away. Maybe he's claimed different nights, establishing a schedule that avoids overlap.

The thought makes me unreasonably disappointed.

On the third night, I arrive at my usual time—12:15 AM—and settle into my routine. I've brought my calculus homework, the kind of problem set that requires quiet focus. I spread the pages out on the small table near my chair and work through derivatives in the pool of light from my phone.

At 1:04 AM, I hear the window open.

My hand freezes mid-equation. I listen to the soft sounds of someone climbing through, the near-silent footsteps crossing the periodicals section. I know it's Evan before I hear him reach the stairs. Something about the rhythm of movement, the careful quiet that speaks of practice.

The footsteps ascend the stairs. Pass the second floor. Continue to the third.

He's claiming the top floor. Giving me the second. Creating physical distance between us.

I should feel grateful. This is the best possible outcome—we both get to keep using the library, we just stay out of each other's way. No confrontation necessary. No negotiation required. Just an unspoken agreement to pretend the other doesn't exist.

But I can't focus on my calculus anymore. I'm too aware of his presence above me. The occasional creak of floorboards marking his movement. The knowledge that we're both here, both awake, both hiding in the same building for probably the same reasons.

I pack up my homework at 2 AM and descend the stairs carefully, listening. The third floor is silent. I slip out the window and walk back to Hargrove Hall wondering if this is how it's going to be from now on. Ships passing in the night. Coexisting without acknowledging.

The pattern holds for the next two nights. Evan arrives between 1 and 1:15 AM. I'm already there, established on the second floor. He goes to the third. We don't see each other. Don't speak. Maintain the fiction that we're each alone in the building.

During the day, nothing changes. Evan still sits alone at lunch. Still claims his isolated corner table during study period. Still exists on the periphery of Thornbridge's social world. I still sit with Madison and the pre-law group. Still participate in class. Still perform my role as junior class Honor Council representative.

We pass each other in the hallway between calculus and English. Our eyes don't meet. We're strangers who happen to share a secret, nothing more.

On the fourth night, everything shifts.

I arrive at 11:50 PM, unusually early because Caroline had gone to bed right after dinner, claiming a headache. I've been in my corner for over an hour when I realize I need a different book. The one I've been reading—a collection of Mary Oliver poetry I found in the literature section—is finished. I want something new. Something absorbing enough to quiet my still-racing thoughts.

I check my phone: 1:07 AM. Evan is probably here already, settled on the third floor.

I can get a book from the first-floor stacks without going anywhere near him. Easy.

I descend the stairs from the second floor, moving quietly out of habit. The main reading room is shadowy and vast. I head toward the fiction section along the west wall, running my finger along spines, looking for something familiar. Maybe Austen. Something I've read before so I don't have to concentrate too hard.

I'm reaching for *Emma* when I hear footsteps on the stairs. Descending from the third floor.

Evan.

My hand freezes on the book spine. I have maybe three seconds before he reaches the bottom of the stairs and sees me standing here. I should move. Hide. Do something.

But I don't.

Evan rounds the corner from the staircase and stops.

We're maybe ten feet apart. Both frozen. Both clearly visible in the ambient light from the windows.

For a long moment, neither of us moves. Neither speaks. We just stare at each other across the reading room like we've been caught doing something forbidden—which, technically, we have. Just not by each other.

This close, in better light than that night when he passed my hiding spot, I can actually see him. His dark hair is messier than it is during the day, like he's been running his hands through it. He's wearing jeans and a Thornbridge hoodie—the same casual violation of dress code I'm wearing. His eyes are wide with surprise, maybe alarm.

He looks younger than he does during the day. Less guarded. More like a regular sixteen-year-old kid and less like someone carrying the weight of exile.

I should say something. Acknowledge this. But what? "Sorry for hiding in the periodicals section and watching you that first night?" "Nice sanctuary you have here?" "Want to work out a custody schedule for the library?"

Before I can figure out what words to form, Evan moves. He steps aside—away from the stairs, clearing the path. Creating space for me to pass.

It's a deliberate gesture. Careful. Respectful. Like he's acknowledging my presence without forcing interaction.

I nod—a small, jerky movement—and walk toward the stairs. As I pass him, I'm acutely aware of the few feet between us. Close enough that I can see the title of the book in his hand—*The Great Gatsby*, same as what we're reading in English. Close enough to notice he's holding a highlighter, like maybe he was working on homework too.

Close enough that if I wanted to say something, he would definitely hear me.

I don't say anything. Neither does he.

I climb the stairs back to the second floor, my heart pounding for reasons I don't quite understand. It was just a moment. A few seconds of eye contact and physical proximity. Nothing significant.

But it felt significant. Like we'd crossed some invisible line from "people who don't know each other exist" to "people who are choosing not to talk about knowing."

I settle back into my wingback chair but I can't read. I hear Evan move through the first floor—getting whatever he came down for, probably—then climb the stairs. His footsteps pass the second floor and continue to the third.

Back to our separate territories.

But something has shifted. We've seen each other now. Made eye contact. Acknowledged, however silently, that we're both breaking the same rules for access to the same space.

I stay until 2:30 AM, and the whole time I'm hyper-aware of the presence above me. Not threatening. Not intrusive. Just... there. Another person existing in this supposedly-empty building. Another rule-breaker seeking sanctuary.

The next night—the fifth night since I discovered Evan's secret—I arrive at midnight and settle in with my biology reading. At 1:10 AM, I hear the window. Hear the footsteps ascending to the third floor. Hear the familiar creaks that mark Evan's movement around his claimed territory.

And I realize something.

This is our routine now. Our pattern. We're sharing this space the way the library was probably meant to be shared—not through scheduled time slots or rigid rules, but through mutual respect and unspoken understanding.

We both know each other's secret. We're both keeping it. The library is big enough for both of us if we maintain careful distance.

It's not quite friendship. Not even acquaintance, really. But it's something. An unspoken truce. A silent agreement.

You don't tell, I don't tell. You take the third floor, I take the second. We both get what we need from this space, and we both pretend the other is a ghost.

I can live with that.

During the day, I see Evan in calculus and my eyes slide past him the same way everyone else's do. At lunch, I sit with Madison while he sits alone three tables away. In the hallway, we're strangers.

But at night, we're something else. Two people orbiting each other in the darkness. Two people who've found the same escape hatch from Thornbridge's relentless expectations.

Two people who understand, without ever speaking about it, why someone might risk everything for a few hours of peace.

I don't know Evan Cross. I don't know his story beyond rumors and speculation. I don't know what he thinks about during his solitary lunches or why he never raises his hand even when he knows the answer.

But I know this: he needs the library the same way I do. And knowing that—knowing I'm not the only one desperate enough to break rules and risk consequences for sanctuary—makes me feel less alone than I have since arriving at Thornbridge.

Even if we never say a word to each other.
