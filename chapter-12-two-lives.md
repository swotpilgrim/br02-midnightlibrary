# Chapter 12: Two Lives

Monday lunch feels like being in two places at once.

I'm sitting at my usual table with Madison, Julia, and three other pre-law juniors. Madison is holding forth about early decision strategies—whether it's better to apply early to reach schools or play it safe with targets. Julia thinks early decision shows commitment. Someone else argues it limits financial aid negotiation leverage.

I should be engaged in this conversation. This is exactly the kind of strategic thinking I used to love. Six weeks ago, I would have had opinions, would have been debating the merits of different application timelines.

But I'm barely listening.

Because across the dining hall, three tables away, Evan is sitting alone with a book propped against his water glass. He's eating methodically, fork to mouth to plate, his eyes never leaving the page. He's reading *Mrs. Dalloway*, the Woolf we talked about Saturday night.

I wonder if he's at the part where Clarissa walks through London, or if he's gotten to Septimus yet. I wonder if he'll want to talk about it tonight, if we'll end up debating Woolf's portrayal of mental illness or the novel's meditation on time and memory.

"Harper? Hello?" Madison waves her hand in front of my face.

I snap my attention back to the table. "Sorry. What?"

"I asked if you're planning to apply anywhere early decision. You've been completely spacing out for the past five minutes."

"I don't know yet. Still weighing options."

It's a safe answer that means nothing. The truth is I haven't even started seriously researching colleges beyond the list my mother sent me: Yale, Columbia, UPenn, all their law schools carefully highlighted.

"Well, you should decide soon," Madison says. "Early decision deadlines are in less than a month. And you need a clear first choice."

I nod like I'm taking this seriously. Like my first choice isn't actually sitting in an empty library at midnight talking to a boy no one else bothers to know.

Julia launches into a story about her cousin's roommate at Yale, and I let the conversation wash over me. My eyes drift back to Evan.

He's turned a page. Still completely absorbed in his book, existing in his bubble of careful isolation. During the day, we're strangers. Less than strangers—we're people who actively pretend the other doesn't exist.

Saturday night we sat inches apart sharing hot chocolate, talking about what we actually wanted from our lives. This morning we passed each other in the hallway between calculus and English, and our eyes didn't even meet.

The disconnect is making me feel split in half.

"Are you okay?" Madison asks quietly while the others are debating something about Yale's residential college system. "You've been really distracted lately."

"Just tired," I lie. "Not sleeping well."

It's not entirely a lie. I'm getting maybe four hours of sleep most nights—staying up until 3 AM in the library, getting back to my room by 3:30, lying awake processing conversations with Evan until I finally drift off around 4:30, then dragging myself up for 6:30 classes.

I'm functioning on a deficit that would have destroyed me two months ago. But I'm also happier than I've been all year, which makes no logical sense.

"You should see the school nurse," Madison suggests. "Or talk to Dr. Whitmore about adjusting your schedule. You're taking a really heavy course load."

"I'm fine. Really."

Madison doesn't look convinced, but she lets it drop.

After lunch, I have a free period while most people have afternoon classes. I head to the library—the daytime library, bright and crowded and nothing like the sanctuary I know at midnight.

I claim a spot at one of the long study tables near the windows. A few other students are scattered around—some seniors working on college applications at the computers, a sophomore reading for English class, a junior I vaguely recognize from debate team.

And Evan, in his usual isolated corner table near the biography section.

He's maybe thirty feet away, but it might as well be miles. During the day, we exist in completely separate spheres.

I pull out my history textbook and try to focus on reading about Progressive Era reform movements. But I'm hyperaware of Evan's presence. Every time he shifts in his chair or turns a page, I notice. It's like there's a magnetic pull between us that I have to actively resist.

Fifteen minutes into my reading, I glance up and find Evan looking at me.

Our eyes meet across the crowded library. Just for a second. Long enough for him to give me the smallest smile—barely there, gone almost before it registers. A smile that says I know you're there, I see you, this is ours.

I smile back, equally small, equally quick. Then we both return to our work like nothing happened.

But my heart is racing.

That tiny moment of connection in the middle of the day feels transgressive. Dangerous. Like we're breaking a different kind of rule than the midnight curfew violations.

Because acknowledging each other during daylight hours would require explaining how we know each other. Would risk questions neither of us wants to answer.

So we don't. We maintain our separate lives and wait for midnight when we can be real.

The cognitive dissonance is exhausting.

After study period, I have Honor Council at four. We're reviewing a case about a student who was caught plagiarizing a history paper—copied three paragraphs verbatim from an online source without citation. It's pretty clear-cut. The student admits it, claims they panicked about a deadline and made a bad choice.

We vote unanimously for academic probation and a failing grade on the assignment. I give a short speech about how academic integrity is fundamental to Thornbridge's mission, how we all face pressure and deadlines but that doesn't justify dishonesty.

The words taste like ash in my mouth.

Because I'm lecturing about integrity while lying to everyone in my life about where I spend my nights. I'm judging someone for breaking rules while I'm currently serving on a council I wouldn't qualify for if anyone knew about my own violations.

The hypocrisy sits in my chest like a stone.

Victoria Ashford, the senior Honor Council president, catches me after the meeting.

"Good thoughts on the plagiarism case," she says. "You have a clear sense of principle."

"Thanks."

"I wanted to mention—we'll be nominating next year's council positions in the spring. I think you'd make an excellent vice president as a senior. You should consider running."

This should feel like validation. Recognition of my leadership and judgment.

Instead, it feels like being trapped in a role I'm not sure I want to play anymore.

"I'll think about it," I manage.

That evening, I sit in my room pretending to do homework while actually watching the clock. 7:00. 8:00. 9:00. Caroline is at the desk across from me working through calculus problem sets, occasionally sighing dramatically when an integral doesn't work out.

"Do you ever feel like you're living two completely different lives?" I ask suddenly.

Caroline looks up, surprised by the question. "What do you mean?"

"Like... there's the version of you that everyone sees, and then there's the real you that only exists in private. And those two versions don't quite match up."

Caroline considers this. "I guess? I mean, I'm more myself with my close friends than I am in class or at formal dinners with my parents' friends. But I don't think they're completely different people. Just different contexts bringing out different aspects."

"What if the private version and the public version are so different they feel incompatible?"

"Then maybe you're hiding too much of yourself," Caroline says thoughtfully. "Or maybe you're in an environment where being fully yourself isn't safe."

The observation hits closer than I expected.

"Why are you asking?" Caroline adds.

"No reason. Just thinking."

Caroline accepts this and goes back to her calculus. But I sit with her words.

Am I hiding too much of myself? Or is Thornbridge an environment where being fully myself isn't safe?

Both, maybe.

The version of Harper Mills that everyone sees—accomplished student, Honor Council representative, pre-law track, perfect GPA—is real. I'm not faking the work or the achievement. But she's incomplete. She's what I can manage to be when I'm performing at maximum capacity, meeting everyone's expectations, following the script I've been given.

The version of me that exists in the library at midnight—uncertain, questioning, honest about my fears and insomnia and the constant pressure of pretending to have everything together—feels more whole. More real.

But that version only exists with Evan. Only exists in a space we're not supposed to be in, during hours when we're supposed to be sleeping.

The me that Madison sees and the me that Evan knows are different people. And I don't know how to reconcile them.

At 11:45 PM, Caroline finally goes to bed. I wait for her breathing to even out, then slip into my routine: hoodie, sneakers, careful navigation of creaky floorboards and the squeaky third stair.

The walk to the library feels different tonight. Not like escape, but like returning home.

When I climb through the window and settle into my chair, I feel the tension in my shoulders finally start to release. Here, I don't have to perform. Don't have to pretend I have all the answers or know what I want from my future.

Here, I can just be Harper. Messy, uncertain, flawed Harper who's trying to figure things out.

Evan arrives at 1:08 AM. He takes his usual chair, close enough that I could reach out and touch him if I wanted to.

"How was your day?" he asks, like we're normal friends who talk during normal hours.

"Exhausting. Yours?"

"Long."

We fall into easy conversation about nothing important—a teacher's ridiculous assignment, something funny that happened in the dining hall, whether the library's heating system is actually broken or just chronically unreliable.

It's comfortable and familiar and real in a way my daytime life isn't.

And sitting here with Evan, talking about mundane things that somehow feel meaningful, I realize something.

I don't want to keep living two separate lives.

I don't want to keep pretending we're strangers during the day while being... whatever we're becoming at night.

But I don't know how to bridge that gap without risking everything.

So for now, I stay in this holding pattern. Daytime Harper and nighttime Harper. The public version and the private version. Two lives that feel increasingly impossible to maintain simultaneously.

But I don't know which one to choose.

Or if choosing is even an option.
