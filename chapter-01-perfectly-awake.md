# Chapter 1: Perfectly Awake

The ceiling of Hargrove Hall has forty-three water stains.

I know because I've counted them. Twice. Tonight alone.

It's 2:17 AM, and I'm wide awake, listening to Caroline's peaceful, rhythmic breathing from the other side of our dorm room. She sleeps the way she plays field hockey—confidently, without hesitation. In through the nose, out through the mouth, steady as a metronome. Meanwhile, I'm lying here cataloging ceiling damage like it's some kind of productive use of my insomnia.

I roll onto my side, careful not to let my ancient twin bed creak. The sheets are cold despite the radiator clanking away beneath the window. October in western Massachusetts means the heating system hasn't quite figured out what it's doing yet—too hot during the day, too cold at night, the ancient pipes in Thornbridge's historic buildings protesting every temperature change with groans and rattles that sound like the whole place might come apart.

My mind won't stop.

Tomorrow—well, technically today, since it's already past two—I have calculus at 8 AM. I haven't finished the problem set. After that, there's English Lit, where I'm supposed to lead the discussion on *The Great Gatsby*. I've read it twice, made color-coded notes, prepared discussion questions. But have I prepared *enough*? What if someone asks something I haven't considered? What if I sound stupid in front of Ms. Patterson, who writes recommendation letters that can make or break Ivy League applications?

Then there's Honor Council at four. We're reviewing a case about plagiarism—a sophomore who copied half an essay from the internet. I'll have to weigh in on the consequences, maintain my reputation as fair but firm, uphold the academic integrity standards that Thornbridge has maintained since 1889.

After that, debate team practice until six. Dinner at seven, where I'll sit with Madison and the other pre-law track students and discuss college rankings and LSAT prep courses and summer internship opportunities. Then evening study hall from eight to ten, where I'll finish that calculus problem set and start my college essay that's been haunting me for three weeks.

And maybe, if I'm lucky, I'll be back in this bed by eleven, staring at the ceiling again, my brain refusing to shut down.

This has been my life for six weeks now. Since the semester started. Since junior year began and suddenly everything became about college applications and maintaining my 4.0 and proving I belong at Thornbridge Preparatory Academy, where half the students are legacy admissions whose families have attended for generations, and the other half—people like me—are here on academic merit and can't afford to slip even once.

My mother calls every Sunday. "How are your grades, Harper? Have you scheduled your SAT retake? You need at least a 1500 to be competitive for the schools we're looking at. Have you started your Common App essay? You know, when I was applying to law school..."

The thing is, I used to love this. The structure. The achievement. The satisfaction of seeing my name on the Dean's List, of being elected junior class representative on the Honor Council, of having teachers nod approvingly when I raise my hand. I used to sleep at night.

I don't know when that changed.

I stare at the water stains and try to make shapes out of them. That one looks like a bird. That one might be a face. Or maybe I'm losing my mind from sleep deprivation.

Caroline shifts in her bed, and I freeze. The last thing I need is for her to wake up and find me staring at the ceiling like some kind of insomniac vampire. Caroline is lovely—practical, straightforward, the kind of person who solves problems by making lists and following them. But she's also the kind of person who would suggest I talk to the school counselor about my sleep issues, which would then go on my student health record, which would then become one more thing to explain on college applications.

*Yes, I had trouble sleeping junior year due to crippling anxiety about meeting expectations I set for myself based on my mother's unfulfilled ambitions and my own terror of being exposed as someone who doesn't actually belong at this school.*

That'll look great next to my extracurricular achievements.

Caroline's breathing evens out again. I exhale slowly.

The room is small, barely big enough for two twin beds, two desks, and a shared dresser. Caroline's side is neat—her field hockey stick propped in the corner, her blazer hanging perfectly on the back of her desk chair, her textbooks stacked in order of class period. My side looks presentable but feels like a stage set. Everything in its place. Everything arranged to project competence and control.

My phone screen glows on my nightstand: 2:34 AM.

In five and a half hours, I need to be awake, alert, and ready to perform being Harper Mills, successful student. The girl who has it all together. The girl who makes it look easy.

I close my eyes and try to slow my breathing. In through the nose, out through the mouth, like Caroline. Maybe if I imitate her peaceful sleep, my brain will get the message.

Instead, I start mentally reviewing the Gatsby themes I'll need to cover in English. The American Dream. Class and social stratification. The past's hold on the present. The difference between who we are and who we pretend to be.

That last one hits a little too close.

My eyes snap open. The ceiling stains blur together.

I can't do this again. Another night of lying here, exhausted but unable to sleep, my thoughts spiraling through every obligation and inadequacy and fear until the sun comes up and I drag myself through another day running on nothing but determination and anxiety.

I need to move. I need to go somewhere. Anywhere but this bed, this room, this feeling of being trapped inside my own head.

At 2:47 AM, I slip out from under the covers.

The hardwood floor is cold against my bare feet. I grab my Thornbridge hoodie from the back of my desk chair and pull it on over my pajamas, then ease my feet into my sneakers without bothering to tie them. Every movement is calculated for silence. The floor creaks near the door, so I step carefully to the left where the boards are more solid.

Caroline doesn't stir.

I pause with my hand on the doorknob, my heart beating faster than it should for someone who's just getting out of bed. What am I even doing? It's almost three in the morning. I'm on Honor Council. I'm supposed to uphold the rules, not break them. Thornbridge has a strict 11 PM curfew. Students are meant to be in their dorms, in their rooms, sleeping like normal people who don't count ceiling stains and catastrophize about Gatsby discussions.

But I turn the knob anyway.

The hallway is dimly lit by emergency lighting, casting everything in a yellowish glow that makes the corridor look longer than it is during the day. The pay phone at the end of the hall—an ancient relic that barely anyone uses now that we all have cell phones—gleams dully. The bathroom door is closed. Everything is silent except for the building's night sounds: the radiator, the old wood settling, the distant sound of wind against the windows.

I close my door with a soft click and stand there for a moment, waiting to see if anyone heard. If anyone cares.

No one appears.

I walk down the hallway, each step feeling like a small rebellion. Past the bathroom. Past the common room with its worn couches and the kitchenette with its ancient microwave. Down the stairs, gripping the banister to avoid the squeaky third step that everyone knows about.

The first floor is darker. The main entrance is locked from the inside—you can leave but you need a key card to get back in. I should go back upstairs. Go back to bed. Try again to sleep.

Instead, I push open the side door that leads to the quad.

The October air hits me like cold water. I gasp, and the sound seems too loud in the absolute silence of the campus at night. The quad stretches out before me, a perfect rectangle of grass surrounded by brick buildings that look like they've been here forever. The library rises to my right, three stories of colonial architecture with its tall windows dark. The academic buildings frame the other sides. Above, the stars are sharp and clear in a way they never are during the day, when campus is full of noise and movement and the constant pressure to be someone worth noticing.

Out here, there's no one to notice me at all.

My breath forms clouds in the cold air. I shove my hands into my hoodie pockets and start walking, not sure where I'm going, just knowing I need to move.

I need somewhere quiet. Somewhere I can think clearly. Somewhere the weight of expectation doesn't press down on my chest like a physical thing.

I need to find a place where I can finally breathe.
