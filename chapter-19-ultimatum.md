# Chapter 19: Ultimatum

I tell Evan about Caroline the moment I see him.

It's midnight, and he's already in our usual spot when I climb through the window. The familiar weight of my backpack, the cold January air, the emergency lighting—it should all feel normal. But there's a knot in my stomach that's been there since Caroline confronted me.

"We need to talk," I say as I approach.

Evan looks up from his book, and something in my expression makes him set it aside immediately. "What's wrong?"

I sit down and explain what happened. Caroline waiting up for me. Her ultimatum. My promise to stop. The lie I told because I had no other choice.

As I speak, Evan's expression shifts from concern to something heavier—resignation, maybe, or the confirmation of a fear he's been carrying all along.

"We should stop," he says when I finish. His voice is quiet but firm.

"What?"

"We should stop meeting like this." He won't quite meet my eyes. "Your roommate knows you're sneaking out. She could catch you again, or she could report you. This is getting too risky."

"I told you," I say, trying to keep my voice steady, "I promised Caroline I'd stop, but I'm not actually going to—"

"Harper." He cuts me off, and now he does look at me. "You shouldn't be lying to your roommate for this. For me."

Heat flares in my chest. "That's my choice to make."

"Is it?" Evan stands up, starting to pace in the small space between our chairs. "Because from where I'm sitting, you're the one with everything to lose. Honor Council position, college applications, your perfect reputation. If Caroline reports you, or if you get caught some other way, your entire future goes up in smoke."

"I know the risks—"

"Do you?" His voice rises slightly, then he catches himself and lowers it to an urgent whisper. "Harper, I have nothing here. No friends, no reputation to protect, no big future hanging in the balance. If I get caught breaking curfew, it's just another mark against the weird transfer kid. But you? You're supposed to be the model student. You're on Honor Council, for god's sake. The hypocrisy alone would destroy you."

I stand up too, my hands clenched at my sides. "So what, you're deciding for me now? You're determining what's worth the risk in my life?"

"I'm being realistic." Evan stops pacing and faces me directly. "I won't let you throw away everything you've worked for because of this. Because of me."

"Stop acting like you're doing me a favor," I snap. "This isn't noble. It's insulting. Like I'm not capable of making my own decisions."

"That's not—" He runs a hand through his hair, frustrated. "I'm trying to protect you."

"I don't need your protection. I need you to trust that I know what I'm doing."

"But do you?" The question comes out sharper than I think he intended. "Because from where I'm standing, you're making increasingly reckless choices for something that has no future."

The words hit like a slap. "What do you mean, no future?"

Evan is quiet for a long moment. When he speaks again, his voice has lost its edge, replaced by something that sounds like defeat. "Harper, think about it. We graduate in a few months. You're going to some prestigious university—Yale or Princeton or wherever. You'll major in pre-law, join the debate team, make new friends who fit into your world."

"And?" I ask, though I think I know where this is going.

"And I'll be a weird memory from junior year. The guy you used to sneak around with in the middle of the night. Something you'll look back on and wonder what you were thinking."

"That's not true."

"Isn't it?" He finally looks at me fully, and the vulnerability in his expression makes my chest ache. "Be honest with yourself. In a year, two years, when you're living your real life with people who actually matter, do you really think you're going to remember the isolated transfer student who sat alone in the library?"

"You matter," I say fiercely. "You matter more than—"

"More than what? Your future? Your reputation? Your carefully planned life?" He shakes his head. "Don't say things you don't mean. We both know how this ends. I'm a detour, Harper. A brief rebellion against all that perfection you're supposed to maintain. But eventually you'll get back on track, and I'll just be a footnote."

The casual way he dismisses our connection, dismisses himself, makes anger surge through me. "How dare you tell me what I feel. How dare you act like you know what matters to me better than I do."

"I know what should matter to you," Evan says quietly. "Your education, your future, your goals. All the things you've worked for since freshman year. You shouldn't be risking that for someone like me."

"Someone like you," I repeat, my voice sharp. "What does that even mean? Someone who's been falsely accused? Someone who eats alone because people are too quick to judge? Someone who actually sees me as a person instead of a collection of accomplishments?"

"Harper—"

"No." I'm trembling now, whether from anger or hurt or both, I can't tell. "You don't get to decide you're not worth it. You don't get to make that choice for me."

"I'm trying to save you from making a mistake you'll regret."

"What if the mistake would be giving this up?" The question comes out raw, honest. "What if the thing I'd regret is listening to you right now?"

Evan closes his eyes briefly. "You say that now. But when you're sitting in some college interview trying to explain why you got suspended from Thornbridge for breaking and entering, when you're watching your college acceptances turn to rejections, when your mother is disappointed and your future is derailed—you'll regret it then."

"You don't know that."

"I know that I'm not worth ruining your life over." His voice cracks slightly on the words.

"That's the thing you're not getting." I step closer to him, forcing him to look at me. "This—what we have here—this is the first time in months, maybe years, that I've felt like my life is actually mine. Not my mother's plan for me, not Thornbridge's expectations, not Madison's competitive timeline. Mine."

"And what happens when you have to choose?" Evan asks. "When it comes down to keeping this secret or protecting your future, what are you going to pick?"

The question hangs between us, heavy with the weight of everything we're not saying. Because he's asking me to prove something I can't prove. Not with words. Not here in the dark where we're safely hidden from the consequences of our connection.

"I don't know," I admit, and the honesty feels like defeat. "I don't know what I'd choose if it came to that."

Evan nods slowly, like I've just confirmed what he already believed. "That's what I thought."

"But I'm here now," I press. "Despite Caroline's ultimatum, despite the risk, despite everything that says this is a terrible idea—I'm here. Doesn't that count for something?"

"It counts for a lot," Evan says softly. "But it also scares me. Because the more you risk for this, for me, the more I'm going to hurt you when it all falls apart."

"When, not if?"

"Harper." He says my name like a prayer or a goodbye. "I've already watched one person I trusted throw me under the bus to save herself. I know how this works. When push comes to shove, people choose self-preservation. It's not wrong—it's human. But I can't go through that again."

"I'm not Melissa," I say, finally understanding. This isn't really about Caroline's ultimatum or the risk to my future. This is about Evan protecting himself from another betrayal.

"I know you're not. But you're also not in an impossible situation yet. Once you are—when you actually have to choose between me and everything else—that's when I'll find out who you really are."

"And you've already decided I'll choose everything else."

"I've decided," Evan says carefully, "that I don't want to be the reason you lose everything. Even if that means losing this."

We stand in silence, the space between us feeling wider than it is. The library suddenly feels less like a sanctuary and more like a cage—a place where we can pretend our connection exists in a vacuum, separate from the real world.

"So that's it?" I ask. "You're just deciding this is over?"

"I'm saying maybe we should take a step back. Before someone gets hurt."

"I think it's too late for that," I whisper.

Evan's expression softens, and for a moment I think he's going to reach for me. But he doesn't. "I'm sorry. I'm just—I'm trying to be smart about this."

"Since when is being smart the most important thing?"

"Since you're risking your entire future for a relationship that exists only in secret, in the middle of the night, in a place we're not supposed to be." He picks up his backpack. "Think about what you really want, Harper. Think about what you're willing to sacrifice. And if the answer is everything—your Honor Council position, your college plans, your carefully built life—then we can talk. But until you're sure, maybe we shouldn't keep doing this."

He heads toward the window, and panic surges through me. "Evan, wait—"

He pauses but doesn't turn around. "I'll give you space to figure out what you actually want. What you're really willing to risk. Because I can't keep pretending this doesn't have an expiration date."

Then he's gone, slipping through the window into the January darkness, leaving me alone in the library with the emergency lights and the weight of questions I don't know how to answer.

What am I willing to sacrifice? What do I actually want?

And the most terrifying question of all: Is Evan right that I'll choose my perfect future over him when it really matters?

I sink into my chair and stare at the empty space where he was sitting minutes ago. The library feels different now—less like a sanctuary and more like a reminder of everything I'm risking and everything I might lose.

I don't know how long I sit there before I finally make myself leave. But when I climb back through the window into the cold night air, I'm no closer to having answers.

I just know that the thought of not seeing Evan tomorrow night, or the night after, feels like losing something essential.

But maybe he's right. Maybe I need to figure out what I'm really willing to give up before I can ask him to believe in us.

The question is: am I brave enough to find out?
