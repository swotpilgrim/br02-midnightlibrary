# Chapter 11: Growing Closer

Evan arrives on Saturday night carrying something I don't immediately recognize.

It's 1:12 AM and I'm curled up in my chair rereading *Pride and Prejudice* for the pure pleasure of it, no essay required. I look up when I hear him on the stairs, and that's when I see it: a silver thermos, the kind from the dining hall that they use for coffee service.

"Did you steal that?" I ask, trying not to smile.

"Borrowed," Evan corrects, settling into his chair. "I'll return it eventually."

"What's in it?"

"Hot chocolate. Real hot chocolate, not the powdered stuff. I convinced one of the dining hall staff to let me take some before they closed up for the night."

"How did you manage that?"

Evan shrugs. "I helped her carry supplies to the storage room a few weeks ago. She remembered. People are nicer than you'd think if you're actually nice to them first."

He unscrews the top and pours hot chocolate into the thermos cap, then hands it to me. Steam rises from the dark liquid, and the smell of real chocolate fills the space between us.

I take a sip. It's perfect—rich and warm and exactly what I didn't know I needed.

"This is amazing," I say.

"Thought you might like it. You mentioned being cold last night."

Had I mentioned that? I don't remember saying it explicitly, but my hands had been freezing and I'd been rubbing them together while we talked. Evan noticed. And remembered.

Something warm unfolds in my chest that has nothing to do with the hot chocolate.

We share the thermos back and forth, taking turns drinking from the cap while we talk. Tonight's conversation starts with books—Evan is reading Virginia Woolf for his own interest, not for class.

"She's brilliant," he says. "The way she captures how people actually think instead of how they pretend to. It's real."

I've never thought of Woolf that way. We read *To the Lighthouse* last year and I found it difficult.

"Maybe I should give her another chance," I say.

"You should. But don't read her for school. Read her for yourself. There's a difference."

We debate this, getting into questions about autonomy and choice and whether anything can be fully enjoyed if it's required. Evan argues that obligation kills pleasure. I counter that sometimes we don't know what we'll love until someone makes us try it.

"So you're saying you're grateful for being forced to read boring books in English class?" Evan challenges.

"I'm saying some of the books I love most are ones I had to read for class. I never would have picked up *Jane Eyre* on my own. But now it's one of my favorites."

"Fair point. Though I'd argue you loved it despite it being assigned, not because of it."

"Maybe. Or maybe the structure of assignment gave me permission to spend time with it that I wouldn't have given myself otherwise."

Evan considers this, taking another sip of hot chocolate. "You're saying sometimes we need external permission to do things we actually want to do?"

"Maybe. I don't know. I'm thinking out loud."

"I like when you think out loud."

The comment is casual, almost throwaway. But it hits me square in the chest. Because I like thinking out loud with Evan. I like that I can work through half-formed ideas without being judged for not having all the answers immediately. I like that he engages with my arguments instead of just agreeing or dismissing them.

I like him.

The realization isn't new, exactly. I've been aware for weeks that Evan matters to me, that our conversations are the best part of my day. But sitting here in the pool of greenish emergency lighting, sharing stolen hot chocolate, arguing about Virginia Woolf and the nature of autonomy, I realize it's more than just liking.

I'm falling for him.

Somehow, impossibly, I'm falling for Evan Cross—the weird transfer kid everyone dismisses, the outsider who eats alone, the boy no one bothers to know. Except I do know him. I know he's thoughtful and funny and kind. I know he notices when people are cold and remembers to bring them hot chocolate. I know he reads philosophy and literary fiction at 1 AM because he actually loves it, not because it will look good on college applications.

I know him in ways no one else at Thornbridge does. And that knowing has become the most important thing in my life.

The realization must show on my face because Evan asks, "What?"

"Nothing. Just... thanks. For the hot chocolate. And for this." I gesture vaguely at the library around us, at the space we've claimed together.

"No problem," Evan says. But he's looking at me with an intensity that makes my skin feel warm.

We've been sitting closer lately. I'm not sure when it started exactly, but the distance between our chairs has shrunk from six feet to maybe three. Close enough that I can see the details of his face in the dim lighting—the way his dark hair falls across his forehead, the sharp line of his jaw, the almost-smile that plays around his mouth when I say something that amuses him.

Close enough that when he hands me the thermos cap refilled with hot chocolate, our fingers brush. The contact lasts maybe a second, but I'm hyperaware of it. Of the warmth of his skin. The careful way he pulls back so as not to spill.

"What do you actually want to do?" I ask suddenly. "If you could do anything. If your parents' expectations didn't matter."

Evan is quiet for a moment. Then: "I want to write. Not just as a hobby or on the side. I want to study literature and creative writing in college. I want to try to write something that matters, even if I'm not very good at it. I want to spend my life trying to understand how stories work and why they matter and how to tell them well."

The passion in his voice is unmistakable. This isn't a casual dream. It's a deep need that he's been suppressing in favor of more "practical" paths.

"You should do it," I say.

"My parents will never agree."

"So don't ask them. Just do it. Apply to programs in creative writing. See what happens."

"And when they refuse to pay for it?"

"Then you figure it out. Scholarships. Financial aid. Working your way through. People do it."

"You're very optimistic about something that's not your life," Evan observes, but there's no bite to it.

"I'm optimistic about yours because I can't seem to manage it for mine," I admit. "I still haven't figured out what I actually want. Just what I don't want. Which is law school and following my mother's path and living my whole life according to someone else's script."

"So what do you want instead?"

"I don't know. Maybe literature, like you said before. Maybe teaching. Maybe something I haven't even considered yet because I've been so focused on the pre-law track that I haven't let myself look at alternatives."

"You should look," Evan says quietly. "You're too smart and too interesting to spend your life doing something you don't love just because it's what someone else wants."

"So are you."

We're looking at each other now, and the air between us feels charged with something I can't quite name. The conversation has shifted from abstract discussion to something more personal, more intimate. We're not just talking about career paths. We're talking about choosing ourselves. About being brave enough to want things that don't fit other people's expectations.

About being seen.

"Can I tell you something?" Evan asks.

"Always."

"This—" he gestures at the space between us "—is the best part of every day. Coming here. Talking to you. Being able to say things I can't say to anyone else. It's... it matters. You matter."

My heart is doing complicated things in my chest. "You matter to me too."

"Even though we don't acknowledge each other during the day? Even though we're basically leading double lives?"

"Especially because of that, maybe. Because this is real in a way the daytime stuff isn't. When I'm here with you, I don't have to perform. I can just... be."

"Yeah," Evan says softly. "Me too."

We're sitting close enough now that if I leaned forward just slightly, I could touch him. Close enough that I can see the way his eyes reflect the emergency lighting. Close enough that I'm acutely aware of every breath, every small movement.

I should probably move back. Create distance. This is getting into territory that feels dangerous in all the best and worst ways.

But I don't move back. And neither does he.

"Harper," Evan says, my name soft on his lips.

"Yeah?"

"I'm really glad you found that window."

"Me too."

We sit in the charged silence, neither of us quite brave enough to name what's happening between us. Because naming it would make it real. Would require figuring out how to navigate it, what it means, whether it can survive outside these midnight hours.

The chapel clock chimes 2:30 AM. The sound breaks the moment, and Evan sits back slightly, creating space.

"I should probably let you actually read," he says, nodding toward the *Pride and Prejudice* in my lap that I haven't looked at in over an hour.

"Right. Yeah."

But I don't open the book. And Evan doesn't open his philosophy text. We just sit in comfortable silence, closer than we've ever been, the thermos of hot chocolate between us creating a small circle of warmth in the vast, quiet library.

I catch myself staring at his hands—the way he holds his book, the occasional gesture he makes when he's thinking. I notice the line of his profile when he's looking out the window at the snow still falling. I'm aware of him in ways that go beyond simple observation.

When our eyes meet again, something passes between us. An understanding that we've crossed another invisible line tonight. That whatever this is between us, it's becoming something we can't ignore.

Something that feels a lot like falling.

Later, when I'm back in my room staring at the ceiling, I'm not thinking about insomnia or anxiety or college essays. I'm thinking about the way Evan said my name. The warmth of his fingers when they brushed mine. The intensity in his eyes when he said "You matter."

I'm thinking that I'm happier in those midnight hours than anywhere else in my life.

And I'm starting to realize it's not just about the library anymore.

It's about him.
