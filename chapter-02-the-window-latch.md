# Chapter 2: The Window Latch

I've never seen Thornbridge like this.

During the day, campus is all movement and noise—students rushing between classes, voices calling across the quad, the constant hum of achievement and competition. But at three in the morning, it's transformed. The brick buildings look like the old photographs from 1889. The ivy creates shifting shadows. The lampposts cast pools of amber light across empty pathways.

It's beautiful. And completely off-limits.

I keep to the shadows, my heart hammering at every sound. Getting caught would mean a disciplinary hearing, possible suspension, and a mark on my record that would haunt every college application.

But I keep walking anyway, because the alternative is going back to that room, that bed, that ceiling and the endless loop of anxiety that won't let me rest.

The quad grass is damp with dew, soaking through my sneakers. I pass Morrison Hall, then Pembroke. The chapel's white steeple points up at the stars. I duck behind a maintenance shed when I hear footsteps—heavy, deliberate. Security patrol. I wait, pressed against the cold metal, until the footsteps fade.

When I'm sure they're gone, I emerge and find myself facing the library.

It rises before me like a cathedral, three stories of brick and tradition. The original building, constructed when Thornbridge was founded. During the day, it's packed with students—at tables, in study carrels, sprawled on the leather couches in the reading room. The reference desk is always staffed. The copy machines are always running. There's never a moment of complete quiet.

But now, the windows are dark except for the faint glow of what must be emergency lighting inside. The main entrance is flanked by two stone lions that guard the steps. The tall windows on the first floor reflect the lamplight from the quad. It looks impenetrable. Impossible. Like it exists in a different time entirely, untouchable and permanent while everything else changes.

I climb the front steps anyway, just to try.

The heavy wooden doors are locked, of course. I pull on the brass handles anyway, rattling them slightly before remembering that I'm trying not to get caught. The sound seems thunderously loud in the silence. I freeze, waiting for security to materialize, for lights to snap on, for my incredibly short-lived rebellion to end in spectacular failure.

Nothing happens.

I descend the steps and walk around the building's perimeter, not sure what I'm looking for. Maybe just a place to sit, somewhere with a view different from my dorm room window. The library is surrounded by a narrow margin of landscaping—boxwood hedges and foundation plantings that probably look neat and proper during the day. Now they're just dark shapes.

On the west side of the building, half-hidden behind an overgrown boxwood, I notice a window that's slightly different from the others.

It's cracked open. Just an inch, maybe less. Enough that I can see it doesn't quite sit flush with the frame.

I glance around, confirming I'm alone, then push through the hedge to get closer. Branches scratch at my hoodie. I probably look completely insane, a junior in pajamas crawling through shrubbery at three in the morning. If Madison could see me now, she'd think I'd had a complete mental breakdown.

Maybe I have.

The window is old—original wavy glass in a wooden frame painted so many times the layers are visible at the edges. I reach up and push gently.

The window swings inward easily, soundlessly.

The latch is broken. Or just old enough that it doesn't catch properly. Either way, it opens wide enough for someone desperate enough to climb through.

I stare at the opening, my hands still on the frame.

This is wrong. I'm on Honor Council. Last week I sat in a hearing about plagiarism and voted for academic probation. I gave a whole speech about integrity, about how Thornbridge's reputation is built on students who honor community standards.

And now I'm considering climbing through a window at three in the morning.

But my brain won't stop spinning. My chest feels tight with the weight of everything—the expectations, the pressure, the constant performance. I haven't slept properly in six weeks. I'm exhausted down to my bones, but I can't rest because my mind won't let me.

I just need somewhere quiet. Somewhere I can think without feeling observed, evaluated, judged.

Just for an hour.

I hoist myself up onto the window ledge. It's higher than it looked, and for a moment I'm balanced awkwardly with one leg inside and one still dangling outside, certain I'm about to fall and break something and have to explain to the school nurse why I was climbing through library windows at three AM.

But I don't fall. I pull myself through with less grace than I'd like but more determination than I knew I had, and then I'm inside.

The periodicals section.

I recognize it immediately from the shelving configuration, though everything looks different in the dim emergency lighting. Rows of magazines and newspapers, organized by date and subject, the smell of aging paper thick in the air. During the day, this section is near the entrance, bright and functional. Now it's shadowy and strange, transformed by the absence of people and fluorescent lights.

I ease the window almost-closed behind me, leaving it the way I found it in case I need to exit quickly. Then I stand still, listening.

Silence. Complete, absolute silence.

No radiator clanking. No Caroline breathing. No voices in the hallway or footsteps above me or the constant ambient noise of three hundred students living in close quarters. Just... nothing. The kind of quiet I haven't experienced since I arrived at Thornbridge in August.

It's perfect.

I move deeper into the library, my sneakers whispering against the old wooden floors. The emergency lighting is minimal—just enough to prevent someone from walking into walls in case of a fire—casting everything in a dim greenish glow that makes the familiar space feel otherworldly. Shadows pool in corners. The tall windows let in ambient light from the quad lamps, creating geometric patterns across the floor.

The main reading room opens before me like a cathedral. During the day, this is the heart of the library—study tables filled with students, the circulation desk staffed by librarians, the worn leather couches occupied by seniors who've claimed prime studying spots. Now it's empty and magnificent. The vaulted ceiling rises two stories above me. The mahogany bookshelves lining the walls seem to absorb what little light there is. The leather furniture sits in silent dignity, waiting.

I walk to the nearest shelf and run my fingers along the spines. Classics, mostly. Dickens, Austen, the Brontës, Hawthorne. Books I've been assigned to read for class, books I've written analytical essays about, books I've never had time to just read for the pleasure of it.

I pull out *Jane Eyre* and carry it to one of the wingback chairs near the windows.

The leather is cracked and broken-in, molded by years of students sitting in this exact spot. I curl up in it, tucking my feet under me, and open the book at random. I'm not really going to read—I've already written two papers on Brontë—but holding it feels right. Purposeful. Like I have a reason to be here beyond the fact that my brain is eating me alive with anxiety.

The chapel clock tower chimes three-thirty. The sound is muffled by the library walls but still audible, marking the passage of time in a world that feels suspended.

And then something happens that I wasn't expecting.

My mind... quiets.

Not completely. The thoughts are still there—tomorrow's calculus problem set, the Gatsby discussion, the Honor Council meeting. But they're distant now, manageable. Like background noise instead of sirens. The knot in my chest loosens slightly. My breathing deepens.

For the first time in weeks, I feel like I can think clearly.

I'm not sure how long I sit there. The chapel clock chimes four, then four-thirty. I page through *Jane Eyre*, reading passages I remember from freshman year. I explore the stacks on the second floor, discovering sections I've never bothered with—art history, philosophy, poetry. I find a window seat on the third floor that overlooks the entire quad.

Through the tall windows, I watch the sky begin to change. Not quite sunrise yet, but the deep black fading to dark blue, the stars becoming less sharp. Pre-dawn.

I should go back. Caroline will be waking up in an hour, and I need to be in my bed, pretending I slept normally, ready to be Harper Mills again.

But I'm different now. The desperate anxiety has subsided. I'm still tired, but it's a normal tired, not the bone-deep exhaustion of running on empty.

I make my way back down to the periodicals section, careful and quiet, hyperaware that I'm still breaking multiple school rules. The window is exactly as I left it. I climb through, grateful for the empty quad, and ease it almost-closed behind me. The faulty latch holds it slightly ajar, invisible unless you're looking for it.

Walking back to Hargrove Hall, I feel lighter. Like I've found something I didn't know I was looking for. A space that's mine, where no one's watching, where I don't have to perform or prove anything. Where I can just exist.

I slip back into my room at 5:43 AM. Caroline is still asleep, one arm flung over her face. I ease out of my hoodie and sneakers and slide back into bed.

The ceiling stains are still there. Forty-three of them.

But this time, when I close my eyes, I finally sleep.
