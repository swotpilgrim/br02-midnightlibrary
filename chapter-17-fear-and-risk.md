# Chapter 17: Fear and Risk

I lie awake all day.

That's the irony that would be funny if it weren't so exhausting: I started sneaking into the library because I couldn't sleep, and now I can't sleep because of what almost happened in the library. The afternoon sun streams through the window, painting bright squares on Caroline's empty bed—she's at field hockey practice—and I stare at the ceiling replaying last night frame by frame.

Evan's hand tucking my hair behind my ear. The way his eyes asked permission. The electricity in the air between us. The exact shade of gray-green his eyes turned in the emergency lighting. How close we were. How much I wanted him to close that final distance.

Then the footsteps. The panic. Running through the cold.

My stomach does a complicated flip, part exhilaration, part terror.

If security had arrived thirty seconds earlier, they would have found us. Not just breaking curfew, not just trespassing in the library—but together, alone, about to kiss. The optics would have been damning. Harper Mills, Honor Council representative, perfect student, caught in a compromising position with Evan Cross, the isolated transfer student everyone suspects is trouble.

I would have been suspended. Maybe expelled.

I roll onto my side, pulling my knees up to my chest. The consequences cascade through my mind in vivid detail: Dr. Whitmore's disappointed face as she calls my parents. My mother's cold fury over the phone. The Honor Council meeting where Victoria would have no choice but to remove me. The note in my permanent record. The college application disaster—trying to explain why I got kicked out of boarding school junior year.

Everything I've worked for since freshman year, gone. The 4.0 GPA meaningless. The debate tournaments, the leadership positions, the carefully cultivated reputation as someone serious and accomplished—all of it destroyed because I couldn't control myself around a boy.

Around Evan.

I press my palms against my eyes. That's the part that should terrify me most, isn't it? That I almost threw away my entire future for someone I've known for a few months. Someone who sits alone in the dining hall and doesn't talk in class and has a past he won't fully explain. Someone my friends dismiss as weird, troubled, antisocial. Someone who represents everything I'm not supposed to want.

But when I remember the way he looked at me last night—like I was someone worth knowing, worth wanting—I can't bring myself to regret it.

That's what actually terrifies me.

Not the near-miss with security. Not the potential consequences. But the fact that even knowing the risk, even understanding everything I could lose, I still want him to kiss me. I still want to climb through that library window tonight and see him and pick up where we left off.

What's wrong with me?

I've spent my entire life being careful. Measuring risks against rewards. Making the smart choice, the safe choice, the choice that keeps me on track toward the future I'm supposed to want. I don't do reckless. I don't do impulsive. I definitely don't do secret relationships with boys everyone thinks are bad news.

Except apparently, I do.

I sit up, running my hands through my hair. Through the window I can see students crossing the quad between classes. Madison reviewing her pre-law notes, Caroline at field hockey practice, Victoria in some Honor Council meeting. They all fit perfectly into Thornbridge's expectations.

And then there's me, lying in bed unable to sleep because I can't stop thinking about a boy I'm not supposed to want and an almost-kiss that could have ruined everything.

My phone buzzes with a text from Madison: "Where are you? Missed you at lunch."

I type back: "Not feeling great. Resting."

It's not even a lie. I'm feeling unmoored, uncertain, like I'm standing on the edge of something I don't fully understand. The ground beneath my feet used to be solid—grades, achievements, plans. Now it's shifting, and I don't know if I'm excited or terrified.

Both. I'm both.

The hours drag by. Caroline comes back from practice, asks if I'm okay. I say I'm fine, just tired.

More lies.

Dinner in the dining hall is torture. I sit with Madison and the other pre-law students, pushing food around my plate while they discuss an upcoming debate tournament. Across the room, Evan sits alone as always, reading while he eats. Our eyes don't meet. We're both too careful for that.

But I'm aware of him with every cell in my body. I know when he gets up to leave. I know which door he exits through. I know he's probably thinking about last night too, wondering the same things I'm wondering.

Will we acknowledge it? Will we pretend it didn't happen? Will we—

"Harper?"

I jerk my attention back to Madison, who's looking at me with concern. "Sorry, what?"

"I asked if you want to study together tonight. You seem really out of it."

"I'm fine," I say automatically. "Just tired. I think I'm going to turn in early."

Madison exchanges a look with another girl at our table—a look that clearly says *Harper is acting weird*—but doesn't push it.

At eleven-thirty I'm lying in my bed, wide awake, listening to Caroline's breathing slow into sleep. My heart is already racing. Tonight we have to talk about what almost happened. We have to decide if we're going to let it happen for real.

Midnight. I slip out of bed, pull on jeans and a sweater, and pad silently to the door. The hallway is empty. I take my usual route—down the back stairs, through the side door, across the dark campus to the library.

The window opens with its familiar resistance. I climb through, and the library wraps around me like it always does—emergency lighting, old books, familiar shadows. But there's a tension in the air tonight that wasn't there before.

Evan is already in our usual spot. He's not reading. He's just sitting, staring at nothing, and when he hears me approach he looks up. His expression is guarded in a way it hasn't been in weeks.

"Hi," I say quietly.

"Hi."

I sit down in my chair—the one that's become mine through repetition—and we exist in awkward silence. We've had comfortable silences before, but this one is full of everything we're not saying.

Finally, Evan speaks. "We should talk about last night."

My stomach drops. "Okay."

He takes a breath, not quite meeting my eyes. "If this is too risky for you, I understand."

I feel something cold settle in my chest. "What do you mean?"

"I mean—" He gestures vaguely. "You have a lot more to lose than I do. Your Honor Council position, your college applications, your reputation. Everything you've worked for. If we'd been caught last night, it would have been bad for me. But it would have been devastating for you."

He's not wrong. But hearing him say it out loud makes something twist painfully in my gut.

"You didn't make me go to the library last night," I say carefully. "That was my choice."

"I know. But maybe it's not a choice you should keep making." He finally looks at me directly. "I won't let you throw away your future for... for this. For me."

"Shouldn't that be my decision?"

"Harper." His voice is gentle but firm. "Be realistic. I'm the transfer student with a questionable past who doesn't have any friends. You're the girl with the perfect GPA and the bright future. What we have here—" He gestures at the library around us. "—it exists in secret, in the dark. But your real life is out there, in daylight, and I'm not part of it."

"That's not fair," I protest, but even as I say it, I know he's right. During the day, I don't acknowledge him. During the day, I'm surrounded by people like Madison who dismiss him without a second thought.

"I'm not trying to be fair," Evan says. "I'm trying to be honest. You should think about whether this is worth the risk. Whether I'm worth the risk."

The question hangs in the air between us. Am I willing to risk everything—my carefully constructed future, my reputation, my achievements—for whatever this is between us?

I should say no. The smart answer is to agree with Evan that we should stop before someone gets hurt.

But when I look at him, I see the only person at Thornbridge who knows about my insomnia, my perfectionism, my fear that I'm living someone else's life. The only person who sees past the performance.

"I don't want to stop," I hear myself say.

Evan's eyes widen slightly. "Harper—"

"I don't," I repeat, more firmly. "Yes, last night was scary. Yes, there's risk. But I..." I struggle to find words for something I barely understand myself. "This matters. You matter. What we have here—it's real. More real than anything else in my life right now."

"Even if it costs you everything you've worked for?"

I take a shaky breath. "I don't know. Maybe. I just know that I'm not ready to give this up."

We sit in silence again, but it's different now. Not awkward, but weighted with significance. Evan is looking at me like he's trying to solve a complicated equation, trying to understand how someone like me could choose someone like him.

"I don't want to stop either," he finally admits, his voice barely above a whisper. "But I need you to understand what you're risking."

"I understand."

"Do you? Because once we cross this line—" He doesn't finish the sentence, but he doesn't have to.

Once we cross from friendship to something more, there's no going back. Once we acknowledge that the almost-kiss wasn't a mistake but something we both want, everything changes.

"I understand," I repeat. And this time, I think I actually do.

The risks are real. The consequences could be devastating. Everything I've spent years building could crumble because of feelings I can't explain and a connection that shouldn't exist but does.

But sitting here looking at Evan, seeing my own fear and hope reflected back, I realize something: maybe the life I've been building isn't the life I actually want. Maybe perfect isn't the same as happy. Maybe being known is worth more than being admired.

Maybe Evan Cross is worth the risk.

"Okay," Evan says quietly. "Okay."

We don't kiss. We don't even touch. But something shifts—an understanding, an agreement that we're both choosing this despite the fear.

We spend the next two hours talking about everything except what we just decided, and when I finally slip back to my dorm as the sky starts to lighten, I feel different.

Terrified, yes. But also certain.

Whatever comes next, we're in this together.
