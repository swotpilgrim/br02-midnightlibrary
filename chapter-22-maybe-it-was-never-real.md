# Chapter 22: Maybe It Was Never Real

I go to the library on the fifth night, my resolve from the previous evening still burning bright.

It's just past midnight when I climb through the window. Eleven days left. Eleven more chances. I can't waste another one.

But when I make my way to our usual spot, my determination wavers.

Evan is there, sitting in his chair with his arms crossed, staring at nothing. He looks tired—not just sleepy, but exhausted in the way that comes from carrying something heavy for too long. When he hears my footsteps, he doesn't look up right away. When he finally does, his expression is carefully blank.

"Hi," I say quietly.

"Hi."

I sit down in my chair, and the silence that follows is different from any we've had before. It's not comfortable. It's not charged with possibility. It's just...hollow. Like we're two strangers who happen to be in the same place at the same time.

Weeks of unsaid things sit between us—the almost-kiss, the arguments, the four days I spent hiding instead of facing him. I don't know how to bridge the distance.

Finally, Evan speaks. "Maybe this was a mistake from the beginning."

The words hit me like cold water. "What?"

He looks at me directly, and there's something in his eyes I haven't seen before—resignation, maybe. Or just tiredness. "This. Us. Whatever we've been doing here. Maybe it was never real."

"How can you say that?" My voice comes out sharper than I intended.

"Because we created a fantasy world in here." He gestures at the library around us. "Late at night, in secret, away from everyone and everything. We pretended we could exist in this bubble where nothing else mattered. But it was never real, Harper. It was just..."

"Just what?"

"An escape. For both of us." He leans back in his chair, and he looks more defeated than I've ever seen him. "You were escaping the pressure of being perfect, and I was escaping being nobody. But our real lives are out there, in daylight, and this—" He gestures between us. "—this doesn't work outside these walls."

I feel like he's just punched me in the stomach. "So all those conversations we had, all the times we were honest with each other, all the things we shared—that was just an escape? It didn't mean anything?"

"I didn't say it didn't mean anything." His voice softens slightly. "But meaning something and being sustainable are different things. You have your life—your Honor Council position, your debate team, your college applications, your friends who fit perfectly into Thornbridge's social structure. And I have my life. Or I don't have my life. Either way, we exist in completely different worlds during the day."

"That doesn't mean we can't—"

"Can't what?" Evan interrupts. "Can't find a way to make this work? Can't figure out how to merge our two completely incompatible existences?" He shakes his head. "Harper, be honest. Can you actually see us together out there? In the dining hall, in class, at school events? Can you picture introducing me to your friends, telling your mother about me, publicly acknowledging the weird transfer kid everyone dismisses?"

I want to say yes. I want to insist that of course I can picture it, that I'm brave enough to choose him regardless of what anyone thinks.

But the image won't quite form. I see Madison's disapproval. Caroline's concern. Victoria's reconsideration. My mother's disappointment. The whispers in the hallway. The questions about my judgment.

My silence is answer enough.

"That's what I thought," Evan says quietly.

"It's not that I don't want to," I try to explain. "It's just—"

"Just risky. Just difficult. Just not worth the cost." He stands up, and I realize he's preparing to leave. "I get it, Harper. I do. You've worked too hard for too long to throw it all away for someone like me."

"Someone like you," I repeat, anger flashing through the hurt. "What does that even mean?"

"It means I'm the scholarship kid with the troubled past. The loner who doesn't fit in. The guy everyone avoids because it's easier to believe the worst than to actually get to know me." His voice is matter-of-fact, like he's stating obvious facts instead of revealing deep wounds. "You've been slumming it with the outcast, and eventually you were always going to realize it's not worth the cost."

"That's not fair."

"Maybe not. But it's true, isn't it?" He looks at me directly. "You've had four days to think about whether this is worth fighting for, and you stayed away. You made your choice."

"I came back," I protest.

"You came back after you'd convinced yourself you should. But your first instinct was to hide. To protect yourself. To choose the safe option." He shoulders his backpack. "There's nothing wrong with that, Harper. Self-preservation is human. But let's not pretend this is some great love story where we overcome all obstacles. We're two people who found temporary comfort in a secret space, and now that space is ending."

"You're giving up," I say, my voice rising. "You're deciding for both of us that this can't work."

"I'm being realistic about what we are," Evan counters. "You want me to believe you're willing to risk everything for me? Then prove it. Tell me you'll sit with me at lunch tomorrow. Tell me you'll acknowledge me in the hallway when Madison's watching. Tell me you'll introduce me to your mother as your boyfriend instead of hiding me like a shameful secret."

The challenge hangs in the air between us. I should accept it. Should promise that yes, I'll do all those things, I'll choose him publicly, I'll risk everything.

But the words stick in my throat.

"That's what I thought," Evan says again, softer this time. "You're not ready. Maybe you'll never be ready. And I can't keep waiting for you to decide I'm worth the risk."

"So you're just...ending this? Making the decision for both of us?"

"I'm saving you from having to choose," Evan says. "Because we both know what you'd choose. Your perfect future, your carefully maintained reputation, your mother's approval. And honestly? I don't blame you. Those things are important. They're real in a way this—" He gestures at the library. "—never was."

"How can you say this wasn't real?" My voice breaks slightly. "You know me better than anyone else at this school. The conversations we've had, the things we've shared—that was real, Evan. How can you dismiss all of it?"

"I'm not dismissing it." He looks away, and for the first time since I arrived, I see cracks in his careful composure. "But real and sustainable aren't the same thing. What we had here was beautiful and honest and more authentic than anything else in my life. But it existed in a bubble. And bubbles always pop."

He walks toward the window, and panic surges through me. "Evan, wait—"

He pauses but doesn't turn around. "What do you want me to say, Harper? That I'll keep meeting you here for the next eleven days until the renovation forces us to stop? That we'll pretend this has a future when we both know it doesn't? That I'll let myself fall even more for someone who's going to choose their perfect life over me the minute it matters?"

"I'm not—" But I stop because I don't know how to finish that sentence. I'm not going to choose my perfect life? But aren't I? Haven't I already, by staying away for four days? By not being able to picture a future where we're publicly together? By hesitating when he challenged me to prove I'm willing to risk everything?

"I care about you," Evan says, still facing away. "More than I've cared about anyone in a long time. Which is exactly why I need to end this before I get hurt worse than I already am."

"And what about hurting me?"

"You'll be fine." He finally looks back at me, and the resignation in his eyes is devastating. "You have your debate team and your Honor Council position and your perfectly planned future. You have friends and goals and a path forward. You don't need me."

"But I want—"

"Want isn't enough," Evan interrupts gently. "Not when what you want contradicts everything else in your life. Not when choosing me means destroying the future you've built. I won't be the reason you lose everything, Harper. Even if that means losing this."

He climbs through the window and disappears into the January night, leaving me alone in the library for the second time. But this time feels different. This time feels final.

I sit in my chair—in our spot that's maybe just my spot now—and try to process what just happened. Evan ended things. Decided we don't have a future. Chose to protect himself before I could hurt him.

And the worst part is, I can't even say he's wrong.

Because when he challenged me to prove I'd choose him publicly, I couldn't do it. When he asked me to picture us together in daylight, I couldn't quite see it. When he accused me of slumming it with the outcast before returning to my real life, some part of me recognized the truth in his words.

I've been treating this like a secret rebellion. A temporary escape from the pressure of perfection. Something that exists apart from my real life instead of being integrated into it.

And now it's over. Not because of the renovation or Caroline's ultimatum or any external force. But because Evan looked at what we had and decided it wasn't worth the inevitable heartbreak. Decided I wasn't brave enough to choose him when it mattered.

Decided he was right not to trust me.

I sit alone in the emergency lighting and wonder if he's right about everything. If this was never real. If we were just two lonely people finding temporary comfort in a space that was always going to disappear.

If I'm really as hollow and performative as I've been feeling.

The library doesn't feel like a sanctuary anymore. It feels like a tomb. A memorial to something that might have been beautiful but was never meant to last.

Eleven days left until the renovation. But what's the point of coming back if Evan won't be here?

What's the point of anything if I'm too afraid to fight for what I actually want?

I leave through the window, and for the first time since finding this place, I don't feel peace. I just feel empty.

Like Evan took something essential with him when he left.

Like maybe it was never real after all.
