# Chapter 14: The Weight of Pretending

The Honor Council meeting on Wednesday is about a junior who was caught using their phone during a test.

It's a straightforward case. The student admits it, says they were checking the time but understands how it looked. We discuss whether phone possession during exams should result in automatic academic dishonesty charges or if intent matters.

I argue that intent matters but that the school's phone policy exists precisely to avoid these ambiguities. That students know the rules. That following them isn't optional just because you think you have a good reason.

The words come out smooth and confident, perfected through weeks of practice at being Harper Mills, principled Honor Council representative.

But inside, I'm screaming at my own hypocrisy.

Because every word I'm saying about rules and accountability and how "good intentions don't excuse violations" applies directly to me. I'm sitting in this meeting discussing how to discipline someone for breaking a minor school rule while I systematically violate curfew, trespass in a locked building, and lie to everyone in my life about where I spend my nights.

If intent doesn't matter for phone possession during a test, how can it possibly matter for what I'm doing?

We vote to give the student a warning and require them to retake the test under supervision. Lenient, considering. The student looks relieved.

I feel sick.

After the meeting, Madison catches up with me.

"Hey, study group for the calculus exam tomorrow night at midnight in the common room. You in?"

My mind goes blank. Tomorrow night. Midnight. Exactly when I'm supposed to be in the library with Evan.

"I... I can't," I say.

Madison's eyebrows raise. "You can't? This exam is twenty percent of our grade."

"I have to finish my college essay. It's due Friday and I'm behind."

"So finish it earlier. The study group isn't until midnight."

"I work better late. I need those quiet hours."

Madison looks at me strangely. "You could write during the day and study with us at night. It's just a few hours."

"I really can't. I'm sorry."

"Harper, is something going on? You've been acting really weird lately."

"I'm fine. Just stressed about college applications."

"We're all stressed about college applications. But you're..." Madison pauses, choosing words carefully. "You're different. Distracted. You space out in conversations. You're making excuses not to do things you'd normally do. If something's wrong, you can tell me."

The concern in her voice is genuine. Madison is my friend—or at least, she's supposed to be. We've been in the pre-law track together since freshman year. She's competitive and sometimes too focused on achievement, but she cares.

And I'm lying directly to her face.

"Nothing's wrong," I insist. "I promise. I'm just trying to manage everything and it's a lot."

Madison doesn't look convinced, but she lets it drop. "Okay. Well, if you change your mind about the study group, we'll be there."

"Thanks."

She walks away, and I stand in the empty hallway feeling like the worst person alive.

Because Madison is right. I am acting weird. I am different. And it's not just stress—it's the exhausting effort of maintaining two completely separate lives and pretending they're compatible.

That afternoon, I go to the library to work on the college essay I supposedly need to finish. I stare at the prompt:

*Describe a challenge you've overcome and what it taught you about yourself.*

I could write about insomnia. But I can't write about the library without admitting to breaking curfew.

I could write about the pressure of expectations. But being honest would mean admitting I'm not sure I want the future everyone expects.

I could write about authenticity in an environment that rewards performance. But that would require acknowledging that most of my life is performance.

Every possible truth would expose something I'm not supposed to admit.

So I write a generic essay about debate team. Technically true but bloodless. Nothing in it reflects who I actually am.

Another performance. Another layer of pretending.

By 11 PM, I'm so exhausted from pretending that I can barely think straight. Caroline asks if I'm okay, and I snap at her that I'm fine, then immediately feel guilty.

When midnight finally comes and I slip out to the library, it feels less like escape and more like switching costumes. Trading one performance for another, even though this one is supposed to be real.

Evan is already there when I arrive, which is unusual. He's at our usual spot, but he's not reading. Just sitting, staring out the window at nothing.

"Hey," I say.

"Hey."

I drop into my chair, suddenly too tired to maintain any kind of façade.

"I'm a terrible person," I announce.

Evan looks over, surprised. "What?"

"I'm a terrible person. A fraud. A hypocrite. Pick your term."

"What happened?"

And suddenly I'm telling him everything. About the Honor Council meeting where I argued about rules and accountability while violating multiple rules myself. About Madison inviting me to the study group and me making elaborate excuses because I can't admit where I actually spend my nights. About lying to everyone constantly while sitting in judgment of other people's rule-breaking.

"I give speeches about integrity," I say bitterly. "About how Thornbridge's standards matter and we're all accountable. And then I climb through a window every night and pretend I'm not doing anything wrong because I have good reasons. Because I need this. But the junior with the phone probably needed to check the time, and I still voted to discipline them."

Evan listens without interrupting. When I finally run out of words, he's quiet for a moment.

Then he asks: "Which version of you is the pretend one?"

"What?"

"The girl who sits on Honor Council and gives speeches about integrity during the day, or the girl who breaks curfew and climbs through windows at night. Which one is performing? Which one is real?"

I open my mouth to answer and realize I don't know.

"Maybe..." I start, then stop. "Maybe they're both real? Or both pretend? I don't know anymore."

"Or maybe," Evan suggests gently, "you're both. And you're still figuring out who you actually are underneath all the expectations and rules and performances."

"That's terrifying."

"Yeah. It is."

"How do you do it?" I ask. "How do you know which version of yourself is real?"

Evan considers this. "I don't know if I do. During the day, I'm the isolated kid who doesn't talk to anyone because it's safer than risking connection. At night, I'm... this. Someone who talks and laughs and trusts, at least a little bit. Are either of those the real me? Or am I somewhere in between?"

"Do you think we can ever be fully ourselves?" I ask. "Or are we always going to be performing some version based on context?"

"I think maybe being fully yourself means integrating the different versions instead of keeping them separate," Evan says. "Bringing the nighttime honesty into the daytime world. Letting people see you—really see you—even when it's scary."

"That sounds impossible."

"Maybe. But living like this isn't sustainable either."

He's right. I can feel it in my bones. The exhaustion of maintaining two lives. The guilt of lying to Madison and Caroline and my mother. The hypocrisy of sitting on Honor Council. The constant fear of being discovered.

Something has to give.

"What would you do?" I ask Evan. "If you were me?"

"I don't know. But I do know you can't keep beating yourself up for being human. For needing things that don't fit neatly into the boxes other people made for you."

"Even if those needs require breaking rules?"

"Especially then. Because sometimes the rules don't account for real human complexity. Sometimes surviving means bending them."

"Is that what we're doing? Surviving?"

"Isn't it?"

I think about that. About the person I was before I found the library—anxious, sleepless, slowly breaking under pressure. About who I'm becoming now—still anxious, still breaking rules, but also more honest about my doubts and fears.

"I don't know if I'm surviving or just running away," I admit.

"Maybe there's not as much difference as you think."

We sit in silence, the weight of everything unsaid pressing down. After a while, Evan speaks again.

"For what it's worth, I don't think you're a terrible person. I think you're someone trying to navigate an impossible situation. And that's... that's human."

"Even though I'm a hypocrite?"

"Everyone's a hypocrite about something. At least you're aware of it."

"That doesn't make it better."

"No. But it means you're honest with yourself, even if you can't be honest with everyone else. That counts for something."

I want to believe him. Want to think that my awareness of my own hypocrisy somehow mitigates it. But I'm not sure it does.

"Evan, do you ever feel like we're just hiding from our real lives? Like this—" I gesture at the library around us "—is avoidance instead of solution?"

"Every day," he admits. "But I also think this is the only place where I'm not hiding. Where I can actually be honest about what I'm feeling and thinking and struggling with."

"So which is it? Hiding or honesty?"

"Both. Paradoxically, we're hiding from our daytime lives in the only place where we can be honest."

The contradiction sits between us, unresolved and unresolvable.

I'm exhausted. Not just from lack of sleep, but from the constant effort of being two different people. Of maintaining performances in both worlds. Of trying to figure out which version is real when maybe neither version is complete.

"I don't know how much longer I can do this," I confess quietly.

"Then maybe you don't," Evan says.

"Don't what?"

"Keep doing it. Maybe you choose. One life or the other. Daytime or nighttime. Performance or authenticity."

The suggestion should feel freeing. Instead, it feels like being asked to cut off part of myself.

Because I can't imagine giving up the library, giving up Evan, giving up these hours of honesty.

But I also can't imagine walking away from Honor Council and my pre-law track and the future I've been working toward since middle school.

I'm trapped between two versions of myself, and I don't know how to reconcile them.

"What if I choose wrong?" I whisper.

"What if there's no wrong choice?" Evan counters. "What if there's just... what you actually want versus what you think you're supposed to want?"

The question echoes in the silence.

What do I actually want?

I don't know.

And that might be the most terrifying thing of all.
