# Chapter 18: Discovery

The door to my dorm room creaks as I push it open at three-thirty in the morning. I've learned to navigate the hallway in complete darkness, to avoid the spots where the floor groans, to ease the door closed with barely a whisper. I'm careful. I'm always careful.

But when I step inside, the lamp on Caroline's desk clicks on, and she's sitting on her bed fully dressed, arms crossed, waiting.

My heart stops.

"Where have you been?" Caroline's voice is low but tight with something that sounds like anger mixed with concern.

For a frozen second, I can't move, can't think. I'm still standing in the doorway in my jeans and sweater, my backpack slung over one shoulder, caught as completely as if security had found me in the library.

"I—" My mind scrambles for an excuse. Couldn't sleep, went for a walk. Studying in the common room. Someone was sick and needed help. But the words won't come because Caroline's expression makes it clear she's not going to accept easy lies.

"Sit down," she says, and it's not quite a command but not quite a request either.

I close the door behind me, my hands shaking slightly, and perch on the edge of my own bed. Caroline is still in her field hockey sweatpants and a Thornbridge t-shirt, her dark hair pulled back in a ponytail. She looks like she's been awake for a while.

"How long have you known?" I ask quietly.

"That you've been sneaking out?" Caroline's eyebrows rise. "I've noticed you leaving at least four times in the past two weeks. Maybe more that I slept through. I kept hoping you'd tell me on your own, but you didn't. So I stayed up tonight."

Four times. She's been tracking me for two weeks and I had no idea. I thought I'd been so careful—waiting until her breathing evened out, moving silently, varying my timing. But apparently not careful enough.

"Where have you been going, Harper?" She leans forward slightly. "And don't lie to me. I care about you too much to let you put yourself in danger."

I consider my options. I could make up something plausible—meeting a boy from town, going to someone else's dorm, having panic attacks that require walks. But Caroline's expression tells me she's done with half-truths. And maybe I'm tired of lying to the person I share a room with, the person who's been nothing but decent to me since freshman year.

"The library," I finally admit.

Caroline blinks. Whatever she was expecting, it clearly wasn't that. "The library? At three in the morning?"

"I can't sleep." The words come out more desperately than I intended. "I haven't been sleeping properly since the semester started. Maybe longer. My mind just—it won't shut off. All the pressure, the expectations, the constant anxiety about grades and college and being good enough. I lie awake for hours every night, and I can't take it anymore."

Caroline's expression softens slightly, but she doesn't uncross her arms. "So you started breaking into the school library?"

"There's a window latch that's broken," I say. "On the first floor, in the periodicals section. I found it one night when I was walking around campus, desperate for somewhere quiet. The library is—it's peaceful at night. No one there, no expectations. Just me and the books and the silence. It's the only place where my brain actually calms down."

"Harper." Caroline says my name like she's talking to a child who's done something dangerous without understanding the risk. "You're on Honor Council. You're supposed to be upholding school rules, not breaking them. Do you understand how this looks?"

"I know." Shame burns hot in my chest. "I know it's hypocritical. But I didn't know what else to do. I can't keep lying awake every night. I can't keep functioning on three hours of sleep. The library has been the only thing that helps."

Caroline stands up and starts pacing the small space between our beds. "This is insane. If you get caught, you'll be suspended. Maybe expelled. Your college applications—Harper, you could lose everything."

"I know," I repeat, quieter this time.

"Then why do you keep doing it?" She stops pacing and looks at me directly. "If you can't sleep, go to the school counselor. Talk to the health center about insomnia. There are legitimate ways to get help that don't involve trespassing and breaking curfew."

She's right. Of course she's right. The smart thing, the proper thing, would have been to seek official help. But the thought of sitting in some counselor's office explaining that I can't handle the pressure everyone else seems to manage fine, admitting weakness, having it noted in my file—that felt impossible.

"I was ashamed," I admit. "Everyone here seems to have their life together. You're managing field hockey and a full course load. Madison is taking five APs and running the debate team. Victoria is Honor Council president and still finds time for orchestra. Everyone's succeeding, and I can't even sleep. It felt like failure."

Caroline sits back down on her bed, some of the anger draining from her expression. "Harper, half the girls in this dorm are on anxiety medication. Three people on my field hockey team see therapists. You think everyone has it together, but we're all struggling. You're not weak for needing help—you're human."

The kindness in her voice makes my throat tight. I've been so focused on maintaining my perfect image that I didn't notice the cracks in everyone else's facades.

"How did you even think this was a good idea?" Caroline asks, her voice gentler now. "Breaking into the library alone in the middle of the night?"

"I wasn't—" I stop myself just in time. I almost said "I wasn't alone." But I can't tell Caroline about Evan. That would make everything infinitely more complicated. Bad enough that I'm sneaking out to trespass—adding a secret relationship with the boy everyone thinks is trouble would sound even worse.

"You weren't what?" Caroline prompts.

"I wasn't thinking clearly," I finish. "I was desperate for sleep, for peace, and I found it in the library. It became a habit."

Caroline watches me carefully, and I have the uncomfortable feeling she knows I'm not telling her everything. But she doesn't push.

"This has to stop," she says firmly. "I know you're struggling with insomnia, and I'm sorry you felt like you couldn't ask for help. But sneaking out every night is not the answer. You're risking your entire future for a few hours of quiet."

I nod, not trusting my voice.

"I want you to promise me you'll stop," Caroline continues. "And I want you to promise you'll actually get help—real help. Talk to the counselor, or your advisor, or even just your mom. But you can't keep doing this."

The request hangs in the air between us. Promise to stop. Promise to give up the library, the only place where I can breathe. Promise to give up seeing Evan, the only person who understands what it's like to feel like you don't fit.

I should tell her I can't promise that. I should be honest and say that the library has become essential, that I'm not ready to give it up.

But Caroline is looking at me with genuine concern, with friendship, and she's offering me a way out. She's not reporting me to Dr. Whitmore or Victoria. She's giving me a chance to fix this quietly, to avoid the consequences that would destroy my carefully constructed future.

"Okay," I hear myself say. "I promise."

The lie tastes bitter on my tongue.

Caroline's shoulders relax visibly. "Thank you. I know this is hard, but it's for the best. You'll see—once you get proper help for the insomnia, you won't need to sneak around anymore."

I nod again, letting her believe it.

"I won't report you," Caroline says. "What you did was wrong, but I understand why you did it. Just—please don't make me regret trusting you. If I find out you're still sneaking out—"

"You won't," I say quickly. Another lie piled on top of the first.

"Good." Caroline stands and switches off her desk lamp. The room plunges into darkness except for the faint moonlight through our window. "We should try to get some sleep. It's almost four."

I change into my pajamas in the dark, my movements automatic. Caroline's breathing evens out quickly—she's probably exhausted from staying up to catch me. But I lie awake, staring at the ceiling, my mind racing.

I just lied to my roommate. Looked her in the eye and promised something I have no intention of keeping.

The old Harper—the one who followed rules and maintained perfect integrity—would be horrified. But that Harper has been shifting, changing into someone I don't fully recognize. Someone who sneaks out at night and lies to friends and chooses moments with Evan over the safety of her carefully planned future.

I should feel guilty. And I do—there's a heavy weight of shame sitting on my chest. But underneath the guilt is something else: resolve.

I'm not going to stop going to the library. I'm not ready to give up the only place where I feel like myself. I'm not ready to give up Evan.

Which means I need to be even more careful. I need to make sure Caroline doesn't catch me again. I need to be smarter about the timing, quieter about my exits, more aware of when she's awake.

I need to get better at lying.

The thought should disturb me more than it does. But as I finally drift off sometime after five in the morning, what I'm thinking about isn't the lies I'm telling. It's how I'm going to explain Caroline's ultimatum to Evan tomorrow night.

Because I will see him tomorrow night. And the night after that. No matter what I promised Caroline.

Some things, I'm beginning to realize, are worth the risk of everything.
