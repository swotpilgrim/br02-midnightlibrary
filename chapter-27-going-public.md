# Chapter 27: Going Public

The library renovation celebration is held on a Friday afternoon, the last day before winter break. The event showcases the newly secured, beautifully preserved historic library.

I'm standing with Madison and Caroline in the main reading room. The new LED lighting brightens every corner. The windows have new latches gleaming. Security panels are barely visible. Everything is perfect, properly locked, completely secure.

Nothing like the sanctuary it used to be.

"This is actually really nice," Caroline says, examining one of the restored reading chairs. "I might actually study here now."

Madison nods, already calculating. "The new private study rooms will be perfect for mock trials. Did you see the sign-up sheet?"

I scan the crowd. Faculty members cluster near the rare books display. Underclassmen drift toward the refreshment table. Victoria Ashford speaks with Dr. Whitmore near the circulation desk.

And standing alone near the periodicals section—our section—is Evan.

He's wearing his usual combination of jeans and a sweater, looking uncomfortable in the crowd. His hands are shoved in his pockets, and he's not quite making eye contact with anyone. A few students have glanced his direction, but no one approaches. He's as isolated here as he is everywhere else on campus.

During the day, anyway.

My pulse quickens. This is it. The moment where I stop living two separate lives. Where I choose authenticity over approval.

I'm terrified.

"Harper? You listening?" Madison's voice breaks through my thoughts.

"Sorry, what?"

"I asked if you wanted to check out the new archives room." Madison's eyes narrow slightly. "You've been distracted all week. What's going on?"

Caroline exchanges a look with Madison. They've both noticed my strange mood—the exhaustion from not sleeping, the distance from their conversations, the way I've been going through the motions without really being present. They think it's stress. Exam pressure. College application anxiety.

They have no idea that I've been spending my nights remaking my entire understanding of who I am.

"I'm fine," I say automatically. Then I stop. Because that's exactly the kind of performative response I'm supposed to be done with. "Actually, I'll catch up with you guys in a minute. There's something I need to do."

"What thing?" Madison asks.

But I'm already walking away, my heart hammering so loud I'm sure the entire library can hear it.

I cross the reading room toward Evan. Every step feels weighted. People are starting to notice—I can feel their eyes tracking my movement. Harper Mills walking toward the weird loner. That's unusual enough to draw attention.

Evan sees me approaching. Surprise flickers across his face, then hope mixed with apprehension. We had our moment this morning in the quad. But that was six AM. This is different. This is in front of the entire school at an official event.

This is choosing him publicly, irreversibly.

I reach him and stop, acutely aware that at least fifty people can see us. Can see Harper Mills, Honor Council representative, perfect student, standing close to Evan Cross, the guy everyone dismisses.

"Hi," I say, my voice steadier than I feel.

"Hi." Evan's eyes search mine. "You okay?"

"Terrified," I admit quietly. Then, louder: "Want to show me around the renovated library?"

Evan blinks. "Are you sure?"

Instead of answering with words, I reach out and take his hand.

The gesture is small. Simple. Just my fingers intertwining with his in the middle of the library reading room during the renovation celebration while half the school watches.

But it feels enormous.

I can practically hear the whispers starting. See the double-takes out of the corner of my eye. Feel the weight of people's attention shifting to us.

"I'm completely sure," I say, loud enough that anyone nearby can hear.

Evan's hand tightens around mine. "Then yeah. I'd love to show you around."

We start walking through the library together. Hand in hand. Not hiding.

The new study rooms are impressive. The archives are state-of-the-art. The reading nooks are beautiful. But I'm barely processing any of it because I'm hyper-aware of the stares, the whispers, the shocked expressions following us.

There's Thomas Brennan, the library assistant, who gives us a knowing smile and a small nod—he figured out our secret weeks ago.

There's a cluster of sophomore girls who stop talking entirely when we walk past, openly staring.

There's Mr. Harrison, my debate coach, looking confused.

And there, near the circulation desk, is Madison.

She's standing with her arms crossed, watching us with an expression I can't quite read. Caroline is next to her, equally puzzled. As Evan and I approach, Madison steps forward.

"Harper. Can I talk to you?" Her voice is tight.

"Of course." I don't let go of Evan's hand. "But Evan should stay. Madison Riley, this is Evan Cross. Evan, this is my friend Madison."

The word "friend" is deliberately chosen. Not "my debate partner" or "someone from pre-law track." Friend. Because Madison is my friend, even if she doesn't know me as well as she thinks she does.

"We've seen each other around," Madison says coolly, barely glancing at Evan. Then to me: "What's going on? Since when are you..." She gestures at our joined hands.

"Since we've been getting to know each other," I say carefully. "And I like him. A lot."

Madison's eyes widen. "You like him. Evan Cross."

"Yes."

"The guy who sits alone and doesn't talk to anyone and has been here for months without making a single friend?"

I feel Evan tense beside me, but I squeeze his hand. "The guy who's brilliant and thoughtful and sees people for who they actually are instead of who they're performing to be. Yes, that guy."

Madison looks at me like I've grown a second head. "Harper, do you hear yourself? This doesn't make any sense. You're on Honor Council. You're applying to Ivy League schools. You're—"

"I'm someone who got to know Evan and discovered he's worth knowing," I interrupt. "And maybe if more people gave him a chance instead of dismissing him, they'd realize that too."

The words hang in the air. Madison's mouth opens and closes. Caroline has joined us now, looking between me and Evan with open confusion.

"Is this why you've been so weird lately?" Caroline asks. "Because you're dating him?"

"I've been weird because I've been trying to figure out who I actually am versus who everyone expects me to be," I say. "And yes, Evan is part of that. He helped me see that I've been performing instead of living."

"Performing," Madison repeats flatly. "Harper, we're your friends. When have you ever had to perform for us?"

The question is so genuinely confused that I almost feel bad. But I also recognize that Madison truly doesn't see it—doesn't understand that our entire friendship has been built on achievement and shared goals rather than genuine knowing.

"I appreciate you, Madison. Both of you," I say, including Caroline. "But you've been friends with perfect Harper Mills—the girl with the 4.0 GPA who never steps out of line. You don't really know the messy, uncertain version. The one who's not sure about pre-law and questions whether she wants the life everyone's planned for her."

"So you're having some kind of crisis and decided to date the weird kid?" Madison's tone has shifted to something harder.

"He's not weird," I say firmly. "He's just careful about who he trusts. And he's not 'some kind of crisis.' He's someone who sees me—actually sees me—and likes what he sees even when it's imperfect."

Evan finally speaks. "I should probably let you talk to your friends—"

"No." I hold his hand tighter. "I'm done having separate conversations. Done hiding parts of my life from other parts. Madison, Caroline—Evan matters to me. And if you're my friends, you'll at least try to understand that."

Madison's expression is unreadable. Caroline looks genuinely worried. And I realize this might be the moment I lose them. That choosing authenticity might mean losing friendships built on shared performance.

It hurts. But it doesn't change my mind.

Before either can respond, Victoria Ashford approaches our group. She's wearing her Honor Council pin and her diplomatic expression.

"Harper. Evan." She nods to each of us. "Interesting choice of company for today's event."

Here it comes. The judgment. The reconsideration of my Honor Council position. The consequences I knew were coming.

But Victoria surprises me. "I wanted to say that reputation becoming complicated isn't always a bad thing. Sometimes it means you're becoming authentic instead of just maintaining an image."

She looks directly at me. "Integrity matters more than image, Harper. You've shown courage today. That counts for something."

Then she walks away, leaving Madison looking even more confused and me feeling like maybe the world isn't ending.

"I don't understand what's happening," Madison finally says.

"I'm being honest," I tell her. "Maybe for the first time since we've known each other. And I know it's confusing because it doesn't fit the version of me you expected. But this is who I actually am."

Madison is quiet for a long moment. Then: "I don't get it. But I also... don't not want to try? Maybe? This is really weird, Harper."

It's not acceptance. But it's not total rejection either. It's a maybe. A possibility that our friendship can evolve into something more real.

"That's fair," I say.

Caroline steps forward. "For what it's worth, I think you've seemed happier this week. Even though you're also clearly terrified. So if he—" She nods at Evan. "—makes you happy, then okay."

It's the most reluctant endorsement possible. But it's something.

"Thank you," I tell them both.

The conversation is interrupted by Dr. Whitmore calling everyone's attention for closing remarks. The crowd shifts, people moving toward the front of the library. Madison and Caroline drift with the flow, still looking uncertain but at least not actively hostile.

Evan and I stay near the periodicals section. Our section. Which looks nothing like it did at midnight, but holds all the same memories.

"That was brave," Evan says quietly.

"That was terrifying," I correct. "But necessary."

"You don't have to do this for me—"

"I'm not doing it for you." I turn to face him fully. "I'm doing it for me. Because living two separate lives was killing me, and I'd rather face judgment for being real than praise for being fake."

Evan cups my face gently. "You're extraordinary."

"I'm scared."

"You can be both."

As Dr. Whitmore begins her speech about tradition and progress, about honoring the past while securing the future, I stand next to Evan in full view of everyone. Our hands are still joined. People are still staring. The whispers haven't stopped.

But I'm not hiding anymore.

And for the first time in years, I can breathe.
